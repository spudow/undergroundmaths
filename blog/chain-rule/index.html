Percolation, patience and the chain rule

Julian Gilbey, Underground Mathematics
Associated resource
Chain mapping
Chain Rule & Integration by Substitution
Students are led to an understanding of what the chain rule means and why it works, using mapping diagrams. It builds on two earlier mapping diagrams resources.

There is an idea floating around that it is important to see demonstrable progress in each lesson. But sometimes things take far longer to reach fruition. In this post, I will explore my growing understanding of the chain rule, which took me somewhat longer than one lesson! The mathematics itself is just my vehicle for reflecting on patience and what it might mean to really understand a mathematical idea.

I first learnt the chain rule as an A-level student from Backhouse and Holdworth’s A-level textbook (as revised by Horril). The authors introduced the chain rule by asking students to find the derivatives of expressions like (2x+3)2(2x+3)2 by expanding, and to look for a possible general rule. They then wrote the following explanation:

Suppose yy is a function of tt, and tt is itself a function of xx. If δyδy, δtδt, and δxδx are corresponding small increments in the variables yy, tt, and xx, then
δyδx=δyδt×δtδx(1)(1)δyδx=δyδt×δtδx

When δyδy, δtδt, and δxδx tend to zero,

δyδx→dydx,δyδt→dydt,δtδx→dtdxδyδx→dydx,δyδt→dydt,δtδx→dtdx
and equation (1)(1) becomes
dydx=dydt×dtdxdydx=dydt×dtdx

This important result is known as the chain rule. …

This was followed by examples of its use, such as y=(3x+2)4y=(3x+2)4 being rewritten as y=t4y=t4 where t=3x+2t=3x+2, and then an exercise to practise the technique. I’m sure this clear explanation made sense to me at the time, and I carried it with me for years afterwards: that constituted my understanding of the chain rule.

When I studied mathematical analysis a few years later, I learnt about the epsilon-delta approach to calculus, and was able to then “formally” prove the chain rule using analytical arguments, which gave more insight into what is actually happening. One basically thinks of the chain rule as trying to differentiate a composition of functions such as g(f(x))g(f(x)). This is already a somewhat different perspective than the substitution approach introduced in the A-level textbook; I have no idea whether I realised at the time that they were talking about a composition of functions.

Working with the composite function g(f(x))g(f(x)), the “calculus from first principles” approach to calculating the derivative requires one to find the limit of g(f(x+h))−g(f(x))hg(f(x+h))−g(f(x))h as h→0h→0. This in turn leads one to think of the approximation f(x+h)≈f(x)+f′(x)hf(x+h)≈f(x)+f′(x)h. When this is substituted into g(f(x+h))g(f(x+h)), one can then calculate
g(f(x+h))−g(f(x))h≈g(f(x)+f′(x)h)−g(f(x))h≈g(f(x))+g′(f(x))(f′(x)h)−g(f(x))h=g′(f(x))f′(x)hh=g′(f(x))f′(x)g(f(x+h))−g(f(x))h≈g(f(x)+f′(x)h)−g(f(x))h≈g(f(x))+g′(f(x))(f′(x)h)−g(f(x))h=g′(f(x))f′(x)hh=g′(f(x))f′(x)

The second line here takes some thinking about, and there is a lot of algebra floating around. To show that this really works, we learnt to translate this sequence of approximations into a formal epsilon-delta argument and, lo and behold, everything came out nicely. But the formal analysis approach, while showing that the approximate argument does indeed work, gave me no further insight into this particular rule, and I continued to use the chain rule in a practical sense by using the method I had learnt from my A-level days. (This is not to knock analysis itself: it is a beautiful subject, and is a vital tool for modern mathematics, giving deep insight and powerful techniques for many theoretical and practical problems.)

At some point later, I learnt about the extension of the chain rule to multivariable calculus, which involved thinking about derivatives as linear transformations approximating the function of interest, and I’m sure I understood it at the time, at least at the level of being able to manipulate and use the equations. But on reflection now, I really don’t think that I actually attached that much meaning to the symbols, and I certainly didn’t have a relational understanding (to use Skemp’s terminology). I did think of a derivative as telling us about the tangent plane to a surface in two dimensions, just as it tells us about the tangent line to a curve in one dimension, but I did not connect that to the chain rule.

And that is the way that the chain rule stayed in my mind for many years. During that time, I taught it at school, and taught the multivariable extension to undergraduates.

About a year ago, I was at the ATM Easter Conference, where I attended a double session with Martin Flashman, of Humboldt State University. He had been developing an approach to teaching algebra and calculus using mapping diagrams, such as this for representing f(x)=3xf(x)=3x:

I had been brought up with two parallel concepts of differentiation. The first was the traditional gradients-of-secants approach, which appears in most textbooks, and is also used in our resource Binomials are the answer!. The second was the “zooming in” approach, which was introduced to me by my teacher (and quite possibly derives from David Tall’s work); this is exemplified by Zooming in and A tangent is…. But I had never contemplated the idea of using mapping diagrams for thinking about calculus. In Martin Flashman’s session, we looked at a variety of mathematical ideas that one could develop using these diagrams, ranging from solving linear equations to thinking about calculus, and it both challenged and excited me to see things differently.

Was I a good student? Did I demonstrate good progress in the “lesson”? Well, I could certainly answer questions at the end of the session that I couldn’t at the beginning, but did I understand more? I had learnt a new technique, but I hadn’t yet begun to internalise it.

Over the next few months, as we were working on the calculus stations, I kept returning to the mapping diagrams. At some point, and I don’t know quite how, something clicked for me and I had a moment of clarity. I finally understood differentiation! There was nothing quite tangible to this experience, and I could point to all of those earlier times in my life when I had read and written formulae which said exactly the same thing, but I think it was the first time that I actually truly internalised what differentiation is. And with that, the chain rule became blindingly obvious to me: no longer is it just a rule for me, but something which is patently true.

I could try to explain my understanding, but I struggle to do so in a way which will make sense to someone else who has not been through such a learning experience. There is a sense in which you, my gentle reader, might look at my explanation and say “but that’s obvious”, and in many ways it is, though it might be painful for me to hear that. Indeed, it had been staring me in the face for almost all of this time. Or more likely, you might look at it, and say “yes, I get that”, yet not really do so in the way that I finally have. Though as it took me several decades to reach this level of clarity of understanding, that would not surprise me! But if I can help others get there more quickly, I’d be happy.

And what of our students, those who sit in our classrooms and struggle over a piece of mathematics, perhaps successfully answering our questions or working through their textbook exercises? Do they really understand the mathematics they are currently learning? And would it be reasonable of us to expect them to do so the first time around, or even the fifth time? Do we need to give them time to play with ideas, to see them again with fresh eyes, to let them percolate, and to offer a variety of experiences which might help them to build an understanding, one which is deep and robust? Such understanding is unlikely to develop in a lesson or a short sequence of lessons, yet over time, we can work towards it. I know no way of teaching understanding, only of teaching knowledge, though using rich teaching materials such as ours can certainly help students to develop their own understanding. And with deep understanding comes a level of confidence in the mathematics that would serve our students so well.

We have developed a sequence of resources based on these ideas, called Mapping a function, Mapping a derivative and Chain mapping. These will hopefully lead students to this new understanding of what a derivative “really” is and why the chain rule is “obvious”; of these, the first is the most substantial, as it introduces a new perspective on linear functions (those of the form f(x)=ax+bf(x)=ax+b). And though students may only obtain a limited understanding the first time visiting these ideas, they may well sow the seeds for a future, richer understanding of differentiation.

For those of you who would like a potted summary, I can present the idea algebraically, though it will be more meaningful by seeing the visual presentation in these resources. If we write f(x+h)≈f(x)+f′(x)hf(x+h)≈f(x)+f′(x)h for small hh, then we can rearrange this as f(x+h)−f(x)≈f′(x)hf(x+h)−f(x)≈f′(x)h. What does this say? One way to describe it is this: if we are near xx on our input, then a displacement of hh away (that is the change from xx to x+hx+h) is scaled to a displacement of f′(x)hf′(x)h in the output (that is the change from f(x)f(x) to f(x+h)f(x+h)), so f′(x)f′(x) is the scale factor from input to output. So the derivative is just a scale factor describing how the function behaves near xx. This perspective is very opaque in the normal illustration of a function using an xx-yy graph, but becomes completely transparent when using mapping diagrams. Now what would happen if we had a composition of functions? I will leave you to ponder on that one, or to visit the Chain mapping resource!
