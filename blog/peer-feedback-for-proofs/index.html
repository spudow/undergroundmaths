Peer feedback for proofs

Daniel Reinholz, San Diego State University

This post builds an idea from a previous post, called Peer-Assisted Reflection (PAR). PAR is a structured routine for helping students provide feedback to their peers. You may wish to read the previous post first before you continue.

In this modified PAR activity, students use PAR as a way to support their mathematical arguments. Students generate their proofs in a two-column format. Here, the left column is for formal mathematics and the right column is used for “annotations.” Annotations can serve a variety of purposes. From the author’s perspective, students may annotate their own work to include definitions, diagrams, their thought processes, areas of uncertainty, or anything else that they think a reader should know. From the reader’s perspective, the annotations column allows for students to provide peer feedback. This helps students have personal ownership over proof, rather than feeling disconnected from the overly formal mathematics.

Authors annotate their own work in pencil, and readers provide feedback in pen. After receiving feedback, students can revise their work before submitting it to be graded. In the final submission, students once again annotate their work, providing a “response to reviewer feedback.” They do not need to agree with all of the feedback received, but if students choose not to use feedback, they need to describe why, just as in a real academic review process.

Again, PAR has a similar cycle to when used for problem solving. In PAR, students:

  1. complete a draft solution to a homework problem,
  2. reflect on their work,
  3. exchange peer feedback, and
  4. revise before submission.

When used for proofs, the peer feedback exchange takes more time than in problem solving. You may want to allot close to 20 minutes for this activity. Other than the feedback exchange, the rest of these activities occur at home.

This technique can be used in any course that focuses on mathematical argumentation. In my work with college students, many of them have described how annotations helped make proof feel personal to them. Because the formalism of mathematics can turn away many students, having a free-flowing annotations column that allows for any type of writing can be liberating. Play around with these ideas in your classrooms, and if you do, I’d love to hear how it goes and learn from you.

 

About the author, Daniel Reinholz

I am an assistant professor in the department of mathematics and statistics at San Diego State University. I specialize in STEM education transformation with the aim of increasing equity and diversity in STEM fields. My work is grounded in a holistic design perspective, which draws on research in disciplinary learning, equity, and organizational change. At the classroom level, I focus on how reflection and peer feedback can deepen disciplinary learning. Beyond the classroom, I study how cultural and structural features of higher education can support and inhibit meaningful transformation. Through my leadership in the Access Network, I work to improve access to meaningful STEM learning across the US.
