We need to talk about symmetry

Luciano Rila, University College London

According to BuzzFeed, no human has a perfectly symmetrical face. If you ever wondered what celebrities would look like if their faces were symmetrical, Figure 1 shows the transformation for Harry Styles.

Figure 1: Imposing symmetry to Harry Styles’ face.

I asked Google why we are so obsessed with symmetry and what struck me the most was that the understanding of symmetry seems to be generally reduced to mirror-image symmetry, i.e. symmetry with respect to reflection. In mathematics, symmetry has a much broader definition. One of my favourites was offered by physicist Richard Feynman: ‘a thing is symmetric if there is something we can do to it so that after we have done it, it looks the same as it did before.’

Graphs of functions can exhibit symmetry. Even functions such as y=x2y=x2 have the reflection symmetry in the yy-axis much desired by celebrities. In fact, any polynomial consisting of even powers of xx is an even function. Odd functions such as y=x3y=x3 have rotational symmetry around the origin. Similarly any polynomial consisting odd powers of xx is an odd function.

Translating a graph does not change the shape of the graph so any symmetry is preserved after translation. Let’s consider two quadratics, namely y=(x−1)(x−3)y=(x−1)(x−3) and y=x(x−4)y=x(x−4), shown in Figure 2, both translations of y=x2y=x2. They both have x=2x=2 as their line of symmetry.

If we now combine both quadratics to form a rational function, the symmetry is preserved. Figure 3 shows the graph of y=(x−1)(x−3)x(x−4)y=(x−1)(x−3)x(x−4) where x=2x=2 remains a line of symmetry.

Graphs of all cubics have rotational symmetry about their point of inflection (for y=x3y=x3, the point of inflection is the origin). The cubic y=x3−6x2+9x+1y=x3−6x2+9x+1, shown in Figure 4, has rotational symmetry about the point (2,3)(2,3). For further exploration, check out Cubic Spin on NRICH.

Symmetry is not restricted to shapes. Algebraic expressions that remain unchanged by a permutation of its variables are considered symmetric. For example, xy+yz+zxxy+yz+zx is symmetric whereas x2+y2−z2x2+y2−z2 is not. One of way of thinking about algebraic symmetry is to assign numerical values to the variables, say x=1x=1, y=2y=2 and z=3z=3. Any permutation of these values, e.g. x=2x=2, y=3y=3, z=1z=1, does not change the result of the expression. We say that the variables are interchangeable.

We can explore algebraic symmetry to factorise expressions. A STEP 1 question (2007, question 4) explores the symmetry of x3−3xbc+b3+c3x3−3xbc+b3+c3. It is given that (x+b+c)(x+b+c) is a factor and we are asked to find the quadratic factor. Since the cubic expression is symmetric, its factorisation should also be symmetric. The symmetry in the linear factor (x+b+c)(x+b+c) dictates that the quadratic factor needs to be symmetric as well. By inspection, we can see that the quadratic factor should have the terms x2x2, b2b2 and c2c2. So far we have:

x3−3xbc+b3+c3=(x+b+c)(x2+b2+c2+ some symmetric expression in x, b, c).x3−3xbc+b3+c3=(x+b+c)(x2+b2+c2+ some symmetric expression in x, b, c).

The expression in xx, bb, cc must complete the symmetry of the quadratic factor. The term −3xbc−3xbc on the LHS of the identity suggests cross products. It is not hard to check that −xb−xc−bc−xb−xc−bc works and the factorisation becomes:

x3−3xbc+b3+c3=(x+b+c)(x2+b2+c2−xb−xc−bc).x3−3xbc+b3+c3=(x+b+c)(x2+b2+c2−xb−xc−bc).

Now consider the symmetric expression

a3+b3+c3−a2b−a2c−b2c−c2a−c2b+2abc.a3+b3+c3−a2b−a2c−b2c−c2a−c2b+2abc.

Given that one of its factors is (a−b−c)(a−b−c), can you factorise it fully? Note that, unlike the previous example, (a−b−c)(a−b−c) is not symmetric and yet the factorisation must be. How can you complete the symmetry?

We have discussed symmetric graphs and equations so how about curves defined by symmetric equations? It is not surprising that curves with symmetric equations exhibit symmetry. For example, the Follium de Descartes is an algebraic curve defined by the symmetric equation

x3+y3−3xy=0.x3+y3−3xy=0.

Since xx and yy are interchangeable, if a point (a,b)(a,b) lies on the curve then the point (b,a)(b,a) must also lie on the curve. Therefore the curve has reflection symmetry in the line y=xy=x. Figure 5 shows a sketch of the Follium of Descartes. Note that the line y+x+1=0y+x+1=0 is an asymptote.

Another example of a symmetric equation is

x2y+y2x+x2+y2−8xy=0.x2y+y2x+x2+y2−8xy=0.

You can use Desmos to sketch its curve. Even better, you can create and sketch your own symmetric equation!

Symmetry is such an important concept in mathematics that some may argue that mathematics is all about identifying patterns and symmetries, and understanding the reasons behind them. No wonder Underground Maths includes symmetry in their Pervasive Ideas.
