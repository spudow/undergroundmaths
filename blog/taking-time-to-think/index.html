Taking the time to think mathematically

Brian Hudson

In the months of February and March 2017, Underground Mathematics held two workshops in Cambridge focusing on the themes of mathematical thinking and pedagogical actions to promote mathematical thinking. The workshops were led by Prof John Mason and attended by teachers, students, resource developers, researchers, and teacher trainers. The following blog post contains reflections on the first Mathematical Thinking workshop from the esteemed Prof Brian Hudson.


Driving to Cambridge on the morning of the first day of the Underground Mathematics workshop on Mathematical Thinking provided some welcome time to reflect after a week that had been much busier than expected. This time also provided an opportunity to think further about the tasks that had been set prior to the workshop but although I was looking forward to the day with a high degree of anticipation, I did feel quite under-prepared. It was quite a long time since I had attended a workshop as a participant and it was quite clear from the information provided in advance that we were all going to be participating! It was also some time since I had actively engaged in doing mathematics and I had a feeling that my mathematical thinking would be very ‘rusty’. Very soon after my arrival, our group became involved in mathematical activity and worked on the “Classify” task:

Classify or characterise all the numbers that can be expressed as one more than the product of four consecutive integers.

There had been a general introduction to the aims of the day by John and Barbara but nothing specific to the particular tasks, which John distributed on separate sheets of paper at the start. The chosen medium brought with it an element of surprise and also provided a common starting point for all members of the group.

In reflecting on this particular task later that morning, it was noticeable that we spent quite a considerable amount of time working as six individuals in our group. Little was said and there was a lot of rough working. For my part, I spent a considerable amount of time feeling stuck on this first task. My initial strategy was to focus on the product of four integers and then to look for differences between specific consecutive examples e.g. 1,2,3,4 then 2,3,4,5 etc. On reflection later in the day I asked myself why I had paid so much attention to the “product of four consecutive integers” at the expense of “one more than” and why I had not started with the simplest case. Through small group and whole group discussion, I came to see the resolution of the problem at which point it seemed blindingly obvious. However it was so important that I had struggled, experienced the feeling of “being STUCK” (Mason et al., 2010) and eventually took actions that helped me towards seeing the resolution of the problem. This process took some time and did not happen quickly.

Later in the day I was very struck by the contribution to the whole group discussion from Tabitha when she asked an open question about the amount of time these tasks had taken. She drew attention to the pressure that teachers can feel under in the classroom on a daily basis and also referred back to her own experience as a classroom teacher of being observed by senior leaders in the school. In doing so she raised the issue of laying oneself open to being criticised by such colleagues in terms of the “pace and challenge of the lesson”.

The issue of almost constant classroom observation really resonated with similar accounts that I have heard in recent years from some of the teachers I have worked with. Even more shocking to me have been accounts of individual teachers being judged against the Ofsted rating scale from outstanding to inadequate on a regular and almost routine basis. Also of concern in what Tabitha reported are the assumptions, on the part of the senior leaders she has in mind, which might underlie such concerns about pace and challenge. I asked myself “What is pace in such a context?” Is it being confused with speed? If so, is it the speed of observable actions, such as completing an exercise of 20 questions neatly in an exercise book? Is that what they might be looking for? And if so what might they make of my rough working which was all that I had to show after the first half hour of the day.

Tabitha’s account also had particular resonance with one of the themes to emerge from our study into developing mathematical thinking at the primary level in Scotland (Hudson et al., 2015). We identified this as “being allowed” and it arose from the analysis of interviews with some of the teachers involved in the project.

One teacher, Angela, reflected on her sense of liberation in “being allowed” to write things down and draw a picture and in turn to allow her pupils to do likewise. Another teacher, Alice described how she had built her confidence by “being allowed” to take time over her teaching of mathematics.

‘I think being allowed to actually take time over a piece of mathematics. (….) it’s not just a case of getting through 15 pages of maths. I’m quite happy now to get through a couple of written pages, I look at the concepts that you are being asked to teach and I tackle them in different ways, I choose a variety of teaching methods and I find the children respond much better to this as well.’

We theorise the theme of “being allowed” with reference to Bernstein’s (2000) concept of framing in education that can be seen as a way of describing the locus of control over the selection, sequencing, pacing and criteria for evaluating or assessing what counts as legitimate knowledge. A major issue in our project was the locus of control between central regulation and the individual teacher. For example, if there are highly prescriptive national curriculum regulations that leave limited possibilities for teachers to plan their teaching practice, then the framing is strong. In contrast weaker framing provides teachers with more freedom to choose their methods of teaching and learning. However the teachers’ own experience as learners of mathematics highlighted the negative impact of the strong framing over the criteria for evaluating and assessing of the traditional formal assessment system. This was especially so at secondary school level for some of these teachers and related to experiences of being labelled as “not able to do mathematics”.

Also in our project we observed how the impact of excessive high stakes external testing can result in too much emphasis on drill and practice associated with superficial memorised and algorithmic reasoning. In turn such emphasis can create the conditions that lead to an experience for learners of mathematics that is fearful and anxiety inducing, boring and demotivating and alienating from the subject itself. Reflecting back on Tabitha’s contribution, such constant classroom observation and concern for “pace and challenge” will inhibit most teachers from taking risks to spend the necessary time to promote mathematical thinking and most likely reinforce the drive towards superficial memorised and algorithmic reasoning.

We contrast this with an approach which presents mathematics as fallible, refutable and uncertain and which promotes critical thinking, creative reasoning, the generation of multiple solutions and of learning from errors and mistakes. This perspective is reflected in US Standards of the National Council of Teachers of Mathematics (NCTM, 2000) that recognise reasoning and proof as fundamental aspects of mathematics:

People who reason and think analytically tend to note patterns, structure, or regularities in both real-world situations and symbolic objects; they ask if those patterns are accidental or if they occur for a reason; and they conjecture and prove (p. 56).

In conclusion I propose that such creativity needs to be recognised as an orientation or disposition toward mathematical activity that can be fostered broadly in school. However sufficient time needs to be given to the development of such creativity, central to which is learning from errors and mistakes and last but not least taking the time to think mathematically.

Mason, J., Burton, L. and Stacey, K. (2010) Thinking Mathematically, Harlow: Prentice Hall.

NCTM (2000) Principles and standards for school mathematics, , Reston, VA: The National Council of Teachers of Mathematics.

 

About the author

Brian Hudson is Professor of Education, and was formerly Head of School (2012-16), in the School of Education and Social Work at the University of Sussex. He is the main Organizer of the World Education Research Association (WERA) International Research Network on Didactics - Learning and Teaching; a member of the WERA Outreach Committee; an Associate Editor of the Journal of Curriculum Studies and a Board member the Teacher Education Policy in Europe (TEPE) Network. He was awarded a National Teaching Fellowship in 2004 and awarded Honorary Membership of the EERA Network on Didactics - Learning and Teaching in 2016. Currently is also Honorary Professor at the University of Dundee; Adjunct Professor at the University of Ghana and Guest Professor at Karlstad University, Sweden. He is a Fellow of the IMA and a Chartered Mathematician.
