Welcome to the brand new Underground Mathematics blog!

Rob Beckett and Susie Parkin

An Underground Mathematics Champion initiative, this will be a place to celebrate mathematics, featuring posts that consider interesting ways to teach and learn mathematics. There will be posts that make you wonder, ask questions and more! We intend to host a post from a different author every fortnight. We are very excited about the launch of the blog and invite anyone interested in writing for us to contact blog@undergroundmathematics.org.


In this post, we feature some of our favourite Underground Mathematics resources…

Odd or even or… (Rob Beckett)

I really like this task as it develops student thinking about the symmetry of functions and whether they are odd, even or something else.

The warm up task offers students an excellent opportunity to discuss the symmetry of functions, be it reflection or rotational. It provides a particularly nice way of introducing the idea of odd and even functions to students through a concept they are very familiar with.

The main problem is one of my favourite types of activity, a card sort. The task provides space for deep and thoughtful discussion in pairs or groups of students and opens way to the idea that more ‘simple’ functions, such as f(x)=−1f(x)=−1, can hold ‘interesting’ properties (in this case being an even function). It also raises an important question: Is the function f(x)=(x−1)3f(x)=(x−1)3, for example, an odd or an even function (see Figure 1)?

Well, the answer is clearly neither, since the definitions of odd and even functions deal with rotational and reflective symmetry about the origin. Now this function, f(x)f(x), does have symmetry but around a different point which results in the function not having either property. This is rather interesting and provides key and vital discussions for students developing their ideas about this concept.

Parabella (Susie Parkin)

This is a task that sits at the Quadratics station and is lovely to look at during the start of year 12, as it links together dealing with quadratics and straight lines, posed as a question which brings out an interesting property of parallel chords on a parabola.

The context of the problem has you wondering what kind of techniques would be required in order to get anywhere, as it is not immediately obvious. It is a great task to use to encourage students to explore and discuss what information is given and the possible ways of working with it. This is a really useful skill, as students are thereby beginning to develop strategies for ‘what to do when you don’t know what to do’.

Parabella draws together recognising and using the difference of two squares, and working with gradients and mid-points of line segments, but in a different context.
