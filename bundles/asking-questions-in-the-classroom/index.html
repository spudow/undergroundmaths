Asking questions in the classroom
Add this bundle to your resource collection as a subcollection

We want to encourage our students to talk about mathematics and to ask questions of themselves, peers and their teachers. But what questions could we, as teachers, ask that will facilitate this in the classroom? The resources in this bundle provide examples of questions that are typical to Underground Mathematics resources and that can be used when working on any mathematical problem.

  • Why use this bundle?
  • How to use this bundle
  • Related webinar
  • 1.
    Transformers Combining Functions
    This resource offers students an opportunity to think more deeply about how graphs of functions are related to one another and encourages them to think about transformations when comparing similar graphs.
  • 2.
    Problem areas Introducing Calculus
    Students are asked to decide which of two functions has the greater area between x=1x=1 and x=2x=2 without using calculus. The functions share several key features, but one of the functions is not drawn for them, so students will have to sketch this function before thinking about the similarities and differences between the two.
  • 3.
    Sector spirals Circles
    Students are asked to calculate a selection of arc lengths and sector areas in a given diagram. They are also encouraged to think about how adaptations to the diagram affect these values and to think about the special case where arc length is equal to sector area.
  • 4.
    Trigsy integrals Calculus of Trigonometry & Logarithms
    Students are asked to think about the different ways they can integrate trigonometric functions such as sin2xsin2⁡x and arccosxarccos⁡x using their knowledge of these functions and basic ideas from calculus. They do not need to have met any trigonometric integration before.
  • 5.
    Choose your families Calculus meets Functions
    A resource offering practice in thinking about features of graphs and functions. Students decide which features of the graphs they will use to put the graphs into families.

Why use this bundle?

Consider the following two questions before reading further:

  1. When do you and your students ask questions in the mathematics classroom?
  2. What types of questions are asked and why do we ask them?

As discussed in other bundles, “asking questions of themselves, peers and their teachers” is a mathematical habit that we want to encourage students to develop. We do not assume that students already know how to ask ‘good’ questions and it is our role as teachers to model this in the classroom so that students can begin to understand what makes a question worth asking.

Underground Mathematics resources are designed to support teachers to do this. We think very carefully about the questions that are used to frame a mathematical problem and suggest questions throughout each resource, often in (brown) chalk boxes. These questions:

  • help students to get started
  • help to reveal misconceptions
  • require students to think more deeply about their methods and approach to a problem
  • encourage students to notice connections between concepts and problems in mathematics

The resources featured in this bundle provide specific examples of these types of questions, many of which are typical of Underground Mathematics resources and can be used when working on any mathematical problem.

    How to use this bundle

    When looking at these resources, it may be useful to think about four broad types of questions, listed below, and where and how they are used. When you see a question suggested in a resource it is often helpful to take a moment to think about why it was asked in that way and what kind of response the author was intending to provoke.

    1. Questions that are used to frame a problem, such as:
      • Which is bigger?
      • How could you sort these?
      • How many ways can you find to do this?
    2. Questions that are used to help students to get started on a problem, such as:
      • What can you say about this? (What do you see?)
      • What might you do first? Why?
      • What’s the same and what’s different?
    3. Questions that are used to help students to think more deeply about a problem, such as:
      • How else could you represent this? (What does it look like?)
      • Is there another way of doing this?
      • What happens if you change …?
      • How do you know that’s true?
      • What else would you like to know?
      • Can you give another example / non-example of that?
    4. Questions that encourage students to reflect, such as:
      • What do you expect / predict will happen?
      • Why did you do that?
      • Are you convinced about that?
      • How is your strategy the same as / different from …?

    We would expect that many of the questions suggested in a resource could be used directly by teachers in the classroom. Also, some questions may require more thinking time than others in order for students to respond meaningfully. In addition to this, we suggest examples of key questions in the teacher notes within each resource.

    There are frequently many questions offered in a resource, and we would not expect them all to be used in a single lesson. Instead, each teacher will make judgements based on their knowledge of their own students and their desired outcomes when using that particular resource in that specific lesson.
