Bundles
Bundle of resources

Developing a mathematical classroom
Add this bundle to your resource collection as a subcollection

The five resources in this bundle help students develop a common base to build on, including emphasising certain ways of working. The resources can be tackled using ideas from GCSE mathematics but are non-routine problems.

  • Why use this bundle?
  • How to use this bundle
  • Related webinar
  • 1.
    Between the lines Geometry of Equations
    Find the area of a region between two straight lines given minimal information. This task involves the geometry and equations of straight lines.
    Emphasis on
    • Asking questions
    • Drawing on experience
    • Importance of the journey
  • 2.
    A tangent is ... Introducing Calculus
    This resource is designed to provoke discussion and to help build up a sense of what a tangent is. Students are presented with several descriptions and asked to find or sketch an example to show that the proposed definition does not work for all curves.
    Emphasis on
    • Talking and collaboration
    • Justifying ideas
    • Communicating ideas
  • 3.
    A perfect fit Thinking about Geometry
    This problem requires students to develop a strong chain of reasoning as they are asked how they would construct a circle inside a right-angled triangle. As the problem develops there are opportunities to make connections between algebraic and geometric representations.
    Emphasis on
    • Drawing on experience
    • Justifying ideas
  • 4.
    Quadratic solving sorter Quadratics
    Students are often unclear as to which is the most appropriate method for solving a particular quadratic equation. In this resource they sort a set of equations according to how they might solve them.
    Emphasis on
    • Talking and collaboration
    • Asking questions
    • Justifying ideas
  • 5.
    Name that graph Quadratics
    Students are encouraged to think about the different forms of a quadratic equation and how they are related to a quadratic graph.
    Emphasis on
    • Drawing on experience
    • Importance of the journey
    • Communicating ideas

Why use this bundle?

A Level mathematics is built upon ideas that students have met throughout their schooling. Indeed, it is often the case that the A Level course begins with ‘revision’ of some of these ideas, offering an opportunity for students with differing prior mathematical experiences to have a common base to build on. The five resources in this bundle can be used to achieve this. The resources can be tackled using ideas from GCSE mathematics but are non-routine problems that also challenge students, helping them to build resilience. The higher level of mathematical thinking that may emerge from grappling with these problems can in turn result in a deeper and more sustained understanding of the relevant mathematical ideas.

The resources in this bundle are also designed to promote certain ways of working that can enhance students’ experiences of mathematics. Each resource has been carefully selected because it encourages students to develop some of the following mathematical ‘habits’:

  • Talking about mathematics and working collaboratively to explore a problem
  • Asking questions of themselves, peers and their teachers
  • Drawing on previous mathematical experiences when looking at an unfamiliar problem
  • Appreciating the importance of the journey through a problem, rather than the final solution
  • Attempting to justify ideas and asking others to do the same
  • Communicating mathematical ideas effectively to others in the classroom

How to use this bundle

We envisage these five resources being used as a package as each resource focuses on different mathematical content and habits of mind. They are presented in an order that takes students on a journey through some mathematical ideas. However, we do not expect every teacher to use the bundle in exactly the same way. You may choose to use the five resources in a sequence of five lessons, you may choose to intersperse them with your own resources, guided by the needs of your students at the time.

It is also worth bearing in mind that these resources do not have to be used at the beginning of year 12. It may be just as valuable to use or revisit these resources at other points in the A Level course, to re-establish a ‘problem-solving’ classroom, to revise ideas and to reflect on new techniques that may influence students’ approaches to the same problems.
