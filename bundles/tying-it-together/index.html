Bundle of resources

Tying it together
Add this bundle to your resource collection as a subcollection

How do we 'revise' effectively for a mathematics exam? The resources in this bundle encourage students to bring together their experiences in multiple areas of mathematics, helping to reveal and reinforce connections between a variety of mathematical ideas and problem solving strategies.

  • Why use this bundle?
  • How to use this bundle
  • Related webinar
  • 1.
    Pick a card... Quadratics
    A set of toggle cards revealing different properties of a quadratic; from one revealed card, students are asked to fill in the rest.
  • 2.
    Can we identify the right sketch of a transformed function? Combining Functions

    Consider a combination of transformations performed on a function. What do we know about the features of the graph of the resulting function?

  • 3.
    Slices of ππ Trigonometry: Triangles to Functions
    When is sinθsin⁡θ bigger than cosθcos⁡θ? When is tanθtan⁡θ smaller than cosθcos⁡θ? In this resource, inequalities of the form sinθ<tanθ<cosθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ<cos⁡θ have been used to slice up a semi-circle. Which inequality defines each slice?
  • 4.
    Can we find the three inequalities that define this region? Geometry of Equations

    This problem asks students to use inequalities to define a region enclosed by an ellipse, a straight line and the xx-axis. Students are not required to have met the equation of an ellipse before tackling this problem.

  • 5.
    Two-way calculus Calculus meets Functions

    Students generate examples and identify common features by linking together stationary points, increasing and decreasing behaviour, integration and differentiation. For detailed notes on this resource, and examples of student work, please see Two-way calculus - teacher support.

  • 6.
    What's the shared area for these two circles? Circles

    This resource is an example of where a good diagram can reveal helpful information, including symmetry, which might otherwise be missed. Sector areas, Pythagoras’ Theorem and Trigonometry are some of the mathematical ideas brought together in this problem.

Why use this bundle?

At various stages students will ‘revise’ the mathematics that they have covered, often in preparation for some kind of assessment. Schools, mathematics departments and indeed the students themselves may have an idea about what ‘revision’ in mathematics should look like and this will usually include extensive work with past examination papers. Whilst acknowledging the benefits of becoming familiar with the format and style of an examination, we should also explicitly support students to:

  • Recognise where and how prior learning can be applied to a question that they have never seen before
  • Identify areas in which their understanding is less well formed
  • Reflect on where and how connections between mathematical ideas could be made
  • Draw on mathematical behaviours and problem solving strategies to see a way into a problem, and to help them to become ‘unstuck’ when necessary
  • Reflect on whether there are more efficient approaches to particular problems (or parts of problems), making use of alternative representations where possible

Each resource in this bundle challenges students to revisit and re-examine their prior experiences in multiple areas of mathematics. They include opportunities for students to work on unfamiliar problems in which they need to make choices about the mathematics to be used and the approaches they might take.

    How to use this bundle

    As mentioned above, each resource in this bundle explores multiple areas of mathematics covered in the first year of A level. It will be useful to spend some time familiarising yourself with the variety of approaches, possible misconceptions and mathematical connections that could be made when students are exploring each problem. The Teacher notes, Suggestions, Things you might have noticed, and Solution sections of each resource may help to reveal these and provide prompting questions that could be asked to help students to get the most out of time spent on each problem.

    All of the resources are designed to be used in a classroom setting, where students have the opportunity to talk about what they are doing, to discuss and reflect on different approaches. The resources can also be used by students working independently. Either way, the teacher can play an essential role in guiding students to ‘revise’ effectively, using thoughtful questions to encourage students to work on the elements of effective revision listed above (in the Why use this bundle section).

    Some of the resources, such as Pick a card, could be used as several shorter activities over a series of lessons, reinforcing messages about multiple representations in mathematics as well as refining students’ fluency with quadratics.
