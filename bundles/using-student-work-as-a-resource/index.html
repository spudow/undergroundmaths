Bundle of resources

Using student work as a resource
Add this bundle to your resource collection as a subcollection

The resources in this bundle provide a variety of opportunities for students (and teachers) to analyse and critique different approaches to mathematical problems. They have been selected from a variety of stations and can be used at any stage in the A Level course.

  • Why use this bundle?
  • How to use this bundle
  • Related webinar
  • 1.
    Name that graph again Combining Functions

    Students think about different ways in which they can determine the equation of a parabola given the coordinates of three points on the curve.

    Some ‘student starting points’ are provided that can be used to emphasise different approaches and encourage students to think differently about the problem.

  • 2.
    Can you find... curvy cubics edition Calculus meets Functions

    This activity asks students to think about how they can use information about derivatives and stationary points to work “backwards” to possible cubic curves.

    Three examples of student work are presented (you will need to scroll down the page to find it).

  • 3.
    Discriminating Quadratics

    Discriminating can be used to consolidate understanding of the discriminant and make connections between graphs and algebra. Students are given statements about the number of solutions of a quadratic equation and asked to decide whether MUST, MAY or CAN’T is the correct choice of word within the statement.

    Sample student work can be found in the teacher support for this resource Discriminating - teacher support.

  • 4.
    Two-way functions Thinking about Functions

    This resource gives students an opportunity to explore their understanding of roots and asymptotes of functions, both by identifying properties that functions have in common and also by seeking functions that have particular properties.

    Sample student work can be found in the teacher support for this resource Two-way functions - teacher support.

Why use this bundle?

When students are asked to work on rich mathematical problems they can become preoccupied with reaching a solution as quickly as possible, unaware of the key choices they have made in their approach. They may also be confused about what an acceptable solution might look like and how it will be assessed.

The resources chosen in this bundle are accompanied by some examples of student work that can be used in the classroom to draw out more from the original problem. We hope that exposing students to some of these different approaches will help them to understand that there is very rarely just one correct way of tackling a mathematical problem.

All of the resources can be used to build upon the mathematical behaviours discussed in the Developing a mathematical classroom bundle.

  • Asking questions of themselves, peers and their teachers
  • Communicating mathematical ideas effectively to others in the classroom
  • Drawing on previous mathematical experiences when looking at an unfamiliar problem
  • Appreciating the importance of the journey through a problem, rather than the final solution
  • Attempting to justify ideas and asking others to do the same

How to use this bundle

Please be aware that the resource links above take you directly to those pages that contain examples of student work (this is not always the main problem page of the resource).

The resources in this bundle can be used with students or teachers to support discussion of how we assess work on rich mathematical problems. As with the Developing a mathematical classroom bundle, they provide an opportunity to establish a shared understanding of classroom expectations in mathematical thinking.

In all cases it is essential that the students or teachers have had an opportunity to grapple with the main problem, both individually and in collaboration with peers, before they encounter and attempt to make sense of other student or sample work.

Many of the examples of student work are accompanied by questions that encourage a deeper engagement with the material. In the longer term we hope to encourage students to continually ask the questions:

  • What is this student doing?
  • Why are they doing that?
  • What could they do next?

If students can internalise these questions, and begin to pose them routinely as they work on mathematical problems, then they are more likely to be able to communicate their thinking and make more conscious and productive decisions about their approach.
