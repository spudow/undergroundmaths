Chain Rule & Integration by Substitution
Fluency exercise

I can see uu!

Solution

If u=2x+1u=2x+1 and y=u3y=u3 what is yy as a function xx?

If u=2x+1u=2x+1 then substituting 2x+12x+1 for uu in y=u3y=u3 gives y=(2x+1)3.y=(2x+1)3.

Working backwards, if y=(2x+3)−2y=(2x+3)−2, how could you express yy as a function of uu where uu is a function of xx?

Looking at y=(2x+3)−2y=(2x+3)−2, I might describe it as “one over something squared”, so one option is to let the “something” be u.u. In a function machine for yy, the squaring could happen before or after taking the reciprocal, giving further options.

Here are some possibilities for writing yy as a function of uu where uu is a function of x.x.

  • y=u−2y=u−2 and u=2x+3u=2x+3

  • y=u−1y=u−1 and u=(2x+3)2u=(2x+3)2

  • y=u2y=u2 and u=(2x+3)−1u=(2x+3)−1

However, there is no reason why yy can’t be the identity function of uu or uu can’t be the identity function of xx, so there are two other possibilities.

  • y=(2u+3)−2y=(2u+3)−2 and u=xu=x

  • y=uy=u and u=(2x+3)−2u=(2x+3)−2

If you also wanted to work out dydxdydx, how would this affect your choices of uu?

As yy is a function of a function, I can use the chain rule to differentiate it if I can find dydudydu and dudx.dudx. Can I do this for all the possibilities I found for uu?

For the first case I can find dydudydu and dudxdudx directly. In the second and third cases, I can find dydudydu but need to think a bit more about how I could differentiate u.u. The chain rule could help, or I could look at Slippery slopes …. another derivative to help me.

The final two cases don’t help me either because I’d need the chain rule to find dydudydu or dudx.dudx.

Take a look at the functions in the table below.

Write yy as a function of uu where uu is a function of xx so that you can use these to find dydx.dydx.

You may be able to do this in more than one way.

y=(5−2x)3y=(5−2x)3 y=3x−1⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√y=3x−1 y=5x⎯⎯√y=5x
y=9x2−6x+1y=9x2−6x+1 y=e5xy=e5x y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x
y=1x2+4x+4y=1x2+4x+4 y=ex+4y=ex+4 y=lnx2y=ln⁡x2
y=cotxy=cot⁡x y=xy=x y=lnx2+ln8xy=ln⁡x2+ln⁡8x
y=sinx2y=sin⁡x2 y=tanx(sec2x−1)y=tan⁡x(sec2⁡x−1) y=12(1−cos2x)y=12(1−cos⁡2x)

I can tackle these functions in any order, but scanning the table suggests a few ways to group the functions. As in the previous example, I could use the identity function and say either y=uy=u or u=xu=x, but this won’t necessarily help me to find dydxdydx, so I’ll need to look for other options in most cases. I’ll find possibilities for uu first then try to differentiate. There is a table with suggestions for uu and expressions for dydxdydx at the end of this section.

Possibilities for uu

The functions in the top row are all powers of a linear polynomial. This makes me wonder if I could also express 9x2−6x+19x2−6x+1 and 1x2+4x+41x2+4x+4 as powers of a linear polynomial.

For y=1x2+4x+4y=1x2+4x+4 I can choose u=x+2u=x+2 and y=u−2.y=u−2. But I could also choose u=(x+2)2u=(x+2)2 and y=1u.y=1u. If I now look at 5x⎯⎯√5x with this in mind, I notice that it can also be written as 25x⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√.25x. How does this affect my choices for uu?

Several functions in the table involve either exponential or logarithmic functions and I can see more than one uu for several of these functions. For example, e5xe5x could be thought of as 5x5x composed with exex, but also as exex composed with x5.x5. In a similar way, rewriting the logarithms may help me to see different possibilities for u.u. However, if I am rewriting expressions, I need to think about which values of xx the original expression is defined for, and whether my new expression is also defined for all these values of x.x. For example, where are lnx2ln⁡x2 and 2lnx2ln⁡x defined?

The logarithms in y=lnx2+ln8xy=ln⁡x2+ln⁡8x can be combined and separated in different ways, including ln8x3ln⁡8x3, ln(2x)3ln⁡(2x)3 and 3ln2x3ln⁡2x, which gives me lots of options for u.u.

Looking now at the trigonometric functions at the bottom left of the table, I can see that sinx2sin⁡x2 is a function of x2.x2. But how can y=cotxy=cot⁡x be treated as a composition of functions? I recall that cotx=1tanxcot⁡x=1tan⁡x, which gives me an idea for u.u.

The other functions look a bit more complicated, and I may need to rewrite them using identities. For example, sec2x=1+tan2xsec2⁡x=1+tan2⁡x so maybe I can express tanx(sec2x−1)tan⁡x(sec2⁡x−1) in terms of tanx.tan⁡x.

I can think of 12(1−cos2x)12(1−cos⁡2x) as a function of cos2xcos⁡2x, or of 2x2x, but I also recognise it from the double angle formulae, where 12(1−cos2x)=sin2x.12(1−cos⁡2x)=sin2⁡x. This looks like a very different function!

The only remaining function in the table is y=xy=x, which looks like the simplest function in the table. I could have included y=xy=x in the powers of linear polynomials group, but there may be some other ways of thinking about it.

As a first step, I can think about the function that has no effect on its inputs: the identity function. So I can let u=xu=x and then I need y=u.y=u.

Looking around the table gives me an idea. I need the composition of two functions to have no effect, but each of the functions can change its inputs into something else. For example, I could let u=exu=ex and have y=lnu.y=ln⁡u. Similarly, provided x≠0x≠0, I could let u=1xu=1x and y=1u.y=1u. I need to be careful about the domains, but what other functions could I choose?

Looking back, I realise that almost all of the functions in the table can be thought of as a composition of a linear polynomial and another function. It may be interesting to think about how this affects their derivatives.

I also notice that I could think of a few of these functions as a composition of three functions. For example, for y=1x2+4x+4y=1x2+4x+4 I could have had u=x+2u=x+2, v=u2v=u2 and y=1v.y=1v. Which others could I break down using three functions instead of two? How do I differentiate a composition of three functions?

Finding the derivatives

I can use the chain rule to differentiate these functions. But as I found earlier, some of the possibilities for uu may not be as helpful as others.

For the powers of linear polynomials, I’ll just consider a few examples.

For y=(5−2x)3y=(5−2x)3, uu could be 5−2x5−2x and then y=u3.y=u3. Applying the chain rule gives dydx=3u2⋅−2=−6(5−2x)2dydx=3u2⋅−2=−6(5−2x)2, so the −2−2 from 5−2x5−2x is acting as a scale factor in the derivative. If I had (ax+b)3(ax+b)3 instead, what could I choose as aa and bb if I didn’t want to introduce a scale factor in the derivative like this?

Earlier I found that I could write y=9x2−6x+1y=9x2−6x+1 as a composition of functions, but I already know how to differentiate it directly, so I could just take y=uy=u and u=9x2−6x+1u=9x2−6x+1. How does differentiating y=9x2−6x+1y=9x2−6x+1 using these different functions compare with differentiating it directly?

Again, I can differentiate y=5x⎯⎯√y=5x directly, so I could have taken y=uy=u and u=5x⎯⎯√.u=5x. If instead I differentiate yy using u=x⎯⎯√u=x and y=5uy=5u then I get

dydx=−5u2⋅12x⎯⎯√=−52x3/2dydx=−5u2⋅12x=−52x3/2

but what happens if I use u=25xu=25x and y=u⎯⎯√y=u instead?

I saw two different possibilities for uu for e5xe5x and ex+4ex+4 and one might make the differentiation simpler than the other.

I also noticed that e5xe5x and ex+4ex+4 are both functions of linear polynomials, so when I differentiate them using the chain rule will I see the same effect of scale factors in the derivatives as I found for powers of linear polynomials?

Differentiating y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x using u=3xu=3x and y=lnuy=ln⁡u gives me

dydx=1u⋅3=1xdydx=1u⋅3=1x
which seems surprising. I might have expected a scale factor of 33 in the derivative, but looking at my other option for uu helps to confirm that my derivative is correct. Another thing to think about is that y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x is the inverse of an exponential function and I have already differentiated some exponential functions. What is the inverse of y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x and how could I use its derivative to find the derivative of y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x?

Looking now at y=lnx2y=ln⁡x2, I can use u=x2u=x2 and y=lnuy=ln⁡u to give

dydx=1u⋅2x=2xdydx=1u⋅2x=2x
but what if I had rewritten yy as y=2lnxy=2ln⁡x instead?

Turning now to y=lnx2+ln8xy=ln⁡x2+ln⁡8x, I have lots of options for uu based on different ways of rewriting lnx2+ln8xln⁡x2+ln⁡8x and some may help me to differentiate the function more simply than others. However, having differentiated y=lnx2y=ln⁡x2 and y=ln3xy=ln⁡3x, I can already conjecture what the derivative of y=lnx2+ln8xy=ln⁡x2+ln⁡8x is.

Again, I’ll just consider a couple of examples.

Using u=tanxu=tan⁡x and y=1uy=1u to differentiate y=cotxy=cot⁡x gives

dydx=−1u2⋅sec2x=−sec2xtan2xdydx=−1u2⋅sec2⁡x=−sec2⁡xtan2⁡x
and writing this in terms of sinxsin⁡x and cosxcos⁡x could help show how this could be simplified.

I had lots of options for uu for y=12(1−cos2x)y=12(1−cos⁡2x), but the first was u=cos2xu=cos⁡2x, which I could differentiate using the chain rule or ideas from Sine stretching. Alternatively I could use u=2xu=2x and y=12−12cosuy=12−12cos⁡u since then I can can find dydudydu directly. Both of these lead me to dydx=sin2x.dydx=sin⁡2x.

But I also noticed that I can write 12(1−cos2x)12(1−cos⁡2x) as sin2x.sin2⁡x. How does using u=sinxu=sin⁡x and y=u2y=u2 lead to the same derivative as the other routes?

I already know the derivative, but what happens if I write y=uy=u and u=xu=x and use the chain rule?

How do the different possibilities for writing y=xy=x as a composition of functions lead to this same result?

What other expressions could be seen as a composition of functions? Can you suggest some that you can also differentiate? Are there different options for uu as there were with many of the functions in this table?
