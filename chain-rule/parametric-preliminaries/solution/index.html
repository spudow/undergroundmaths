Solution

Take a look at triangle OPQOPQ in this diagram. Explain why PP has the coordinates given.

Imagine we move PP by increasing ∠QOP∠QOP, whilst keeping PQPQ perpendicular to the xx-axis. How could PP move if

  1. the length of OPOP is fixed but QQ can move along the xx-axis as required?

  2. QQ remains fixed but the length of OPOP can change?

In each case, can you express the coordinates of PP in terms of ∠QOP∠QOP ?

If the length of OPOP is fixed but QQ can move along the xx-axis, then as ∠QOP∠QOP increases, PP moves anticlockwise, tracing out an arc of a circle of radius 11, centre (0,0).(0,0).

If we write ∠QOP=t∠QOP=t then the locus of PP has parametric equations

What could the domain of tt be in this case?

Note that we’ve shown the path that PP traces out as ∠QOP∠QOP increases to slightly less than π.π. What value will ∠QOP∠QOP need to reach for PP to move round a full circle?

If the length of OPOP can change but QQ remains fixed at (12,0)(12,0), then PP will move up the line x=12x=12 from the point (12,3√2)(12,32) as ∠QOP∠QOP increases.

If we write ∠QOP=t∠QOP=t then can you explain why the locus of PP has these parametric equations?

Now try this

Sketch the curves or lines defined by the following parametric equations. Mark on your sketch any key values of tt and the direction in which the curve is traced out as tt increases.

  1. x=2tx=2t and y=3t−1y=3t−1 for t∈ℝ.t∈R.

  2. x=t3x=t3 and y=t2y=t2 for −2≤t≤2.−2≤t≤2.

  3. x=3sintx=3sin⁡t and y=3costy=3cos⁡t for 0<t<π.0<t<π.

For each curve, find a Cartesian equation (an equation that only involves xx and yy).

Although you may be able to find a Cartesian equation very easily, it is worth trying to sketch using the parametric equations as this may help with more complicated examples.

Both xx and yy vary linearly with tt, so we might expect a straight-line graph. What else do we need to know?

As tt increases, both xx and yy increase because they both have a positive coefficient for t.t. What does this tell us about the direction in which the line is traced out as tt increases?

We can now think about some important points. For example, when t=0t=0 then x=0x=0 and y=−1.y=−1. What is tt when y=0y=0?

Finally, note that yy increases at 3232 times the rate at which xx increases.

By eliminating tt from the two equations, we can obtain Cartesian equations, such as y=32x−1y=32x−1 or 3x−2y−2=0.3x−2y−2=0.

Note that when t=0t=0, x=0x=0 and y=0y=0 so the curve passes through the origin.

When tt is negative and increasing xx is negative and increasing and yy is positive but decreasing. When tt is positive and increasing both xx and yy are positive and increasing. Thus the graph is traced out from left to right and y≥0.y≥0.

Note too that replacing tt with −t−t gives the same value of yy. What effect does it have on xx? What does this tell us about the curve?

Thinking about how rapidly xx and yy change as tt increases, note that dydtdydt is linear, but dxdtdxdt is quadratic. Therefore as tt increases from 00, xx increases progressively faster compared to y.y. What about when tt is negative but increasing?

Finally we can substitute for tt to obtain the Cartesian equation y3=x2y3=x2 or y=x23y=x23, where −8≤x≤8−8≤x≤8.

Again, let’s start by thinking how xx and yy vary with t.t.

As tt increases from 00 to ππ we use our knowledge of trigonometric functions to note that xx increases from 00 to 33 and back again (with x=3x=3 when t=π2t=π2). The value of xx changes most quickly when tt is close to 00 or π.π.

As tt increases from 00 to ππ, yy decreases from 33 to −3.−3. When t=π2t=π2, y=0y=0 and yy is changing most rapidly.

The sketch suggests that the curve could be the part of the circle with Cartesian equation x2+y2=9x2+y2=9 for which 0<x≤30<x≤3 and −3<y<3−3<y<3. How can we eliminate tt from the parametric equations to obtain x2+y2=9x2+y2=9?

Can you suggest parametric equations for the same curve, but traced out anticlockwise instead? What if you wanted to trace out the full circle?

The parametric equations of a curve tell us about its shape by showing how xx and yy vary as the parameter varies. The equations may also tell us about the symmetry of the curve.

Just as there can be several Cartesian equations for the same curve, parametric equations are not unique. It may be more convenient to work with some than others, so it is worth exploring different parametrizations of the same curve.
