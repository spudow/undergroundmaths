Review question

How many normals to a parabola pass through PP?

Solution

Find the equation of the normal to the parabola y2=4axy2=4ax at the point (at2,2at)(at2,2at).

Approach 1: differentiate with respect to the parameter tt.

Differentiating with respect to tt, we see dydx=dy/dtdx/dt=2a2at=1tdydx=dy/dtdx/dt=2a2at=1t.

So the gradient of the normal at TT is −t−t.

The point (at2,2at)(at2,2at) is on the line which gives us its equation as y=−tx+at3+2aty=−tx+at3+2at.

Approach 2: find yy directly

We have y=2ax⎯⎯⎯⎯√y=2ax (taking the positive square root for the top half of the curve).

Now we can differentiate to find dydx=2⋅12(ax)−1/2a=ax⎯⎯⎯⎯√dydx=2⋅12(ax)−1/2a=ax, and at x=at2x=at2 (noting that tt is positive), this gives us dydx=1tdydx=1t. As above, we conclude the normal has gradient −t−t.

We get the same result for the bottom half of the curve.

Approach 3: implicit differentiation

Differentiating y2=4axy2=4ax with respect to xx gives, using the chain rule,

2ydydx=4a2ydydx=4a
and therefore the gradient at (at2,2at)(at2,2at) is 4a2y=4a4at=1t4a2y=4a4at=1t.

Prove that, if p2>8p2>8, two chords can be drawn through the point (ap2,2ap)(ap2,2ap) which are normal to the parabola at their second points of intersection, and that the line joining these points of intersection meets the axis of the parabola in a fixed point, independent of pp.

We seek points T=(at2,2at)T=(at2,2at) where the normal at TT passes through P=(ap2,2ap)P=(ap2,2ap).

So we need (ap2,2ap)(ap2,2ap) to lie on the line y=at3+2at−xty=at3+2at−xt.

Substituting in these values of (x,y)(x,y) we obtain

2ap=at3+2at−ap2t2ap=at3+2at−ap2t
or (cancelling the aa)
t3+(2−p2)t−2p=0.t3+(2−p2)t−2p=0.

We already know one solution: p=tp=t (because the normal at the point given by pp passes through the curve at this same point.)

So we can take out from this cubic a factor of (p−t)(p−t) to give

t2+pt+2=0.t2+pt+2=0.

A quadratic has two roots if and only if its discriminant is positive, or “b2−4ac>0b2−4ac>0”, which here gives simply p2−8>0p2−8>0, as required.

Finally, we consider the line joining T1=(at21,2at1)T1=(at12,2at1) and T2=(at22,2at2)T2=(at22,2at2) on the parabola (where t1t1 and t2t2 denote the two roots of the quadratic).

We want to find where this intersects the axis of the parabola, the line y=0y=0.

The gradient of the line is given by ‘the difference in yy coordinates divided by the difference in xx coordinates’, which is

2a(t2−t1)a(t22−t21)=2t1+t2.2a(t2−t1)a(t22−t12)=2t1+t2.

Hence the equation of the line is

y−2at1=2t1+t2(x−at21),y−2at1=2t1+t2(x−at12),
and by setting y=0y=0 we find
x=at21+12(t1+t2)(−2at1)=−at1t2.x=at12+12(t1+t2)(−2at1)=−at1t2.

We can recall now that the product of the roots of the quadratic x2+ax+b=0x2+ax+b=0 is given by bb, the constant term.

But in t2+pt+2=0t2+pt+2=0 the constant term is simply 22, giving the xx-intercept to be x=−2ax=−2a, which is indeed independent of pp.
