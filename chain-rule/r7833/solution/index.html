Chain Rule & Integration by Substitution
Review question

Can we show these tangents to this hyperbola form a square?

Solution

Sketch on the same axes the two curves C1C1 and C2C2, given by
C1C2:xy:x2−y2=1,=2.C1:xy=1,C2:x2−y2=2.

Note that C2C2 does not intersect the yy-axis.

The curves intersect at PP and QQ. Given that the coordinates of PP are (a,b)(a,b) (which you need not evaluate), write down the coordinates of QQ in terms of aa and bb.

If PP has coordinates (a,b)(a,b), then QQ has coordinates (−a,−b)(−a,−b).

This is easily checked by substituting back into the equations for C1C1 and C2C2.

The tangent to C1C1 through PP meets the tangent to C2C2 through QQ at the point MM, and the tangent to C2C2 through PP meets the tangent to C1C1 through QQ at NN. Show that the coordinates of MM are (−b,a)(−b,a) and write down the coordinates of NN.

The gradient of C1C1 at any point is given by:

y+xdydx=0⟹dydx=−yx.y+xdydx=0⟹dydx=−yx.
Similarly, the gradient of C2C2 at any point is given by
2x−2ydydx=0⟹dydx=xy.2x−2ydydx=0⟹dydx=xy.
 Thus the tangent to C1C1 at P(a,b)P(a,b) has equation
y−b=−ba(x−a),y−b=−ba(x−a),
 while the tangent to C2C2 at Q(−a,−b)Q(−a,−b) has equation
y+b=ab(x+a).y+b=ab(x+a).

The intersection of these two lines is at MM, so we solve the equations simultaneously to find the coordinates of MM.

In the process of doing this, we need to use the information that (a,b)(a,b) are the coordinates of point PP, that is, (a,b)(a,b) lies on both C1C1 and C2C2. So we should expect to use the equations
aba2−b2=1=2ab=1a2−b2=2

at some point.

We want to solve

abx+a2−b2b=−bax+2b.abx+a2−b2b=−bax+2b.
Multiplying through by abab, we can eliminate denominators, and since ab=1ab=1 we do not need to change the last term. We can also replace a2−b2a2−b2 by 22. We get
a2x+2a=−b2x+2b,a2x+2a=−b2x+2b,
 so
(a2+b2)x=2(b−a).(a2+b2)x=2(b−a).

We want to deal with a2+b2a2+b2. We could try to write it in terms of b−ab−a, since that’s on the right-hand side.

We have

a2+b2=(a−b)2+2ab=(a−b)2+2,a2+b2=(a−b)2+2ab=(a−b)2+2,
so the above equation becomes
[(a−b)2+2]x=2(b−a).[(a−b)2+2]x=2(b−a).

We can divide through by a−ba−b (it cannot be zero because a2−b2=2a2−b2=2), and use a2−b2=2a2−b2=2 to give 2a−b=a+b2a−b=a+b, using the familiar difference of two squares.

Thus the equation becomes

((a−b)+2a−b)x=−2,((a−b)+2a−b)x=−2,
that is,
(a−b+a+b)x=−2.(a−b+a+b)x=−2.
 Now we can tidy up to get
x=−1a=−b,x=−1a=−b,
 and using either of the equations of the tangents, say the first one, then gives
y=b2a+2b=b2+2aba=b2+2a=a.y=b2a+2b=b2+2aba=b2+2a=a.

So MM has coordinates (−b,a)(−b,a), as required.

Then we see immediately that NN has the coordinates (b,−a)(b,−a).

Show that PMQNPMQN is square.

Consider the two tangents through point PP:
y−by−b=−ba(x−a)=ab(x−a),y−b=−ba(x−a)y−b=ab(x−a),

and note that

−ba×ab=−1.−ba×ab=−1.
The tangent of C2C2 at PP is therefore also the normal of C1C1 at PP. By an analogous argument around PMQNPMQN all intersecting straight lines meet at right angles.

All we then need to do is show that one of the side lengths is equal to any one of the others, and indeed we have

|PM|=(a−−b)2+(b−a)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=2(a2+b2)⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√|PM|=(a−−b)2+(b−a)2=2(a2+b2)
and
|QN|=(−a−b)2+(−b−−a)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=2(a2+b2)⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√.|QN|=(−a−b)2+(−b−−a)2=2(a2+b2).

Thus PMQNPMQN is a square.
