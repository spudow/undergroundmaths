Appendix

This appendix reviews various formulae for finding the curved surface area and volume of a cone.

Surface area of a cone

Here’s a diagram of a cone. We’ve drawn some lengths and an angle on it.

When we cut the (slanted) surface of the cone in a straight line from the vertex to the base and then unwrap it, we end up with a sector of a circle, like this:

The radius of this sector is the slant length of the cone, which is ll. The outer arc of the sector was the circumference of the base circle of the cone, so its length is 2πr2πr.

Now the circumference of the whole of this circle is 2πl2πl, and therefore the sector is a fraction 2πr2πl2πr2πl of the whole circle. The area of the sector, and hence of the slanted surface of the cone, is therefore

2πr2πl×πl2=πrl.2πr2πl×πl2=πrl.

Alternatively, we could have worked out ϕϕ first, using the formula for the arc length of a sector “l=rθl=rθ”, giving in this case 2πr=lϕ2πr=lϕ, so ϕ=2πrlϕ=2πrl. Then the area of the sector is given by the formula “A=12r2θA=12r2θ”, so calculating 12l2.2πrl=πrl12l2.2πrl=πrl gives us the area as before.

It’s always good to check that our answers are sensible before going too far in a problem!

How could we do this here?

Here are a few things that we can do to check that our answer is sensible.

  1. Try special values.

    What values could we put in to our variables that would give us something we can check?

    In this case, if we make our cone very “flat”, so the vertex is barely above the base, the cone will look very much like a circular disk of radius rr. Then the slant length ll will be almost the same as rr, and the answer our formula gives us, πrsπrs will be almost the same as πrr=πr2πrr=πr2, which is the area of a circular disk of radius rr.

    This gives us some confidence that our answer is reasonable.

  2. Check the dimensions.

    The formula we have come up with, A=πrlA=πrl (where AA is the sloping surface area), must have correct dimensions to stand any chance of being correct.

    The left hand side is an area, measured in m2m2, so we say it has dimension [L2][L2], where [L][L] means a length.

    The right hand side is ππ, a pure number (so dimensionless), times rr, which is a length, times ll, which is a length. So it has dimension [L]×[L]=[L2][L]×[L]=[L2].

    So the dimensions of the left and right hand sides agree, giving us confidence that our answer is at least plausible.

Sometimes we will know some measurements more readily than others. So we could try rewriting this answer using other variables.

Since l2=r2+h2l2=r2+h2 using Pythagoras, we could also write the surface area as πrr2+h2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√πrr2+h2 or πll2−h2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√πll2−h2 if we wish.

If we draw a cross-section of the cone, we see that we have a right-angled triangle:

From this diagram, we see that h=lcosθh=lcos⁡θ and r=lsinθr=lsin⁡θ, so we can substitute for ll in our earlier answer πrlπrl to find that we can also write the surface area as

πrl=πrhcosθ=πr2sinθ.πrl=πrhcos⁡θ=πr2sin⁡θ.

Using the reciprocal trigonometric functions, we could also write these as A=πrhsecθ=πr2cosecθA=πrhsec⁡θ=πr2cosec⁡θ.

Volume of a cone

The area of the base of the cone is πr2πr2. The volume of any pyramidal shape is given by

13×base area×perpendicular height13×base area×perpendicular height
so in this case, the volume is 13πr2h13πr2h. This result can be proved using calculus.
