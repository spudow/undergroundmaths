Things you might have noticed

This diagram shows the ellipse with equation x2+4y2=16x2+4y2=16 together with the straight line y=x+cy=x+c.

The line and ellipse intersect at the points AA and BB, and the midpoint of ABAB is MM.

Find the coordinates of MM for some different values of cc.

Let’s say we begin by considering the case where c=1c=1, so we have the two equations x2+4y2=16x2+4y2=16 and y=x+1y=x+1.

Before we continue, let’s just stop and think. Why did we choose c=1c=1?

  • What does the case where c=1c=1 look like in the diagram?
  • What other values of cc could we have chosen?
  • Are there any values of cc that you would not choose? Why?
To find the coordinates of AA and BB, we substitute y=x+1y=x+1 into the quadratic to get
x2+4(x+1)2=16.x2+4(x+1)2=16.
 If we expand the brackets and rearrange, we get the quadratic
5x2+8x−12=0.(1)(1)5x2+8x−12=0.

We can use the formula to find the solutions of this quadratic: they are

x=−8±82+4×5×12⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√10.x=−8±82+4×5×1210.
We could simplify this, but instead we notice that these two solutions are the xx-coordinates of AA and BB. As the xx-coordinate of MM is the mean of these, we only need to add them together and halve the answer. When we add the two solutions together, the square roots cancel and we are left with −810+−810−810+−810. So the xx-coordinate of MM is half of this, which is −810=−45−810=−45.

Since MM lies on the straight line y=x+1y=x+1, it follows that the yy-coordinate of MM is −45+1=15−45+1=15. Hence MM has coordinates (−45,15)(−45,15).

We could have been even more sneaky in saving work for ourselves!

If we begin to complete the square for the quadratic in equation (1)(1), we have

5x2+8x−12=5(x2+85x)−12=5(x+45)2+k5x2+8x−12=5(x2+85x)−12=5(x+45)2+k
for some number kk. The graph y=5x2+8x−12y=5x2+8x−12 therefore has a line of symmetry x=−45x=−45 and so the two solutions are symmetrically placed about this xx-value, as shown in this sketch. It follows that their mean is −45−45.

And we could do this algebraically, too. If we write

5x2+8x−12=5(x−α)(x−β)=5(x2−(α+β)x+αβ),5x2+8x−12=5(x−α)(x−β)=5(x2−(α+β)x+αβ),
where the solutions (or roots) of the quadratic are αα and ββ, then we see that −5(α+β)=8−5(α+β)=8, so the sum of the roots is −85−85, and their mean is −45−45.

In the example above, we used c=1c=1 and found that MM had coordinates (−45,15)(−45,15). When you use other values of cc, you may find that the coordinates are also quite simple. There does seem to be an interesting pattern to the coordinates: what happens if you plot them?

What would happen if you used straight lines of a different gradient instead?

  • What is the locus of MM in these cases?

  • Can you find a relation between the gradient of the straight lines and the locus of MM?

A straight line through the centre of an ellipse is called a diameter of the ellipse.

The diameter y=mxy=mx and the locus of MM are called conjugate diameters. There are some fascinating theorems about conjugate diameters of an ellipse, which you might wish to research further.

This problem considered an ellipse. You might find it interesting to compare this with your answer to Parabella. You could also try answering the same question for a hyperbola such as x2−4y2=16x2−4y2=16, which is the other type of conic section. Does that also give a similar answer? If so, why, and if not, why not?
