Main solutions

Here are some examples for you to try your ideas out on. For each set of three points, find the equation of the circle passing through them.

It is certainly worth sketching a graph to help you understand what is going on in each case!

  1. A(3,2)A(3,2), B(3,6)B(3,6), C(5,8)C(5,8)

A geometric approach

In the suggestion, we saw that the centre of the circle must be the same distant from AA and BB, and it turns out that those points which are lie on the perpendicular bisector of the line segment ABAB.

If we draw a diagram with a point PP that is equidistant from AA and BB, and mark on the midpoint MM of ABAB, we get the following:

The triangles AMPAMP and BMPBMP are congruent by side-side-side (SSS):
AMPAMP=BM=PB=MPM is midpoint of ABP is equidistant from A and B (given)common sideAM=BMM is midpoint of ABPA=PBP is equidistant from A and B (given)MP=MPcommon side

Therefore the angles ∠AMP∠AMP and ∠BMP∠BMP are equal. But together they form a straight line (180∘180∘), so each angle is a right angle. Thus ∠AMP=90∘∠AMP=90∘ and the point PP lies on the perpendicular bisector of ABAB.

So if we draw the perpendicular bisector of ABAB, the circle’s centre must lie on it.

Likewise, if we draw the perpendicular bisector of ACAC or of BCBC, the circle’s centre must lie on that, too.

The point where these perpendicular bisectors meet must therefore be the centre of the circle through the points AA, BB and CC.

We can now see that the perpendicular bisector of ABAB is going to be the line y=4y=4 since AA and BB have the same xx-coordinate.

The perpendicular bisector of the chord BCBC passes through the midpoint of BCBC, which is (4,7)(4,7). (The midpoint of a line between two points can be found by averaging the coordinates of the two points: its coordinates are half way between the coordinates of BB and CC.)

The gradient of line through the points BB and CC is

m=8−65−3=22=1,m=8−65−3=22=1,
so the gradient of the line perpendicular to that is
m′=−1m=−11=−1m′=−1m=−11=−1
 (the negative reciprocal of mm).

The equation of this second perpendicular bisector is therefore
⟹y−7y=−(x−4)=11−x.y−7=−(x−4)⟹y=11−x.

We now need to find the point of intersection of these two lines as this is the centre of our circle. Solving the equations y=4y=4 and y=11−xy=11−x simultaneously gives the coordinates of their intersection, which is O(7,4)O(7,4). Finally, we want the radius of our circle so we work out the length |OA||OA|:

r=|OA|=(3−7)2+(2−4)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=20⎯⎯⎯⎯√.r=|OA|=(3−7)2+(2−4)2=20.
Therefore the equation of the circle is
(x−7)2+(y−4)2=20.(x−7)2+(y−4)2=20.

Incidentally, note that as the equation for the circle only needs r2r2 and not rr itself, we could have found r2=|OA|2r2=|OA|2 and dispensed with all square roots. We will do this from now on in this question.

Which method is quicker/easier here?

Tough call - which do you prefer?

It is interesting to notice that in the diagram for this part, the centre of the circle appears to lie on the chord ABAB. Is this really the case?

If the centre does lie on ABAB, then ABAB is a diameter, and therefore the angle ∠ACB∠ACB would have to be a right angle, as angles in semicircles are right angles. And the converse is true, too: if ∠ACB∠ACB is a right angle, then ABAB is a diameter. So we need only check whether ACAC and CBCB are perpendicular, and we can do this by calculating their gradients.

The gradient of ACAC is −1−(−5)2−(−1)=43−1−(−5)2−(−1)=43, and we have already calculated that the gradient of CBCB is −34−34. These multiply to −1−1 and so they are perpendicular.

It follows that the centre of the circle is the midpoint of ABAB, which is (−32,−32)(−32,−32), as we found earlier.
