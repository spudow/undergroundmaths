Circles
Review question

Can we show these two circles lie entirely outside each other?

Solution

Prove that the points whose coordinates satisfy the equation

x2+y2+2gx+2fy+c=0x2+y2+2gx+2fy+c=0
lie on a circle. State the coordinates of the centre of the circle and the length of its radius.

Completing the square gives us
(x+g)2−g2+(y+f)2−f2=c,(x+g)2−g2+(y+f)2−f2=c,
 or
(x+g)2+(y+f)2=g2+f2−c.(1)(1)(x+g)2+(y+f)2=g2+f2−c.

This looks like the standard equation of a circle, but the circle only exists if g2+f2−c>0g2+f2−c>0.

By Pythagoras’s theorem, the distance between the points (x1,y1)(x1,y1) and (x2,y2)(x2,y2) is

(x2−x1)2+(y2−y1)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√.(x2−x1)2+(y2−y1)2.

Taking square roots of both sides of (1)(1) gives

(x+g)2+(y+f)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=g2+f2−c⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√(x+g)2+(y+f)2=g2+f2−c
and so the distance between points (x,y)(x,y) and (−g,−f)(−g,−f) is g2+f2−c⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√g2+f2−c. Therefore all points which satisfy this equation are the same distance away from (−g,−f)(−g,−f), which shows they lie on a circle.

The circle has centre (−g,−f)(−g,−f) and radius g2+f2−c⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√g2+f2−c.

We have explained why the points satisfying

(x−a)2+(y−b)2=r2(x−a)2+(y−b)2=r2
lie on a circle with centre (a,b)(a,b) and radius rr; in this case, we had a=−ga=−g, b=−fb=−f and r=g2+f2−c⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√r=g2+f2−c.

In the next part of the question we can use this to quickly identify the centres and radii of the circles.

Prove that the circles

x2+y2−20x−16y+128=0x2+y2−20x−16y+128=0
and
4x2+4y2+16x−24y−29=04x2+4y2+16x−24y−29=0
 lie entirely outside each other, and find the length of the shortest distance from a point on one circle to a point on the other.

Call the first circle AA and the second circle BB.

Circle AA: Using the form of the first part, g=−10g=−10, f=−8f=−8, c=128c=128. So AA is a circle with centre (10,8)(10,8) and radius (−10)2+(−8)2−128⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=36⎯⎯⎯⎯√=6(−10)2+(−8)2−128=36=6.

Circle BB: Divide the equation by 44 to give

x2+y2+4x−6y−294=0x2+y2+4x−6y−294=0
which is of the same form as the first part. Then g=2g=2, f=−3f=−3, c=−294c=−294, so this is a circle with centre (−2,3)(−2,3) and radius 22+(−3)2+294⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=814⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=9222+(−3)2+294=814=92.

The distance between the circles’ centres is, by Pythagoras’ theorem,

(10−(−2))2+(8−3)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=169⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=13,(10−(−2))2+(8−3)2=169=13,
but for there to be a point inside both circles, it would have to be a distance of less than 66 away from one centre and a distance of less than 9292 from the other.

The sum of these distances, however, is only 10.510.5, less than the distance between the two centres themselves, so this cannot be.

So the circles lie entirely outside each other.

The points with the shortest distance between them are the points lying on the line joining the two centres.

To find the length of the shortest distance between the two circles consider the diagram below.

As calculated before, the length of the hypotenuse of the right-angled triangle in the diagram is 1313.

Therefore the shortest distance between points on the two circles is 13−(6+92)=5213−(6+92)=52.
