Combining Vectors
Review question

Can we find the angle between the planes ABCABC and ABDABD?

Solution

The position vectors of four points AA, BB, CC, DD relative to an origin OO are given below. The vectors ii, jj, kk are mutually perpendicular unit vectors.
A:B:C:D:2i+3j+k,2i+3j−k,2i+3i+k,2i+3j.+kA:2i+3j+k,B:2i+3j−k,C:2i+3i+k,D:2i+3j.+k

Find

  1. the equation (in any form) of the line ABAB,

We can write the position vectors AA to DD in column form as a=231a=(231), b=11−1b=(11−1), c=101c=(101) and d=030d=(030).

Writing vectors in column form rather than ii, jj, kk form for the calculations we are going to be doing reduces the likelihood of us mixing up the components of our vectors when we combine them.

A direction vector of the line ABAB is

AB−→=b−a=11−1−231=−1−2−2.AB→=b−a=(11−1)−(231)=(−1−2−2).

To avoid so many minus signs, we will use the direction vector p=−AB−→=122p=−AB→=(122) instead.

Any point on the line ABAB has position vector r=a+spr=a+sp for some real value of ss. Hence an equation of the line ABAB is r=231+s122r=(231)+s(122), where ss is any real number.

  1. the shortest distance between ABAB and CDCD,

Using the same method as above, we let q=−CD−→−=−(d−c)=1−31q=−CD→=−(d−c)=(1−31) (again, aiming to have fewer minus signs). Then an equation of the line CDCD is r=c+tq=101+t1−31r=c+tq=(101)+t(1−31), where tt is any real number.

Why have we used tt here and not ss again? On the other hand, we have used rr again; is this a problem?

Let’s suppose EE is on ABAB and FF is on CDCD with EFEF having the shortest distance between ABAB and CDCD. To help us understand this part, we’ll draw a quick sketch of the situation.

(Note that we have sketched EE as lying between AA and BB, but this may or may not be the case, and similarly with FF. It makes no difference to the arguments we will use.)

Because EFEF has the shortest distance between the two lines, it must be perpendicular to both of them. (Otherwise, if we fix EE, we can make EFEF shorter by moving FF until EFEF is perpendicular to CDCD, and likewise with moving EE.)

There are at least two very different ways to proceed at this point. We could either use scalar (dot) products or vector (cross) products. We will use scalar products here, and then we will show how the rest of this question could be answered in an alternative way using vector products.

Now we can write the position vectors of EE and FF as e=2+s3+2s1+2se=(2+s3+2s1+2s), f=1+t−3t1+tf=(1+t−3t1+t) for some values of ss and tt. This gives

EF−→=f−e=1+t−3t1+t−2+s3+2s1+2s=−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s.EF→=f−e=(1+t−3t1+t)−(2+s3+2s1+2s)=(−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s).

Since EF−→EF→ is perpendicular to both ABAB and CDCD, we can obtain two equations by noting that the scalar product of EF−→EF→ with both direction vectors pp and qq is zero:
EF−→.pEF−→.q=−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s.122=(−1+t−s)+2(−3t−3−2s)+2(t−2s)=0=−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s.1−31=(−1+t−s)−3(−3t−3−2s)+(t−2s)=0EF→.p=(−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s).(122)=(−1+t−s)+2(−3t−3−2s)+2(t−2s)=0EF→.q=(−1+t−s−3t−3−2st−2s).(1−31)=(−1+t−s)−3(−3t−3−2s)+(t−2s)=0

Simplifying these gives 7+9s+3t=07+9s+3t=0 and 8+3s+11t=08+3s+11t=0. Solving these simultaneous equations in ss and tt, we get s=−5390s=−5390, t=−1730t=−1730, so that

EF−→=−1+−1730+5390−3−1730−3+25390−1730+25390=−4445−11901118.EF→=(−1+−1730+5390−3−1730−3+25390−1730+25390)=(−4445−11901118).

The magnitude of EF−→EF→ is therefore (−4445)2+(−1190)2+(1118)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=119082+12+52⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=1110⎯⎯⎯⎯√30(−4445)2+(−1190)2+(1118)2=119082+12+52=111030, and this is the distance between the lines ABAB and CDCD.

  1. the equation (in any form) of the plane ABCABC,

We’ll aim for the Cartesian equation of the plane ABCABC in the form px+qy+rx+u=0px+qy+rx+u=0.

We do this with an eye on the last part of the question. The angle between two planes is the angle between the normals to each plane, and the normal to a plane is easily found from its Cartesian equation.

The point a=231a=(231) is on the plane, so 2p+3q+r+u=0(1)2p+3q+r+u=0(1).

The point b=11−1b=(11−1) is on the plane, so p+q−r+u=0(2)p+q−r+u=0(2).

The point c=101c=(101) is on the plane, so p+r+u=0(3)p+r+u=0(3).

We can eliminate the uu terms by subtracting pairs of equations:

(1)−(2)⟹(2)−(3)⟹p+2q+2r=0p+2q−2r=0(1)−(2)⟹p+2q+2r=0(2)−(3)⟹p+2q−2r=0

Adding these then gives p+3q=0p+3q=0, so p=−3qp=−3q, r=12qr=12q and hence u=52qu=52q.

Thus the equation of the plane ABCABC is −3qx+qy+12qz+52q=0−3qx+qy+12qz+52q=0, or −6x+2y+z+5=0−6x+2y+z+5=0 (dividing by 12q12q; we can multiply or divide a Cartesian equation of the plane by any non-zero constant we wish without changing the plane represented).

  1. the angle between the planes ABCABC and ABDABD.

Using a similar method to that above, we can find the equation of the plane ABDABD.

Once again, we’ll aim for the Cartesian equation of the plane in the form px+qy+rx+u=0px+qy+rx+u=0.

The point a=231a=(231) is on the plane, so 2p+3q+r+u=0(1)2p+3q+r+u=0(1).

The point b=11−1b=(11−1) is on the plane, so p+q−r+u=0(2)p+q−r+u=0(2).

The point d=030d=(030) is on the plane, so 3q+u=0(3)3q+u=0(3).

Again, we can eliminate uu by subtracting pairs of equations (we can always do this with this type of system of equations):

(1)−(2)⟹(1)−(3)⟹2p+2q+2r=02p+2q+2r=0(1)−(2)⟹2p+2q+2r=0(1)−(3)⟹2p+2q+2r=0

Thus r=−2pr=−2p, q=32pq=32p and hence u=−92pu=−92p.

Thus the equation of the plane ABDABD is px+32py−2pz−92p=0px+32py−2pz−92p=0 or 2x+3y−4z−9=02x+3y−4z−9=0.

Hence a normal to the plane ABCABC is −621(−621), while a normal to the plane ABDABD is 23−4(23−4).

To find the angle between these two vectors, we use the scalar product. We have −621⋅23−4=−12+6−4=−10(−621)⋅(23−4)=−12+6−4=−10.

Thus cosθ=−1041⎯⎯⎯⎯√29⎯⎯⎯⎯√=−0.2900…cos⁡θ=−104129=−0.2900…, so the angle between the normals is θ=106.9∘θ=106.9∘ (to 1 d.p.). The angle between the planes is equal to the angle between the normals, so the (obtuse) angle between the planes is 106.9∘106.9∘. If we wish to give the acute angle between the planes instead, this is then 180∘−106.9∘=73.1∘180∘−106.9∘=73.1∘ (to 1 d.p.).

If we draw a sketch of the situation, we may see another possible way of thinking about the angle between these two planes. Though this is less straightforward than the above approach, especially after having done part (iii), it is perhaps more informative. It is also interesting in that it does not require us to work out the equations of the planes.

The two planes ABCABC and ABDABD meet along the line ABAB. We have also drawn the unit vector p̂ p^ (in the direction of BA−→BA→), which is p/|p|=13p=13122p/|p|=13p=13(122).

  1. the equation (in any form) of the plane ABCABC,

The points AA, BB and CC lie in this plane, so the vectors AB−→AB→ and AC−→−AC→ lie within the plane. Hence a normal nn to the plane can be found by computing AB−→×AC−→−AB→×AC→:

n=AB−→×AC−→−=(b−a)×(c−a)=−1−2−2×−1−30=−621.n=AB→×AC→=(b−a)×(c−a)=(−1−2−2)×(−1−30)=(−621).

The equation of the plane is then r⋅n=cr⋅n=c for some constant cc; by substituting in r=ar=a, for example, we can deduce c=a⋅n=−5c=a⋅n=−5, and hence the plane ABCABC has equation r⋅n=−5r⋅n=−5.

  1. the angle between the planes ABCABC and ABDABD.

We can likewise find a normal n′n′ to the plane ABDABD:

n′=AB−→×AD−→−=(b−a)×(d−a)=−1−2−2×−20−1=23−4.n′=AB→×AD→=(b−a)×(d−a)=(−1−2−2)×(−20−1)=(23−4).

Then the angle between the planes equals the angle between the normals nn and n′n′, which we can work out as in the first approach.
