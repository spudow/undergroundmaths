Combining Vectors
Review question

Where are PP and QQ if PQPQ is perpendicular to l1l1 and l2l2?

Solution

The equations of the lines l1l1 and l2l2 are r=a+λbr=a+λb and r=c+μdr=c+μd respectively, where

a=−6i+3j+15k,b=i−2j+3k,c=6i+15j+39k,d=2i−3j+4k,a=−6i+3j+15k,b=i−2j+3k,c=6i+15j+39k,d=2i−3j+4k,
and where λλ and μμ are scalar parameters. The points PP and QQ are on l1l1 and l2l2 respectively, and PQPQ is perpendicular to both l1l1 and l2l2. In any order:

  1. find the position vectors of PP and QQ.

The question tells us we are free to answer the parts of the question in any order that we like. Here we will start with the last part, by finding the vectors OP−→−OP→ and OQ−→−OQ→.

The points PP and QQ can be represented in vector form as pi+qj+rkpi+qj+rk and si+tj+uksi+tj+uk.

If now PP and QQ lie on l1l1 and l2l2 respectively, they have to satisfy the equation of each line. We can hence deduce the six equations

−6+λ3−2λ15+3λ6+2μ15−3μ39+4μ=p,=q,=r,=s,=t,=u.−6+λ=p,3−2λ=q,15+3λ=r,6+2μ=s,15−3μ=t,39+4μ=u.

But we also know that the vector corresponding to PQ−→−=(p−s)i+(q−t)j+(r−u)kPQ→=(p−s)i+(q−t)j+(r−u)k has to be perpendicular to both lines, and hence to the two direction vectors bb and dd. Two vectors are perpendicular if and only if their scalar product vanishes. Hence we have the two additional equations

(p−s)−2(q−t)+3(r−u)2(p−s)−3(q−t)+4(r−u)=0,=0.(p−s)−2(q−t)+3(r−u)=0,2(p−s)−3(q−t)+4(r−u)=0.

This gives us 8 equations in total for 8 unknowns. Combining the first six equations with the last two leaves us with

20λ7λ=84+29μ,=30+10μ,20λ=84+29μ,7λ=30+10μ,

which can be solved to give λ=10λ=10 and μ=4μ=4. Substituting these values back into the equations for l1l1 and l2l2 yields the position vectors for our points:

OP−→−OQ−→−=4i−17j+45k,=14i+3j+55k.OP→=4i−17j+45k,OQ→=14i+3j+55k.
  1. show that PQ=106⎯⎯√PQ=106;

The length of a vector xi+yj+zkxi+yj+zk is given by x2+y2+z2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√x2+y2+z2. Hence all we have to do is to calculate the difference of the position vectors PP and QQ and calculate the corresponding length, giving

  1. find a vector which is parallel to PQ−→−PQ→, giving your answer in the form xi+yj+zkxi+yj+zk;

We already know the vector PQ−→−=10i+20j+10kPQ→=10i+20j+10k. Multiplying this by any constant gives a vector parallel to PQ−→−PQ→. For example, we can multiply by −110−110 to get i+2j+ki+2j+k.

Tackling part (iii) before part (i) may seem strange here. A different order might have made better sense if we had used a different method. For instance, we could use the vector cross product to find a vector perpendicular to the two lines in which case it would be easiest to answer (i) first.
