Counting & Binomials
Review question

Can we find a sequence of solutions to the equation x2−2y2=1?x2−2y2=1?

Solution

  1. Find a pair of positive integers, x1x1 and y1y1, that solve the equation
    (x1)2−2(y1)2=1.(x1)2−2(y1)2=1.

By inspection or by trying a few small values, we can find the solution x1=3x1=3, y1=2y1=2.

  1. Given integers aa, bb, we define two sequences x1x1, x2x2, x3x3, …… and y1y1, y2y2, y3y3, …… by setting
    xn+1=3xn+4yn,yn+1=axn+byn,for n≥1.xn+1=3xn+4yn,yn+1=axn+byn,for n≥1.

Find positive values for aa, bb such that

(xn+1)2−2(yn+1)2=(xn)2−2(yn)2.(xn+1)2−2(yn+1)2=(xn)2−2(yn)2.

Substituting the expressions for xnxn and ynyn into this equation, we get

(3xn+4yn)2−2(axn+byn)2=(xn)2−2(yn)2(3xn+4yn)2−2(axn+byn)2=(xn)2−2(yn)2
that is,
(8−2a2)(xn)2+(24−4ab)xnyn+(18−2b2)(yn)2=0.(8−2a2)(xn)2+(24−4ab)xnyn+(18−2b2)(yn)2=0.

This equation will certainly hold if all three coefficients are zero. In this case, we have

2a2=8,4ab=24,2b2=18.2a2=8,4ab=24,2b2=18.

These equations DO solve, to give a=±2,b=±3a=±2,b=±3. Since we also require that aa and bb are positive, we have a=2a=2 and b=3b=3.

  1. Find a pair of integers XX, YY which satisfy X2−2Y2=1X2−2Y2=1 such that X>Y>50X>Y>50.

We start with our previously found small solution, x1=3x1=3 and y1=2y1=2.

Now we can use the sequences defined above with a=2,b=3a=2,b=3 to generate new solutions. We have

x1=3,y1=2;x1=3,y1=2;
x2=3×3+4×2=17,y2=2×3+3×2=12;x2=3×3+4×2=17,y2=2×3+3×2=12;
x3=3×17+4×12=99,y3=2×17+3×12=70.x3=3×17+4×12=99,y3=2×17+3×12=70.
 So X=99X=99 and Y=70Y=70 are such a pair.

  1. (Using the values of aa and bb found in the second part) what is the approximate value of xn/ynxn/yn as nn increases?

Let’s suppose that xnynxnyn tends to a limit LL as nn tends to infinity.

Then for large n,n,

L≈xn+1yn+1=3xn+4yn2xn+3ynL≈xn+1yn+1=3xn+4yn2xn+3yn
=3xnyn+42xnyn+3=3xnyn+42xnyn+3
≈3L+42L+3.≈3L+42L+3.

Thus L≈3L+42L+3L≈3L+42L+3, which becomes equality when we allow nn to tend to infinity. Thus by solving the equation, L=2⎯⎯√.L=2.

Alternatively, we could argue like this:

For all the pairs (xn,yn)(xn,yn), we have

(xn)2−2(yn)2=1.(xn)2−2(yn)2=1.

Also, because of the way xnxn and ynyn are constructed, both form increasing sequences.

So if we divide through by (yn)2(yn)2, we see that

(xnyn)2−2=1(yn)2≈0for large n,(xnyn)2−2=1(yn)2≈0for large n,
so xn/yn≈2⎯⎯√xn/yn≈2, since xnxn and ynyn are both positive.

This question may remind you of Staircase sequences.
