Problem

Here’s a game for two players.

Player 1: Choose a positive integer that is greater than 11. Call it nn.

Player 2: Choose two positive integers, say aa and bb, in such a way that nn divides the product abab (that is, abab is a multiple of nn).

If nn divides aa or nn divides bb, then Player 1 wins.

If nn divides neither aa nor bb, then Player 2 wins.

  1. Play this game against a partner a few times, to get a feel for the rules. You might want to swap so that you each experience being Player 1 and Player 2. Can you find a winning strategy for Player 1? Can you find a winning strategy for Player 2?

  2. Why does your strategy work? Are you sure that it works? There are some suggestions below that might help you to structure your proof, but do try to come up with your own ideas before you look below.

    You should be really careful that you use only facts about primes and integers that you can prove for yourself. Otherwise you run the risk of having a circular argument: you use some fact to prove your conjecture in this question, and then use the result in this question to prove that fact.

Tip: here’s some conventional notation that you might find useful while working on this problem.

If aa and bb are integers, then we might write a∣ba∣b (the symbol in the middle is a vertical line) to mean “aa divides bb”. More formally, that tells us that there is some integer cc such that b=acb=ac. If you’re reading a∣ba∣b out loud, then you should say “aa divides bb”.

If it is not true that aa divides bb, then we can write a∤ba∤b (that is, a vertical line with a little dash through it), and we’d read that as “aa does not divide bb”.

Be careful to get the symbols in the right order! It is true that 3∣123∣12, but it is definitely not true that 12∣312∣3 (in fact, 12∤312∤3).

On this page, we’ve written things out in words rather than using this notation, but you might like to use it in your own working.

Before we can prove something, we need to formulate a precise statement of what it is that we are trying to prove. So what statement are you trying to prove?

Here’s one statement that we might try to prove.

If pp is prime and pp divides abab, then pp divides aa or pp divides bb.

(Note that ‘or’ in maths is inclusive, so you should read the conclusion above as “pp divides aa or pp divides bb or both”.)

It might be that you have a more refined version of this.

We are trying to prove a result of the form

If XX, then YY or ZZ.

Do you have a strategy for proving such a result? How might you set about it?

Here’s one possible approach.

We are allowed to assume statement XX.

If statement YY is true, then we are done: the result is true.

So we may assume that statement YY is false, and then our job is to show that statement ZZ is true.

In our case, that means that our strategy is to assume that pp is prime, that pp divides abab, and that pp does not divide aa, and then to deduce that pp divides bb.

If you’re following the strategy suggested in the possible answer to suggestion 2 above, then you are making the extra assumption that pp does not divide aa. How can we use that information to help?

We know that pp is prime and that pp does not divide aa. That tells us that the highest common factor of aa and pp is 11 (that is, they are coprime).

The statement that two numbers have highest common factor 11 can be quite a negative statement (they don’t have any higher common factors), so can be quite difficult to work with. But fortunately we have a way to turn that into a more positive statement: we know that if the highest common factor of two numbers is 11, then we can write 11 as a linear combination of them. (You might have found this while working on the open-ended investigation Buckets and ponds.)

So we know that there are integers mm and nn such that am+pn=1am+pn=1.

We’re almost there now.

How can we involve bb? We haven’t yet used the assumption that pp divides abab. How might we use that?

We could multiply the equation am+pn=1am+pn=1 (from the possible answer to suggestion 3) by bb. That would introduce a bb somewhere, and also gives us an abab, which should be useful.

Can you finish off the argument now?
