Solution

Physics suggests that the orbital period, PP, and distance from the sun, rr, are related by a formula of the form

P=ArkP=Ark
where AA and kk are constants. By plotting a suitable graph, estimate the values of AA and kk.

A graph of PP against rr looks like this.

There is a nice smooth curve that fits the data, but unless you are using graphing software with polynomial regression it does not help us to find values for AA and kk. However, if we take the logarithm of each side of the formula and then use the laws of logarithms, we get

logP=logA+klogr.log⁡P=log⁡A+klog⁡r.
So if we plot logPlog⁡P against logrlog⁡r we should get a straight line with gradient kk and axis intercept at logP=logAlog⁡P=log⁡A.

Note that we could use logarithms to any base, but here we have chosen to use natural logarithms (base ee).

lnrln⁡r 4.0594.059 4.6844.684 5.0085.008 5.4295.429 6.6576.657 7.2687.268 7.9637.963 8.4118.411 8.6848.684

This straight line has gradient 1.51.5 and axis intercept −1.6−1.6. So we have k=1.5k=1.5 and lnA=−1.6ln⁡A=−1.6 which means that A=0.20A=0.20. We can write the formula as

P=0.2r1.5.P=0.2r1.5.

The 1.51.5 power might seem surprising, but the Physics of gravity and planetary motion does in fact predict this. It is summed up in Kepler’s Third Law which states that P2∝r3P2∝r3. Note that orbits are actually elliptical and rr is the semi-major axis which is what NASA quote in their table. Kepler also tells us that our constant AA is a function of the mass of the sun, M⊙M⊙.

A=2πGM⊙⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√,A=2πGM⊙,
where GG is the Gravitational Constant, 6.67×10−11m3kg−1s−26.67×10−11m3kg−1s−2. After sorting out the units, our value of A=0.20A=0.20 allows us to estimate the mass of the sun at M⊙=1.98×1030kgM⊙=1.98×1030kg.

Use your results to predict the orbital period of Ceres, a dwarf planet in the asteroid belt with r=414.0r=414.0.

Substituting into our formula gives

P=0.2×4141.5≈1685.P=0.2×4141.5≈1685.
So we can predict that Ceres has an orbital period of 1685days1685days. Astronomical tables give the actual value as 1682days1682days.

Temperature

The surface temperature is generally lower for planets further away from the sun. Use the data to find an exponential law (T=Ae−krT=Ae−kr) or a power law (T=ArkT=Ark) to model this.

  • You might need to use the absolute temperature, measured in Kelvin. A difference of 1K1K is the same as a difference of 1∘C1∘C but 0∘C≈273K0∘C≈273K.

The first thing to notice is that the general rule is not always true – the surface temperature of Venus seems to be exceptionally high. We’ll treat this as some kind of outlier.

The question suggests an exponential or power law, so one or other of the following.

If we suppose that

T=T0e−arT=T0e−ar
then this would mean that
lnT=lnT0−arln⁡T=ln⁡T0−ar
 so a graph of lnTln⁡T against rr would be a straight line.

If we suppose that

T=T1rpT=T1rp
then this would mean that
lnT=lnT1+plnrln⁡T=ln⁡T1+pln⁡r
 so a graph of lnTln⁡T against lnrln⁡r would be a straight line.

So we’ll work out lnTln⁡T and lnrln⁡r and plot the graphs. The hint suggests using absolute temperature, so we’ll add that to our table and evaluate the logarithm of that rather than of the Celsius temperature.

Can you see why using absolute rather than Celsius temperature is important?

The graph against lnrln⁡r is a much better approximation to a straight line. We decided that Venus was an outlier and so we have drawn a line of best fit ignoring its data point. This line has gradient close to −0.43−0.43 and intercept at 7.87.8. So in our formula

Ta=T1rpTa=T1rp
we have p=−0.43p=−0.43 and T1≈e7.8≈2400T1≈e7.8≈2400. Turning the temperature back into ∘C∘C, we can write the formula as
T=2400r0.43−273.T=2400r0.43−273.

As a check, substitute in a few values from the original table. It’s likely that none of them will match exactly, since the data points are scattered around our line of best fit.

There is a theoretical model that predicts that planetary equilibrium temperature varies as 1r⎯⎯√1r and our result is not far off from that. You could add a line to your graph corresponding to this theoretical result and compare it with the data points. You might also think about what factors other than rr could affect the surface temperature and lead to the scattering of the data.

Use your results to predict the surface temperature of Ceres.

Substituting r=414.0r=414.0 into our formula gives

T=24004140.43−273≈−93.T=24004140.43−273≈−93.
So we can predict the temperature to be −93∘C−93∘C or 180K180K. Astronomical tables give the actual value as 168K168K.
