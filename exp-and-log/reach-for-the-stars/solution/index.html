Solution

Imagine plotting a graph of y=2xy=2x, with 1cm1cm to one unit on each axis.

How far along the xx-axis could you go before the graph reached the top of a sheet of paper?

If you extended the graph so the positive xx-axis filled the whole width of a sheet of paper, how tall would the paper have to be?

We could start by drawing up a table of values.

Next we need to decide how big a sheet of paper is. If you are in Europe, you are probably working on A4 paper, which is just under 30cm30cm on its longest side. From the table of values we can see that the graph extending to x=5x=5 would not quite fit on the page. So the answer is somewhere between 44 and 55.

For a more accurate answer, we need to find xx such that 2x302x30.

We could find this by trial and improvement. Alternatively, notice that what we are looking for is log230log230 (the power to which we need to raise 22 in order to get 3030) which you could evaluate using a calculator. By either method we find that xx is roughly 4.94.9.

If instead we were using American Letter size paper, the sheet would be 11in.11in. tall (roughly 279mm279mm). How different would the answer be?

A sheet of A2 paper is much bigger - it is twice as tall as A4. How much further along the xx-axis would that allow us to go? Can you explain your answer?

In order to use xx values all the way across a sheet of A4 (21cm21cm), the height of the paper would have to be 221cm20971m21km221cm20971m21km!

So, 21cm21cm across requires the paper to be 21km21km tall. What if the paper were 26cm26cm across? Can you explain why this works?

How far along the xx-axis would you have to go so that the graph was tall enough to reach

  • to the top of The Shard in London?
  • to the moon?
  • to the Andromeda galaxy?

Try to estimate the answers before calculating them and mark them at the appropriate points along a sketch of the xx-axis.

Work out where they should be and then add some other results such as the distances to the sun and other stars. What do you notice?

We can use the same techniques as above for these problems, but will have to take care with some unit conversions. We have worked through a couple of examples below. You could choose others from the Data sheet or your own research.

The Shard is roughly 309.6m309.6m tall, or 30960cm30960cm. We could use trial and improvement from the table of values, our answer must be greater than 77 and from our 21km21km piece of paper, it must be less than 2121. So we could start with a value between 77 and 2121.
