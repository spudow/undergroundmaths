Solution 1

Two fixed points AA and BB lie in the plane, and the distance between them is AB=2aAB=2a, where a>0a>0.

A point PP moves in the plane so that the ratio of its distances from AA and BB is constant:

PAPB=λ,PAPB=λ,
where λ>0λ>0.

  1. Can you sketch the locus of the point PP for different values of λλ?

    Here are some values of λλ you might consider:

    • λ=1λ=1

    • λ=3λ=3

    • λ=13λ=13

    This GeoGebra applet shows the locus of PP. The points AA and BB are movable, and the value of λλ can be adjusted using the slider.

    You can use this applet to check your sketches for the cases λ=1λ=1, λ=3λ=3 and λ=13λ=13.

    Download GeoGebra file

    You may have noticed that the loci for the cases λ=3λ=3 and λ=13λ=13 look essentially the same as each other, just reflected. Why should this be the case?

    If we write out the equations for these two loci, we see:

    PAPB=3andPAPB=13.PAPB=3andPAPB=13.
    If we now take the reciprocal of the second equation, these become
    PAPB=3andPBPA=3.PAPB=3andPBPA=3.
     The equations are identical, except that AA and BB have swapped roles. So the loci are identical except for swapping AA and BB, which can be achieved by reflecting in the perpendicular bisector of ABAB.

    We’d like to show that the locus really is a circle, which brings us to question 2.

  2. Using Cartesian coordinates, work out (the equation of) the locus of PP.

    You may find it more straightforward to first work with specific values of aa and λλ, say a=2a=2 and λ=3λ=3.

    Suggestion: The question has not specified the coordinates of the points AA or BB, so what options do we have?

    In particular, what would be “nice” coordinates to choose for the points AA and BB?

    As we are going to be using coordinates in this problem, and are looking for the locus of PP, we will let PP have coordinates (x,y)(x,y).

    We could let AA be at (q,r)(q,r) and BB be at (s,t)(s,t), for example. However, this is likely to make our algebra very messy, having so many letters around.

    A better approach is to choose “nice” coordinates for AA and BB. Let’s take AA to be at the origin, and BB to be on the xx-axis. This way, A=(0,0)A=(0,0) and B=(2a,0)B=(2a,0), and most of the coordinates involved are zero.

    Another good choice would be A=(−a,0)A=(−a,0) and B=(a,0)B=(a,0), so that the points are symmetrically placed about the origin. We will go with the first option in this solution, but you might like to have a go with this alternative choice and compare them.

    We let PP have the coordinates (x,y)(x,y), and then apply Pythagoras to find

    PA=x2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√ and PB=(x−2a)2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√,PA=x2+y2 and PB=(x−2a)2+y2,
    as seen in this sketch:

    Using these, we then see that
    PAPB=x2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√(x−2a)2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=λ.(1)(1)PAPB=x2+y2(x−2a)2+y2=λ.

    We will give the rest of the solution first for the specific example suggested, a=2a=2 and λ=3λ=3, and then for the general case.

    We rewrite (1)(1) with a=2a=2 and λ=3λ=3, giving
    x2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√(x−4)2+y2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=3.(2)(2)x2+y2(x−4)2+y2=3.
     If we now square this to get rid of the square roots, we get
    x2+y2(x−4)2+y2=9.x2+y2(x−4)2+y2=9.
     We can multiply through to lose the fractions, obtaining:
    x2+y2=9((x−4)2+y2).x2+y2=9((x−4)2+y2).
     It now makes sense to expand the inner brackets, giving
    x2+y2=9(x2−8x+16+y2).x2+y2=9(x2−8x+16+y2).
     We can then rearrange this to collect all of the terms on one side, giving
    8x2+8y2−72x+144=0,8x2+8y2−72x+144=0,
     which can be divided by 88 to give
    x2+y2−9x+18=0.(3)(3)x2+y2−9x+18=0.

    This is the equation of a circle, so we’ve achieved what we set out to do after question 1!

    We also observe that every step of this argument can be reversed, so if the point (x,y)(x,y) lies on the circle, which means that it satisfies (3)(3), it will also satisfy (2)(2). (Note too that (4,0)(4,0) does not satisfy (3)(3), so there is no problem of dividing by zero in (2)(2) when working backwards.)

    To find the centre and radius of the circle, we complete the square to get

    (x−92)2−(92)2+y2+18=0,(x−92)2−(92)2+y2+18=0,
    so
    (x−92)2+y2=94.(x−92)2+y2=94.

    Therefore the locus is a circle with centre at (92,0)(92,0) and radius 3232.

    To do the general case, we do exactly the same as in the specific example, we simply use aa in place of 22 and λλ in place of 33.

    So we again begin with (1)(1), square it and multiply through to get
    x2+y2=λ2((x−2a)2+y2).x2+y2=λ2((x−2a)2+y2).
     We then expand and rearrange to find
    (λ2−1)(x2+y2)−4λ2ax+4λ2a2=0.(4)(4)(λ2−1)(x2+y2)−4λ2ax+4λ2a2=0.

    If λ2−1≠0λ2−1≠0, that is, λ≠1λ≠1 (as λ>0λ>0), we can divide by λ2−1λ2−1 to get

    x2+y2−4λ2axλ2−1+4λ2a2λ2−1=0.x2+y2−4λ2axλ2−1+4λ2a2λ2−1=0.

    This is the equation of a circle. As in the specific example above, the argument can be reversed, and so every point on the circle satisfies the original condition.

    To find the centre and radius of the circle, we now complete the square to get
    (x−2λ2aλ2−1)2+y2=4λ4a2(λ2−1)2−4λ2a2λ2−1=4λ2a2(λ2−1)2.(x−2λ2aλ2−1)2+y2=4λ4a2(λ2−1)2−4λ2a2λ2−1=4λ2a2(λ2−1)2.

    Thus the locus is a circle with centre (2λ2aλ2−1,0)(2λ2aλ2−1,0) and radius 2λa|λ2−1|2λa|λ2−1|.

    Do check that this gives the same answer as we had above in the case a=2a=2 and λ=3λ=3!

    We need the absolute value in the formula for the radius to handle the case 0<λ<10<λ<1: try substituting in a=2a=2 and λ=13λ=13 to see why.

    If λ>1λ>1, then BB lies within the circle, whereas if λ<1λ<1, then AA lies within the circle, as shown in the applet above. (You might like to try showing this is the case from the algebra.)

    The only remaining case is when λ=1λ=1, where we know that the answer is a straight line. We can obtain this from the algebra: when λ=1λ=1, equation (4)(4) becomes

    −4ax+4a2=0.−4ax+4a2=0.
    Dividing by 4a4a (which is not zero) gives −x+a=0−x+a=0 or equivalently x=ax=a: this is the perpendicular bisector of ABAB.

    This makes sense, because the case λ=1λ=1 gives PA=PBPA=PB, and the locus of all points equidistant from AA and BB is their perpendicular bisector.

    You might like to consider what happens as λλ tends to zero or infinity, or when λλ tends to 11. How does the algebra relate to what you have seen in the applet?

    If you did try this question with coordinates such as (q,r)(q,r) for AA and (s,t)(s,t) for BB, you will have found that the algebra was very messy indeed.

    It is always worth asking oneself what options one has when choosing coordinates: a good choice can have huge advantages.

    In this situation, we were free to choose both the origin and the orientation of the axes, so we were able to choose useful ones.

The locus of PP is known as the circle of Apollonius. See the Historical background for this problem to read more about Apollonius and this circle.

A further question to consider: Can we go backwards?

In other words, if we begin with a specified circle, is it always possible to find two points AA and BB and a value λ>0λ>0 so that the given circle is the locus of the point PP satisfying PA/PB=λPA/PB=λ?

If it is possible, is there any choice in how we can choose AA, BB and λλ?
