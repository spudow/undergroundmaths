Cross product

Printable version

The cross product (or vector product) of the two three-dimensional real vectors aa and bb, written a×ba×b or a∧ba∧b, is another three-dimensional vector. It is defined as follows:

a1a2a3×b1b2b3=a2b3−a3b2a3b1−a1b3a1b2−a2b1.(a1a2a3)×(b1b2b3)=(a2b3−a3b2a3b1−a1b3a1b2−a2b1).
It can also be calculated by expanding the “determinant”
||||||ia1b1ja2b2ka3b3||||||.|ijka1a2a3b1b2b3|.

This is a vector (hence the name ‘vector’ product). The definition does not easily extend to vectors aa and bb with nn components for n≠3n≠3. (Compare this to the dot product, which outputs a scalar and which does have a simple generalisation.)

The cross product combines information about the lengths of aa and bb and their directions. Specifically,

a×b=|a||b|sinθn̂ a×b=|a||b|sin⁡θn^
where |a||a| and |b||b| are the lengths (magnitudes) of aa and bb, θθ is the angle between the two vectors (with 0≤θ≤π0≤θ≤π), and n̂ n^ is a unit vector perpendicular to both aa and bb. The vector n̂ n^ is chosen so that aa, bb and n̂ n^ satisfy the right-hand rule: if you use your right hand, point your first (index) finger in the direction of aa and your second (middle) finger in the direction of bb, then your thumb, when lifted, will point in the direction of n̂ n^ and hence in the direction of a×ba×b.

It follows that for non-zero vectors aa and bb, the cross product is zero exactly when aa and bb are parallel to one another.

Any two vectors aa and bb define a parallelogram and a triangle. The area of the parallelogram is given by |a×b||a×b|, and the area of the triangle is half of this.
