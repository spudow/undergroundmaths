Weighted mean

Printable version

A weighted mean or weighted average of a set of data (or numbers) is a mean in which the different pieces of data are given different weights. “Weight” here has the sense of “importance”, but can also be thought of as literal “weight”.

The simplest case is where each piece of data xixi occurs with a corresponding frequency fifi, then we can think of the data as

x1,…,x1,x2,…,x2,…,xn,…,xnx1,…,x1,x2,…,x2,…,xn,…,xn
with f1f1 copies of x1x1, and so on. Thus the arithmetic mean of these would be
x¯=x1+⋯+x1+x2+⋯+x2+⋯+xn+⋯+xnf1+f2+⋯+fn=∑fixi∑fi.x¯=x1+⋯+x1+x2+⋯+x2+⋯+xn+⋯+xnf1+f2+⋯+fn=∑fixi∑fi.

More generally, if we weight the data xixi with weight wiwi, then the weighted arithmetic mean is ∑wixi∑wi∑wixi∑wi.

This is the same formula as that for the centre of mass of a set of objects (where weight wiwi is replaced by mass mimi): x¯=∑mixi∑mix¯=∑mixi∑mi, that is, the xx-coordinate of the centre of mass is the mean of the xx-coordinates of the objects, where the objects are weighted by their mass.

It is also the same formula as that for the expectation of a discrete random variable XX. If XX can take the values x1x1, …, xnxn with probabilities p1p1, …, pnpn respectively, where p1+p2+⋯+pn=1p1+p2+⋯+pn=1, then the expectation (mean) of XX is given by E(X)=p1x1+p2x2+⋯+pnxn=∑pixiE(X)=p1x1+p2x2+⋯+pnxn=∑pixi. (We don’t need to explicitly divide by p1+p2+⋯+pnp1+p2+⋯+pn as this equals 11.)

Similarly, the weighted geometric mean of a set of data would be

(xw11xw22…xwnn)1/(w1+w2+⋯+wn).(x1w1x2w2…xnwn)1/(w1+w2+⋯+wn).

Weighted means are used when calculating inflation: in the UK, the Retail Price Index (RPI) and Consumer Price Index (CPI) are calculated by first finding a weighted mean cost of a set of items in a typical shopping basket, weighted by the quantity in which they might typically be purchased. More can be found on these at the Office for National Statistics.
