The parabola can be rolled along the xx-axis by moving the right slider.

As we roll the parabola in this way, what is the (cartesian equation of the) locus of the focus FF?

Can you find the coordinates of a general point PP on the parabola?

What do you think would be the most useful form to give the coordinates in?

An obvious answer is that the coordinates of PP are just (x,x24a)(x,x24a), using the equation of the parabola.

However, experience with conics (ellipses, parabolae and hyperbolae) suggests that things will be easier if we use parametric coordinates.

For the parabola y2=4axy2=4ax, the standard parametrisation is (at2,2at)(at2,2at). Our parabola, however, has xx and yy switched, so that its equation is x2=4ayx2=4ay; therefore the parametric coordinates of PP are (2at,at2)(2at,at2).

Instead of the parabola rolling, how else could you think about the scenario?

The parabola rolling on the xx-axis means that the xx-axis is always tangent to the rolled parabola.

So an alternative way to think about this is to draw a tangent to the parabola and work out distances in relation to this tangent, as we have done in this image:

The tangent is drawn from PP, and a perpendicular is dropped from FF to the tangent, meeting the tangent at AA. Then if we know that the point PP ends up at P=(x,0)P=(x,0) on the xx-axis, the xx-coordinate of the focus will be xPAxPA and the yy-coordinate will be AFAF.

What is the equation of the tangent through the point PP?

(If you found different forms of the point PP in mini-question 1 which is most useful?)

We differentiate the equation y=x24ay=x24a to get dydx=x2adydx=x2a.

Then at the point P(2at,at2)P(2at,at2), we have dydx=2at2a=tdydx=2at2a=t.

We can now write down the equation of the tangent: it is

yat2=t(x2at)yat2=t(x2at)
which we can rearrange to
y=txat2.y=txat2.

As we explained above, we need to find the lengths PAPA and AFAF to calculate the new coordinates of the focus. There are several different ways to achieve this; here is one of them which does not require us to determine whether AA lies on the xx-axis.

A simpler argument for the same result is as follows. From the equation of the tangent (see the solution to mini-question 3 above), we deduce that the tangent meets the xx-axis at BB which has coordinates (at,0)(at,0). Then FBFB has gradient aat=1taat=1t, so FBFB is perpendicular to the tangent, so BB and AA are the same point.

There are also other ways to deduce this which are more geometric in nature and use the focus-directrix property of the parabola.
