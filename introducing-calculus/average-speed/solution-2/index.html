Solution 2

We are still along the same stretch of road, with a speed limit of 50mph50mph, and the average speed cameras set at 4miles4miles apart. In this instance our first driver spends the same amount of time driving at 46mph46mph as he does at 62mph62mph. Our second driver drives the first half of the distance at 46mph46mph and the second half at 62mph62mph.

What are the average speeds of each driver? Will either of them be caught speeding?

We have two different ways to think about the journey; as a whole, and split into its two pieces.

We can write the speeds of the two pieces as

46=p12tand62=q12t,46=p12tand62=q12t,
where pp and qq are the distances travelled.

The journey as a whole can be written as

average speed=p+qt.average speed=p+qt.

We know that p+q=4p+q=4 but it turns out that substituting this into the equation is not helpful.

Therefore we can substitute in values for pp and qq in terms of tt,

 average speed =23t+31tt, average speed =23t+31tt,
which leaves us with an average speed of 54mph54mph.

What do you notice about the average speed? Could you have reasoned through this answer more easily, without doing the calculations?

Now we come to the driver who travels half the distance at each speed. Again we can think about the parts of the journey and the journey as a whole.

The separate parts can be written

46=2t1and62=2t2.46=2t1and62=2t2.

The whole journey gives us

average speed=4246+262=52.8mph (3 sf).average speed=4246+262=52.8mph (3 sf).

What happens if the speed cameras are 10miles10miles apart instead? What changes?

Did you find that anything changed as the distance increased? Does this surprise you? Can you prove your findings for any distance?
