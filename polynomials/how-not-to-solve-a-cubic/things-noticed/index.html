Things you might have noticed

  • Can you find a cubic equation ax3+bx+c=0ax3+bx+c=0 where (at least) one of the roots of the corresponding quadratic ax2+bx+c=0ax2+bx+c=0 is also a root of the cubic?

Before diving into this question we might begin by taking a step back to think about what it is asking us to find.

  • Can we represent the problem graphically?

  • Do we expect there to be a unique solution?

We are essentially looking for a quadratic and cubic curve that both pass through a given point (or pair of points) on the xx-axis. It feels as though there may be lots of pairs of curves like this, and indeed we may have already found an example of a cubic and quadratic curve that each pass through (0,3)(0,3) in the Warm-up problem.

But are both equations in the form ax3+bx+c=0ax3+bx+c=0 and ax2+bx+c=0ax2+bx+c=0 as required?

This constraint narrows down the problem. We might need to consider algebraic representations of the problem to help us make progress.

When we reflected on our approach, we realised that there is an easier way to think about the problem.

Where do the cubic and quadratic graphs intersect?

The original question asks when are ax3+bx+c=0ax3+bx+c=0 and ax2+bx+c=0ax2+bx+c=0 both true. This means in particular that we require the cubic y=ax3+bx+cy=ax3+bx+c and quadratic y=ax2+bx+cy=ax2+bx+c to intersect, so we need ax3+bx+c=ax2+bx+cax3+bx+c=ax2+bx+c.

If we now subtract bx+cbx+c from this, we get ax3=ax2ax3=ax2. Therefore the cubic and quadratic always intersect at x=0x=0 and x=1x=1!

We can now finish the problem off by determining whether ax2+bx+c=0ax2+bx+c=0 at either of these points. There will be a (shared) root at x=0x=0 if and only if c=0c=0, and a (shared) root at x=1x=1 if and only if a+b+c=0a+b+c=0. And we are done!
