Review question

Can we sketch the reciprocal of a polynomial?

Solution

Express x3−3x−2x3−3x−2 as the product of three linear factors.

By the factor theorem, a polynomial f(x)f(x) has a linear factor (x−a)(x−a) if and only if f(a)=0f(a)=0.

If we let f(x)=x3−3x−2f(x)=x3−3x−2, we have

So (x−2)(x−2) is a factor of f(x)=x3−3x−2f(x)=x3−3x−2, and f(x)=(x−2)(x2+ax+1)f(x)=(x−2)(x2+ax+1).

Since f(x)f(x) has no x2x2 term, a=2a=2, and f(x)f(x) can be factorised completely as

f(x)=(x−2)(x+1)2,f(x)=(x−2)(x+1)2,
which is the product of three linear factors.

Show that x3−3x−2x3−3x−2 may also be expressed in the form (x−1)2(x+p)+q(x−1)2(x+p)+q, where pp and qq are constants to be found.

Hence, or otherwise, show that the curve whose equation is y=x3−3x−2y=x3−3x−2 has a minimum point where x=1x=1.

We know f(x)=(x−1)2(x+2)−4f(x)=(x−1)2(x+2)−4. When x=1x=1, f(x)=−4f(x)=−4.

Or else we can differentiate. If y=f(x)=x3−3x−2y=f(x)=x3−3x−2, then y′=3x2−3=0y′=3x2−3=0 when x=1x=1.

Sketch the curve whose equation is
y=1x3−3x−2,y=1x3−3x−2,

indicating clearly all its asymptotes.

We know
y=1x3−3x−2=1(x−2)(x+1)2=1(x−1)2(x+2)−4.y=1x3−3x−2=1(x−2)(x+1)2=1(x−1)2(x+2)−4.

In sketching the graph, we will ask four questions:

  1. what are the intercepts?

When x=0x=0, y=−12y=−12. This is the yy-intercept of the curve. There are no xx-intercepts, since the numerator of yy can never equal zero.

  1. what is the asymptotic behaviour?

There are two vertical asymptotes (corresponding to the roots of the denominator) at x=−1x=−1 and x=2x=2.

Since the denominator of yy tends towards ±∞±∞ as xx tends towards ±∞±∞, and as the numerator is constant, we can see y→0y→0 as x→±∞x→±∞. Thus there is one horizontal asymptote, y=0y=0.

  1. where is yy positive and negative?

The sign of yy depends wholly on the sign of (x−2)(x−2), since (x+1)2(x+1)2 is never negative, and so y>0y>0 if x>2x>2 and y<0y<0 if x<2x<2 (provided that x≠−1x≠−1).

  1. are there any turning points?

Finally, the denominator has a local minimum at x=1x=1, and so its reciprocal yy must have a local maximum at x=1x=1.

Hence obtain the number of real roots of the equation
(x+1)(x3−3x−2)=1.(x+1)(x3−3x−2)=1.
We can rewrite this equation as
x+1=1x3−3x−2.x+1=1x3−3x−2.

(Clearly x3−3x−2x3−3x−2 cannot be zero, since in this case the equation is not satisfied.) A real solution to this equation corresponds precisely to an intersection between the line y=x+1y=x+1 and the curve y=1x3−3x−2y=1x3−3x−2.

Thus we have this sketch of the line and the curve. There are two intersections, and hence there are two real roots to the equation.
