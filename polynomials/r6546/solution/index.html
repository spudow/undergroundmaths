Can we prove the Arithmetic and Geometric Mean Inequality?

Solution

  1. Prove that, if aa and bb are real,
    12(a2+b2)≥ab.12(a2+b2)≥ab.

The inequality holds if and only if a2+b2≥2aba2+b2≥2ab, which is true if and only if a2−2ab+b2≥0.a2−2ab+b2≥0.

This is true, since a2−2ab+b2=(a−b)2,a2−2ab+b2=(a−b)2, and any square is either zero or positive. Hence

12(a2+b2)≥ab.12(a2+b2)≥ab.

This is called the arithmetic mean-geometric mean inequality, or the AM-GM inequality, for two variables.

The quantity 12(x+y)12(x+y) is the arithmetic mean of xx and yy, and (if xx and yy are positive) xy⎯⎯⎯⎯√xy is the geometric mean of xx and yy.

So we have established that the arithmetic mean is always greater than or equal to the geometric mean.

This inequality is extremely useful for solving many mathematics problems.
