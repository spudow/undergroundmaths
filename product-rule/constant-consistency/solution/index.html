Some solutions

In how many different ways can you find this?

∫cos(x)sin(x)dx∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x)dx

The integrand is familiar from the identity sin(2x)≡2sin(x)cos(x)sin⁡(2x)≡2sin⁡(x)cos⁡(x) so we could use this to help us.

∫cos(x)sin(x)dx=∫12sin(2x)dx=−14cos(2x)+C∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x)dx=∫12sin⁡(2x)dx=−14cos⁡(2x)+C

If we use the substitution u=cos(x)u=cos⁡(x), then dudx=−sin(x)dudx=−sin⁡(x).

Therefore,

∫cos(x)sin(x)dx=∫−udu=−12cos2(x)+C∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x)dx=∫−udu=−12cos2⁡(x)+C

Is this the only possible substitution we could use?

We have a product of two different functions, so integration by parts may be useful.

∫uv′dx=uv−∫u′vdx∫uv′dx=uv−∫u′vdx

We let u=sin(x)u=sin⁡(x) and v′=cos(x)v′=cos⁡(x), so that u′=cosxu′=cos⁡x and v=sinxv=sin⁡x.

∫cos(x)sin(x)dx=sin2(x)−∫cos(x)sin(x)dx∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x)dx=sin2⁡(x)−∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x)dx

The integral on the right hand side is the same as our original. Has this helped?

Let’s define

∫cos(x)sin(x) dx=I.∫cos⁡(x)sin⁡(x) dx=I.

Then we have

I=sin2(x)−I+c.I=sin2⁡(x)−I+c.

Making II the subject, we have
2I⟹I=sin2(x)+c=12sin2(x)+C.2I=sin2(x)+c⟹I=12sin2⁡(x)+C.

Would the answer have been different if we had swapped the uu and v′v′?

We’ve integrated the function in three different ways and seem to have three different answers. What has happened?
