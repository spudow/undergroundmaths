Product Rule & Integration by Parts
Review question

Can we sketch the graph of y=x(x+1)(x−2)4y=x(x+1)(x−2)4?

Solution

The curve CC has equation

y=x(x+1)(x−2)4.y=x(x+1)(x−2)4.
Show that the gradient of CC is (x−2)3(6x2+x−2)(x−2)3(6x2+x−2) and find the coordinates of all the stationary points.

We can write y=(x2+x)(x−2)4y=(x2+x)(x−2)4 before differentiating using the product and chain rules. This yields
dydx=(2x+1)(x−2)4+4(x2+x)(x−2)3=(x−2)3[(2x+1)(x−2)+4x(x+1)]=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2).dydx=(2x+1)(x−2)4+4(x2+x)(x−2)3=(x−2)3[(2x+1)(x−2)+4x(x+1)]=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2).
Alternatively, we could use the Product Rule for three functions, that is, if y=uvwy=uvw, then y′=u′vw+uv′w+uvw′y′=u′vw+uv′w+uvw′.
dydx=(x+1)(x−2)4+x(x−2)4+4x(x+1)(x−2)3=(x−2)3[(x+1)(x−2)+x(x−2)+4x(x+1)]=(x−2)3[x2−x−2+x2−2x+4x2+4x]=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2).dydx=(x+1)(x−2)4+x(x−2)4+4x(x+1)(x−2)3=(x−2)3[(x+1)(x−2)+x(x−2)+4x(x+1)]=(x−2)3[x2−x−2+x2−2x+4x2+4x]=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2).

To find the xx coordinates of the stationary points we solve

dydx=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2)=0=(x−2)3(3x+2)(2x−1),dydx=(x−2)3(6x2+x−2)=0=(x−2)3(3x+2)(2x−1),
which has solutions given by
x=2 or x=−23 or x=12.x=2 or x=−23 or x=12.
 Substituting these values back in to yy we find the stationary points of CC are at
(2,0),(12,24364≈3.8),(−23,−8192729≈−11.2).(2,0),(12,24364≈3.8),(−23,−8192729≈−11.2).

Determine the nature of each stationary point and sketch CC.

As this function is a degree six polynomial (sextic) with positive coefficient of x6x6 we know that for xx large and negative it is decreasing, and for xx large and positive it is increasing. Between these values the curve undulates so the first and last stationary points must be minima and the middle one a maximum.

Alternatively we could calculate the gradient at the integer values x=−1,0,1,x=−1,0,1, and 33 to verify the nature of the points.

We also know where all the xx intercepts are, and have calculated the stationary points: joining the dots gives us a sketch of our curve.

To determine the nature of the stationary point, we might be tempted to differentiate again and find the sign of this at the points where dydx=0dydx=0:

d2ydx2=3(x−2)2(6x2+x−2)+(x−2)3(12x+1).d2ydx2=3(x−2)2(6x2+x−2)+(x−2)3(12x+1).

If we choose to do this we should notice that if dydx=0dydx=0, then the first term here is bound to be zero, which reduces the workload, but is still more effort than necessary.

We might also think that at x=2x=2 there is a point of inflection because d2ydx2=0d2ydx2=0 here. We can see from the expression for yy that the curve has a quadruple root at x=2x=2, so we expect the curve to touch the xx axis here. (Note that a triple root would indicate a point of inflection.)

In separate diagrams draw sketches of the curves whose equations are:

  1. y2=x(x+1)(x−2)4y2=x(x+1)(x−2)4;

  2. y=x2(x2+1)(x2−2)4y=x2(x2+1)(x2−2)4.

In each case, you should pay particular attention to the points where the curve meets the xx axis.

The xx-intercepts are the same, and the general shape will be similar to our first graph.
