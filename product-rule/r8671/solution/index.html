Review question

Which is the greater of eπeπ and πeπe?

Solution

Show that for x>0x>0, x1/xx1/x has its greatest value when x=ex=e.

Let’s write y=x1/xy=x1/x, and take the natural logarithm of both sides, giving
lny=1xlnx.ln⁡y=1xln⁡x.
 Differentiating this with respect to xx using the product and chain rules gives
1ydydx=−1x2lnx+1x2=1−lnxx21ydydx=−1x2ln⁡x+1x2=1−ln⁡xx2

so

dydx=y(1−lnx)x2=x1/x(1−lnx)x2.dydx=y(1−ln⁡x)x2=x1/x(1−ln⁡x)x2.
Turning points occur when dydx=0dydx=0, which is precisely when
1−lnx=0,1−ln⁡x=0,
 as x1/xx1/x is never zero for x>0x>0. So lnx=1ln⁡x=1, and the only stationary point is at x=ex=e.

Alternatively we can say, let x1/x=ef(x).x1/x=ef(x). Taking natural logs shows that f(x)=lnxx2f(x)=ln⁡xx2.

We can now differentiate y=ef(x)y=ef(x) using the chain and quotient rules.

To determine the nature of the stationary point, we’ll look at the value of yy or dydxdydx either side of x=ex=e.

We have dydx=x1/x(1−lnx)x2,dydx=x1/x(1−ln⁡x)x2, and since x1/xx1/x and x2x2 are always positive for x>0x>0, the sign of the derivative is determined by the sign of 1−lnx1−ln⁡x.

So when x<ex<e, dydx>0dydx>0 and the function is increasing; when x>ex>e, dydx<0dydx<0 and the function is decreasing.

Therefore the function has a maximum at x=ex=e, and this is the global maximum of the function.

Hence, or otherwise, but without use of a calculator, determine which is the greater of eπeπ and πeπe.

From our argument above, we must have π1/π<e1/eπ1/π<e1/e.

Raising both sides to the power eπeπ gives πe<eππe<eπ (this step is valid, since the function f(x)=xeπf(x)=xeπ is strictly increasing for x>0x>0).

So eπeπ is larger.
