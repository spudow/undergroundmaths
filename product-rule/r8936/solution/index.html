Product Rule & Integration by Parts
Review question

When will the arclength QRQR take a maximum value?

Solution

PP is a point on the circumference of a circle XX of diameter 3in.3in. . An arc of another circle YY is drawn with centre PP, intersecting XX at QQ and RR. Express the lengths of PQPQ and the minor arc QRQR of YY in terms of θθ, where θθ is the angle, in radians, between PQPQ and the diameter of XX through PP.

Here’s a diagram of the two circles XX and YY.

If you drew your circle YY larger than your circle XX, your diagram will look quite different to this: there’s no need to worry, the mathematics works just the same.

Drag the point RR to change the size of YY.

We’ll calculate the two lengths in turn. Firstly, to find PQPQ, let’s look at the circle XX.

Since the angle PQP′PQP′ lies in a semicircle, it must be a right angle. Now, from trigonometry,

cosθ=adjacenthypotenuse=length of PQlength of PP′⟹length of PQ=3cosθ.cos⁡θ=adjacenthypotenuse=length of PQlength of PP′⟹length of PQ=3cos⁡θ.

Secondly, to find the length of the minor arc QRQR, let’s look at the circle YY.

The length of an arc of a circle is rϕrϕ, where ϕϕ is the angle subtended at the centre of the circle by the arc.

By symmetry, ∠QPR=θ+θ=2θ∠QPR=θ+θ=2θ, and so the length of the minor arc QRQR is PQ×2θ=6θcosθ.PQ×2θ=6θcos⁡θ.

Prove that the length of the arc QRQR has a stationary value when θ=cotθθ=cot⁡θ, and that this value is a maximum.

We know that

QR=6θcosθ.QR=6θcos⁡θ.
Using the product rule, we have
d(QR)dθ=6θ(−sinθ)+6cosθ=6(cosθ−θsinθ).d(QR)dθ=6θ(−sin⁡θ)+6cos⁡θ=6(cos⁡θ−θsin⁡θ).

So the stationary points are those values of θθ for which

cosθ=θsinθ,cos⁡θ=θsin⁡θ,
which is true if and only if
θ=cotθ.θ=cot⁡θ.

We can divide through here by sinθsin⁡θ, since θ=0θ=0 is not going to be a solution to our equation.

We are only concerned with the interval 0≤θ≤π20≤θ≤π2.

Since θθ increases from zero and cotθcot⁡θ descends from infinity, as illustrated below, our equation has just one solution.

Is this a maximum? We know that QR>0QR>0 when 0<θ<π20<θ<π2, that QR=0QR=0 when θ=0θ=0 and π2π2, and that there’s only one stationary point between 00 and π2π2; therefore, this point has to be a maximum.

We can see this in the changing values in the GeoGebra illustration above.

If we try dragging RR, what happens to QRQR? For what value of θθ is this a maximum? Does θ=cotθθ=cot⁡θ here?

Alternatively, we could calculate the second derivative f″(θ)f″(θ) and check that it’s negative on this interval.
