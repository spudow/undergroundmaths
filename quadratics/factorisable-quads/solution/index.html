Factorisable quadratics

Solution

  1. The quadratic x2+4x+3x2+4x+3 factorises as (x+1)(x+3)(x+1)(x+3). In both the original quadratic and the factorised form, all of the coefficients are integers.

    The quadratic x2−4x+3=(x−1)(x−3)x2−4x+3=(x−1)(x−3) similarly factorises with all of the coefficients being integers.

    How many quadratics of the following forms factorise with integer coefficients? Here, bb is allowed to be any integer (positive, negative or zero). For example, in (a), bb could be −7−7, since (x−2)(x−5)=x2−7x+10(x−2)(x−5)=x2−7x+10.

    1. x2+bx+10x2+bx+10

      We attempt to factorise the quadratic by finding two integers which multiply to 1010 and add to bb. This is because if our quadratic factorises as (x+r)(x+s)(x+r)(x+s), then expanding the brackets gives

      (x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rs=x2+bx+10,(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rs=x2+bx+10,
      so we need r+s=br+s=b and rs=10rs=10.

      We therefore need to find two integers which multiply to 1010, and there are four such pairs:

      mm and nn bb
      11 and 1010 1111
      22 and 55 77
      −1−1 and −10−10 −11−11
      −2−2 and −5−5 −7−7

      Therefore there are four possible values of bb.

      (Why do we not also need to consider 1010 and 11 the other way round, and 55 and 22 the other way round, and so on?)

    2. x2+bx+30x2+bx+30

      This is very similar; this time we are looking for pairs of numbers which multiply to 3030, and there are eight of these: ±1±1 and ±30±30; ±2±2 and ±15±15; ±3±3 and ±10±10; ±5±5 and ±6±6. Thus there are eight possible values of bb.

    3. x2+bx−8x2+bx−8

      There are four pairs of numbers which multiply to −8−8: ±1±1 and ∓8∓8 (this means the pair +1+1 and −8−8 and the pair −1−1 and +8+8); ±2±2 and ∓4∓4. Thus there are four possible values of bb.

    4. x2+bx−16x2+bx−16

      Now there are five pairs of numbers which multiply to −16−16: ±1±1 and ∓16∓16; ±2±2 and ∓8∓8; 44 and −4−4. Thus there are five possible values of bb.

      Note that the final pair gives b=0b=0, and the quadratic which results is x2−16=(x+4)(x−4)x2−16=(x+4)(x−4). This is an example of the difference of two squares.

    5. 2x2+bx+62x2+bx+6

      Thinking about how we factorise this expression (see Quadratic grids for more information on this), we realise that we are looking for two numbers which multiply to 2×6=122×6=12 and add to bb.

      There are six pairs of numbers which multiply to 1212, and each pair has a different sum: ±1±1 and ±12±12; ±2±2 and ±6±6; ±3±3 and ±4±4. Therefore there are six possible values for bb.

      Another way of approaching this is to note that the quadratic will factorise as (px+r)(qx+s)(px+r)(qx+s) with pq=2pq=2 and rs=6rs=6. Therefore we could have p=1p=1 and q=2q=2, and r=±1r=±1, s=±6s=±6 or r=±2r=±2, s=±3s=±3 or r=±3r=±3, s=±2s=±2 or r=±6r=±6, s=±1s=±1. This gives 88 solutions.

      (Why do we not need to consider p=2p=2, q=1q=1? And why do we need both r=1r=1, s=6s=6 and r=6r=6, s=1s=1, for example?)

      But our other approach gave 66 solutions, so what’s going on here? And which is correct?

    6. 6x2+bx−206x2+bx−20

      This time we seek pairs of numbers which add to bb and multiply to 6×−20=−1206×−20=−120. There are lots of these: ±1±1 and ∓120∓120; ±2±2 and ∓60∓60; ±3±3 and ∓40∓40; ±4±4 and ∓30∓30; ±5±5 and ∓24∓24; ±6±6 and ∓20∓20; ±8±8 and ∓15∓15; ±10±10 and ∓12∓12, so there are 1616 pairs and so 1616 possible values for bb.

  2. This time, it is the constant which is allowed to vary: How many quadratics of the following forms factorise with integer coefficients, where cc is a positive integer.

    1. x2+6x+cx2+6x+c

      We want two integers which add to 66 and multiply to cc. Since cc has to be positive, the integers must either be both positive or both negative. But as they have to add to +6+6, they must therefore both be positive. There are therefore only 33 possible pairs: 11 and 55 (so c=5c=5); 22 and 44 (so c=8c=8); 33 and 33 (so c=9c=9).

    2. x2−10x+cx2−10x+c

      This time, the pair of integers have to multiply to cc and add to −10−10, so they must both be negative, giving the possible pairs −1−1 and −9−9; −2−2 and −8−8; −3−3 and −7−7; −4−4 and −6−6; −5−5 and −5−5, so there are 55 possibilities for cc.

    3. 3x2+5x+c3x2+5x+c

      We now need two integers which add to 55 and multiply to 3c3c, so they must both be positive. Of the pairs which add to 55 (being 11 and 44; 22 and 33), only 2×3=62×3=6 gives a multiple of 33, so there is only one possible value for cc.

    4. 10x2−6x+c10x2−6x+c

      This time, the integers add to −6−6 and multiply to 10c10c; no pairs which add to −6−6 multiply to give a positive multiple of 1010, so there are no possible values for cc.

  3. What are the answers to question 2 if cc is only allowed to be a negative integer?

    1. x2+6x+cx2+6x+c

      Now we require two integers which add to 66 and multiply to the negative integer cc. There are infinitely many such pairs: −1−1 and 77; −2−2 and 88; −3−3 and 99; …; in general −n−n and 6+n6+n for any positive integer nn. So there are infinitely many possible values for cc.

    2. x2−10x+cx2−10x+c

      The same happens here: −11−11 and 11; −12−12 and 22, and so on, so again there are infinitely many possible values for cc.

    3. 3x2+5x+c3x2+5x+c

      This time, we require two numbers which add to 55 and multiply to 3c3c, a negative multiple of 33. We could have −1−1 and 66; −4−4 and 99; −7−7 and 1212; in general, 3n3n and 5−3n5−3n (where n≥2n≥2); or we could have −3−3 and 88; −6−6 and 1111; −9−9 and 1414; in general, −3n−3n and 5+3n5+3n (where n≥1n≥1). Therefore there are infinitely many possible values for cc.

    4. 10x2−6x+c10x2−6x+c

      Here, we need two integers which add to −6−6 and multiply to a negative multiple of 1010. −10−10 and 44; −20−20 and 1414; −30−30 and 2424; … will do (in general −10n−10n and 10n−610n−6), so there are infinitely many possible values for cc. (There is also another families of solutions, namely 1010 and −16−16; 2020 and −26−26; …)

  4. Can you generalise your answers to the above questions?

    1. Generalising question 1, if cc is a fixed integer, how many quadratics of the form x2+bx+cx2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Here, bb is allowed to be any integer.

      If we write x2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rsx2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rs, we find that we need c=rsc=rs, and every different pair of numbers rr, ss gives a different value of b=r+sb=r+s. (Swapping rr and ss doesn’t give a different value of bb, though.)

      So the number of different possible values of bb is the number of pairs rr and ss where rs=crs=c and where we ignore the order of rr and ss. (Note that rr and/or ss are allowed to be negative.)

      A further challenge is to find some sort of formula for the number of such pairs; we will not pursue that here.

    2. Further generalising question 1, if aa and cc are fixed integers, with aa positive, how many quadratics of the form ax2+bx+cax2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Again, bb is allowed to be any integer.

      As before, we think about how we factorise this expression (see Quadratic grids for more information on this). We see that we are looking for two numbers which multiply to acac and add to bb.

      Each possible pair of numbers which multiplies to acac will give a different sum, and so the number of possible quadratics is the number of pairs of numbers which multiply to acac.

      As in (a), it would be nice to have some sort of formula for this number.

    3. How can we generalise questions 2 and 3?

      We can generalise question 2 in the case that a=1a=1 by asking the following:

      If bb is a fixed integer, how many quadratics of the form x2+bx+cx2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Here, cc is allowed to be any positive integer.

      Here is one approach to answering this:

      If we again write x2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rsx2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rs, we find that we need b=r+sb=r+s, and every different pair of numbers rr, ss gives a different value of c=rsc=rs. (Swapping rr and ss doesn’t give a different value of bb, though.)

      In addition, since cc has to be positive, we require rr and ss to either both be positive or both be negative.

      We’ll look at the case where bb is positive. Then our pair rr, ss can be 11, b−1b−1, or 22, b−2b−2, or …, or b−1b−1, 11. So there are b−1b−1 pairs. But the order doesn’t matter, and r=1r=1, s=b−1s=b−1 gives the same value of cc as r=b−1r=b−1, s=1s=1, so we’ve counted each answer twice. So there are (b−1)/2(b−1)/2 different pairs, and so this many possible values of cc.

      Is this correct, though? What happens if bb is odd? And what if bb is even?

      We’ll leave the case where bb is negative for you to think about.

      If we try generalising question 2 in the case that aa is not necessarily 11, we can make our life a little simpler by assuming that aa is positive.

      Why are we allowed to do this? Consider 3x2+bx+c3x2+bx+c and −3x2−bx−c−3x2−bx−c. They have almost the same factorisations, if any, since the second expression is just −1−1 times the first one, so we can write

      −3x2−bx−c=−1(3x2+bx+c).−3x2−bx−c=−1(3x2+bx+c).
      Then if we can factorise 3x2+bx+c3x2+bx+c, we get a factorisation of −3x2−bx−c−3x2−bx−c.

      So if aa is negative in a quadratic, we can just take out a factor of −1−1 and answer the resulting question, with the “cc positive” and “cc negative” questions swapping.

      So our generalised question will be:

      If aa and bb are fixed integers, with aa positive, how many quadratics of the form ax2+bx+cax2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Here, cc is allowed to be any positive integer.

      This time, as in (b), we require two numbers which add to bb and multiply to acac. Since aa and cc are positive, acac is also positive, and so the two numbers which add to bb must both be positive or both be negative. Since cc can be any positive number, acac is any multiple of aa, and so we are looking for two numbers of the same sign which add to bb and multiply to give a multiple of aa.

      There doesn’t seem to be an obvious way of finding the exact number of such pairs, but we can at least say that there can only be finitely many of them, since they still have to add to bb.

      Now let’s generalise question 3. First the case a=1a=1:

      If bb is a fixed integer, how many quadratics of the form x2+bx+cx2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Here, cc is allowed to be any negative integer.

      Again, we write x2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rsx2+bx+c=(x+r)(x+s)=x2+(r+s)x+rs, and we need b=r+sb=r+s, with every different pair of numbers rr, ss giving a different value of c=rsc=rs. (Swapping rr and ss doesn’t give a different value of bb, though.)

      This time, since cc is negative, the two numbers which add to bb must have different signs, and there are infinitely many possibilities. If bb is positive, we can take r=b+1r=b+1, s=−1s=−1, or r=b+2r=b+2, s=−2s=−2, and so on (in general, r=b+nr=b+n, s=−ns=−n, where nn is a positive integer). If bb is negative, the argument is similar: we can take r=1r=1, s=b−1s=b−1, or r=2r=2, s=b−2s=b−2, and so on (in general r=nr=n, s=b−ns=b−n, where nn is a positive integer).

      So there are infinitely many possibilities for cc.

      And finally, question 3 in the case that aa is positive:

      If aa and bb are fixed integers, with aa positive, how many quadratics of the form ax2+bx+cax2+bx+c factorise with integer coefficients? Here, cc is allowed to be any negative integer.

      As before in the generalisation of question 2, we require two numbers which add to bb and multiply to acac. But this time, since aa is positive and cc is negative, acac is negative. Since cc is arbitrary (we can choose it), acac is any negative multiple of aa.

      So we could take the two numbers to be kaka and b−kab−ka for any positive integer kk where b−ka<0b−ka<0. For all large enough kk, this will be the case. (How large does kk have to be?) The product of the two numbers is ka(b−ka)<0ka(b−ka)<0, which is clearly a negative multiple of aa, so we can take c=k(b−ka)c=k(b−ka).

      Therefore each such kk gives a possible value for cc, and so there are infinitely many possible values of cc in this case.

      We may well not have found all possible values of cc using this method. But we have shown that there are infinitely many of them. Finding a rule for all possible values of cc seems as though it will be a lot harder in general, so we will stop here.
