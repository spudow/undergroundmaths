Solution

Two points on the axis

Using GeoGebra, plot two points AA and BB on the xx-axis of a graph. Can you instruct GeoGebra to draw a quadratic which passes through both of them?

Here is a solution:

Download GeoGebra file

We want the quadratic to pass through AA and BB on the xx-axis. If we write the coordinates of these points as A=(a,0)A=(a,0) and B=(b,0)B=(b,0), we see that the roots of the quadratic must be aa and bb.

A quadratic which has these roots is y=(x−a)(x−b)y=(x−a)(x−b), so this is what we need.

To draw this in GeoGebra, we need to work out aa and bb. We can do this by typing:

a = x(A)
b = x(B)
y = (x-a)(x-b)

or all in one go as:

y = (x-x(A))(x-x(B))

Taking it further

Developing this idea further, add a point CC to the yy-axis. Can you make your quadratic pass through AA, BB and CC, like this?

Here is a solution:

Download GeoGebra file

In the first part of this problem, we found that one quadratic which passes through AA and BB has equation y=(x−a)(x−b)y=(x−a)(x−b).

We could expand this to get y=x2−(a+b)x+aby=x2−(a+b)x+ab, and we see that the coefficient of x2x2 is 11.

There was no need for us to make this choice, though: we could choose any (non-zero) coefficient for x2x2. So we could equally well have used y=k(x−a)(x−b)y=k(x−a)(x−b) as our equation, as this also passes through AA and BB on the xx-axis.

This new quadratic crosses the yy-axis where x=0x=0; substituting in x=0x=0 shows that the intercept is at (0,kab)(0,kab). As we want it to cross the yy-axis at C(0,c)C(0,c), we choose kk so that

kab=c.kab=c.
This can easily be rearranged to get k=cabk=cab.

We can therefore specify the whole quadratic to GeoGebra as

y = y(C)/(x(A)*x(B)) * (x-x(A))(x-x(B))

We are assuming here that ab≠0ab≠0. If ab=0ab=0, this means that either AA or BB lies at the origin, and it is not going to be possible for the quadratic to then pass through both AA and BB along with CC.

The one exception is if CC also lies at the origin. In this case, we have k=0/0k=0/0, which is not defined. And geometrically, in this situation, we are only specifying where the quadratic crosses the xx-axis, so there are many possibilities for the quadratic.

One point as the vertex

On a new graph, plot a point AA. Can you instruct GeoGebra to draw a quadratic which has its vertex at AA?

Here is a solution:

Download GeoGebra file

Since we are thinking about the vertex of the quadratic, it makes most sense to work with the completed square form of the quadratic:

y=(x−a)2+c.y=(x−a)2+c.
This has its vertex at (a,c)(a,c).

So if we write the coordinates of point AA as (a1,a2)(a1,a2), our quadratic could be

y=(x−a1)2+a2.y=(x−a1)2+a2.

In GeoGebra terms, we can type this in as either:

a1 = x(A)
a2 = y(A)
y=(x-a1)^2+a2

or all in one go as

y=(x-x(A))^2+y(A)

Taking it further

Developing this idea further, add a point BB to the yy-axis. Can you make your quadratic have its vertex at AA and pass through BB, like this?

Here is a solution:

Download GeoGebra file

In the first part of this problem, we found that one quadratic which passes through AA has equation y=(x−a1)2+a2y=(x−a1)2+a2.

As we noted in the “taking it further” part of question 1 above, there was no need for us to make the coefficient of x2x2 equal to 11. We could equally well have used

y=k(x−a1)2+a2y=k(x−a1)2+a2
for any choice of kk, for this also has its vertex at A(a1,a2)A(a1,a2).

This new quadratic crosses the yy-axis where x=0x=0, so at (0,ka21+a2)(0,ka12+a2). As we want it to cross the yy-axis at B(0,b2)B(0,b2) (where BB has coordinates (0,b2)(0,b2), we will choose kk so that

ka21+a2=b2.ka12+a2=b2.
This can easily be rearranged to get k=b2−a2a21k=b2−a2a12.

We can specify this to GeoGebra all in one go as

y = (y(B)-y(A))/(x(A)^2) * (x-x(A))^2 + y(A)

As in question 1, we are assuming that a1≠0a1≠0. What would happen if a1=0a1=0?

Any three points

The final (very hard) challenge: plot three points AA, BB and CC anywhere on the graph, and draw a quadratic passing through these three points, like this:

Here is a solution:

Download GeoGebra file

We write A(a1,a2)A(a1,a2), B(b1,b2)B(b1,b2) and C(c1,c2)C(c1,c2) as in the suggestion.

We take our quadratic to be y=px2+qx+ry=px2+qx+r (using pp, qq and rr instead of the usual aa, bb and cc to avoid confusion). Since the quadratic must pass through the points AA, BB and CC, we can write down three simultaneous equations for pp, qq and rr:
a2b2c2=pa21+qa1+r=pb21+qb1+r=pc21+qc1+r.(1)(2)(3)(1)a2=pa12+qa1+r(2)b2=pb12+qb1+r(3)c2=pc12+qc1+r.
 If we subtract equation (3)(3) from equations (1)(1) and (2)(2), we get
a2−c2b2−c2=p(a21−c21)+q(a1−c1)=p(b21−c21)+q(b1−c1)(4)(5)(4)a2−c2=p(a12−c12)+q(a1−c1)(5)b2−c2=p(b12−c12)+q(b1−c1)

which are two simultaneous equations for the two unknowns pp and qq.

We can make the coefficients of qq the same by multiplying (4)(4) by b1−c1b1−c1 and (5)(5) by (a1−c1)(a1−c1), and then subtracting to get

(a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1)=p((a21−c21)(b1−c1)−(b21−c21)(a1−c1))(a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1)=p((a12−c12)(b1−c1)−(b12−c12)(a1−c1))
and so
p=(a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1)(a21−c21)(b1−c1)−(b21−c21)(a1−c1).p=(a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1)(a12−c12)(b1−c1)−(b12−c12)(a1−c1).

Rather than trying to substitute this into (4)(4) or (5)(5) to find qq, we can start again: multiplying (4)(4) by (b21−c21)(b12−c12) and (5)(5) by (a21−c21)(a12−c12) and subtracting gives
(a2−c2)(b21−c21)−(b2−c2)(a21−c21)=q((a1−c1)(b21−c21)−(b1−c1)(a21−c21))(a2−c2)(b12−c12)−(b2−c2)(a12−c12)=q((a1−c1)(b12−c12)−(b1−c1)(a12−c12))
 so that
q=(a2−c2)(b21−c21)−(b2−c2)(a21−c21)(a1−c1)(b21−c21)−(b1−c1)(a21−c21)=(b2−c2)(a21−c21)−(a2−c2)(b21−c21)(a21−c21)(b1−c1)−(b21−c21)(a1−c1),q=(a2−c2)(b12−c12)−(b2−c2)(a12−c12)(a1−c1)(b12−c12)−(b1−c1)(a12−c12)=(b2−c2)(a12−c12)−(a2−c2)(b12−c12)(a12−c12)(b1−c1)−(b12−c12)(a1−c1),

where we have negated the top and bottom of the final answer to make the denominator the same as for pp.

Finally, we substitute this into (1)(1) to get r=a2−pa21−qa1r=a2−pa12−qa1, so in full,
r=a2−a21((a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1))(a21−c21)(b1−c1)−(b21−c21)(a1−c1)−a1((b2−c2)(a21−c21)−(a2−c2)(b21−c21))(a21−c21)(b1−c1)−(b21−c21)(a1−c1).r=a2−a12((a2−c2)(b1−c1)−(b2−c2)(a1−c1))(a12−c12)(b1−c1)−(b12−c12)(a1−c1)−a1((b2−c2)(a12−c12)−(a2−c2)(b12−c12))(a12−c12)(b1−c1)−(b12−c12)(a1−c1).

This is a bit of a mouthful. It can be entered into GeoGebra, perhaps most easily by working out the common denominator first:

d=(x(A)^2-x(C)^2)(x(B)-x(C))-(x(B)^2-x(C)^2)(x(A)-x(C))
p=((y(A)-y(C))(x(B)-x(C))-(y(B)-y(C))(x(A)-x(C)))/d
q=((y(B)-y(C))(x(A)^2-x(C)^2)-(y(A)-y(C))(x(B)^2-x(C)^2))/d
r=y(A)-p*x(A)^2-q*x(A)
y=p*x^2+q*x+r

While this gives a correct answer, it does not give any idea of what is really going on. It is also not “symmetric”: the roles of AA, BB and CC are identical in the problem, though the formula we’ve ended up with does not look symmetrical at all.

The second approach gives much more insight into what is going on here.

The suggestion asked:

Can you find a quadratic which passes through A(a1,a2)A(a1,a2) and intercepts the xx-axis at (b1,0)(b1,0) and (c1,0)(c1,0)?

What other two similar quadratics can you find?

How can you combine these to solve the problem?

We can use the idea from the extension to question 1 above to find such a quadratic. As it passes through (b1,0)(b1,0) and (c1,0)(c1,0), it must have the form y=k(x−b1)(x−c1)y=k(x−b1)(x−c1). Since it also passes through (a1,a2)(a1,a2), it must satisfy

a2=k(a1−b1)(a1−c1)a2=k(a1−b1)(a1−c1)
so that k=a2/(a1−b1)(a1−c1)k=a2/(a1−b1)(a1−c1), giving
y=(x−b1)(x−c1)(a1−b1)(a1−c1)a2.y=(x−b1)(x−c1)(a1−b1)(a1−c1)a2.

Likewise, a quadratic which passes through BB and intercepts the xx-axis at (c1,0)(c1,0) and (a1,0)(a1,0) is

y=(x−c1)(x−a1)(b1−c1)(b1−a1)b2,y=(x−c1)(x−a1)(b1−c1)(b1−a1)b2,
and one which passes through CC and intercepts the xx-axis at (a1,0)(a1,0) and (b1,0)(b1,0) is
y=(x−a1)(x−b1)(c1−a1)(c1−b1)c2.y=(x−a1)(x−b1)(c1−a1)(c1−b1)c2.

If we add these three quadratics together, we get a quadratic which passes through AA, BB and CC. For example, when x=a1x=a1, the first quadratic evaluates to a2a2, while the second and third evealuate to 00: the sum is therefore a2a2, so the quadratic passes through AA. Likewise, it passes through BB and CC.

So an alternative way of expressing the equation of the quadratic through AA, BB and CC is

y=(x−b1)(x−c1)(a1−b1)(a1−c1)a2+(x−c1)(x−a1)(b1−c1)(b1−a1)b2+(x−a1)(x−b1)(c1−a1)(c1−b1)c2.y=(x−b1)(x−c1)(a1−b1)(a1−c1)a2+(x−c1)(x−a1)(b1−c1)(b1−a1)b2+(x−a1)(x−b1)(c1−a1)(c1−b1)c2.

We can enter this in GeoGebra (all on one line) as

y=(x-x(B))(x-x(C))/((x(A)-x(B))(x(A)-x(C)))*y(A)+
(x-x(C))(x-x(A))/((x(B)-x(C))(x(B)-x(A)))*y(B)+
(x-x(A))(x-x(B))/((x(C)-x(A))(x(C)-x(B)))*y(C)

This formula shows the symmetry of the answer.

The technique we have used here is known as the Lagrange Interpolating Polynomial. The formula was first published by Waring in 1779, rediscovered by Euler in 1783, and published again by Lagrange in 1795.

Can you see how the same idea could be used to fit a cubic through 4 points, or a quartic (degree 4 polynomial) through 5 points, and so on?

Is there more than one quadratic you could draw?

We see from the straightforward approach that the three points give us three simultaneous linear equations in three unknowns (pp, qq and rr). These equations will have a unique solution (which we found), so there is only one possible quadratic.

The only exception to this will be if two or more of AA, BB and CC share the same xx-coordinate, in which case there may be no possible quadratics (if they have different yy-coordinates) or infinitely many possible quadratics (if they are the same point).
