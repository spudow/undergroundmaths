Solution

Here are a few quadratic expressions you can use for practising this method.

  1. 4x2+7x+34x2+7x+3

We can partially fill in a multiplication grid with this quadratic:

…x…x ……
…x…x 4x24x2
…… +3+3

The remaining two cells must add to +7x+7x and multiply to 12x212x2, so they are +4x+4x and +3x+3x, giving:

…x…x ……
…x…x 4x24x2 +4x+4x
…… +3x+3x +3+3

We fill in the outside of the grid by looking for the highest common factor of the first column, which in this case is xx.

xx ……
…x…x 4x24x2 +4x+4x
…… +3x+3x +3+3

We then fill in the rest of the grid to make the multiplication work.

The completed grid is as follows.
xx +1+1
4x4x 4x24x2 +4x+4x
+3+3 +3x+3x +3+3
So 4x2+7x+3=(x+1)(4x+3)4x2+7x+3=(x+1)(4x+3).
  1. 3x2+13x−103x2+13x−10
We can partially fill in a multiplication grid with this quadratic:
…x…x ……
…x…x 3x23x2
…… −10−10

The remaining two cells must add to +13x+13x and multiply to −30x2−30x2, so they are −2x−2x and +15x+15x, giving:

…x…x ……
…x…x 3x23x2 −2x−2x
…… +15x+15x −10−10

We fill in the outside of the grid by looking for the highest common factor of the first column and then fill in the rest of the grid to make the multiplication work.

The completed grid is as follows.
3x3x −2−2
xx 3x23x2 −2x−2x
+5+5 +15x+15x −10−10
So 3x2+13x−10=(3x−2)(x+5)3x2+13x−10=(3x−2)(x+5).
  1. 6x2−5x+16x2−5x+1

We can partially fill in a multiplication grid with this quadratic:

…x…x ……
…x…x 6x26x2
…… +1+1

The remaining two cells must add to −5x−5x and multiply to 6x26x2, so they are −2x−2x and −3x−3x, giving:

…x…x ……
…x…x 6x26x2 −2x−2x
…… −3x−3x +1+1

We fill in the outside of the grid by looking for the highest common factor of the first column and then fill in the rest of the grid to make the multiplication work.

The completed grid is as follows.
3x3x −1−1
2x2x 6x26x2 −2x−2x
−1−1 −3x−3x +1+1
So 6x2−5x+1=(3x−1)(2x−1)6x2−5x+1=(3x−1)(2x−1).
  1. 4x2−2x−124x2−2x−12
We can partially fill in a multiplication grid with this quadratic:
…x…x ……
…x…x 4x24x2
…… −12−12

The remaining two cells must add to −2x−2x and multiply to −48x2−48x2, so they are +6x+6x and −8x−8x, giving:

…x…x ……
…x…x 4x24x2 +6x+6x
…… −8x−8x −12−12

In this question, there are two ways that we can fill in the outside of the grid, depending on whether we start by finding the highest common factor of the first column or of the first row. (Alternatively, if we always start by finding the highest common factor of the first column, there are two different ways depending upon which way round we put the −8x−8x and +6x+6x in the grid.)

Starting with the first column
2x2x −4−4
2x2x 4x24x2 −8x−8x
+3+3 +6x+6x −12−12

So 4x2−2x−12=(2x−4)(2x+3)4x2−2x−12=(2x−4)(2x+3).

Starting with the first row
xx −2−2
4x4x 4x24x2 −8x−8x
+6+6 +6x+6x −12−12

So 4x2−2x−12=(x−2)(4x+6)4x2−2x−12=(x−2)(4x+6).

In the previous questions, it made no difference which one we started with. Why are there two possible answers in this case?

Why are there two different possibilities here?

Our quadratic has a factor of 22, and so our two factorisations each have a term which has a factor of 22:

(4x+6)(x−2)(2x+3)(2x−4)=2(2x+3)(x−2)=2(2x+3)(x−2)(4x+6)(x−2)=2(2x+3)(x−2)(2x+3)(2x−4)=2(2x+3)(x−2)

So actually these are the same factorisation, just with the factor of 22 included in the two different brackets. It is a good idea to take out any common factors from the answer as well, as then the structure of the quadratic becomes a little clearer.

In fact, it would probably be easier to take out the common factor of 22 before beginning to factorise: we then have

4x2−2x−12=2(2x2−x−6).4x2−2x−12=2(2x2−x−6).

The resulting quadratic 2x2−x−62x2−x−6 is also easier to factorise, as the numbers involved are smaller.

  1. 2x2−3xy−2y22x2−3xy−2y2

This looks a little different, as there are now appearances of xx and yy. But we can still partially fill in a multiplication grid, only this time, the factors are going to involve xx and yy which means the grid will have this form:

…x…x …y…y
…x…x 2x22x2
…y…y −2y2−2y2

The remaining two cells must add to −3xy−3xy and multiply to −4x2y2−4x2y2.

Pairs of xyxy terms which multiply to −4x2y2−4x2y2 are 4xy4xy and −xy−xy, −4xy−4xy and xyxy, 2xy2xy and −2xy−2xy. Only −4xy−4xy and +xy+xy sum to −3xy−3xy. This gives the grid:

…x…x …y…y
…x…x 2x22x2 −4xy−4xy
…y…y +xy+xy −2y2−2y2

We can now fill in the outside of the grid:

xx −2y−2y
2x2x 2x22x2 −4xy−4xy
+y+y +xy+xy −2y2−2y2
So 2x2−3xy−2y2=(x−2y)(2x+y)2x2−3xy−2y2=(x−2y)(2x+y).

Reflecting

How does this method of factorising quadratics compare to other methods you know?

You may have learnt some other methods, and using whichever seems most appropriate to the context is often sensible.
