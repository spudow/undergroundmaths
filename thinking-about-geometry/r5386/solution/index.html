Thinking about Geometry
Review question

Can we show we have three corners of a rectangle?

Solution

The coordinates of the points AA, BB, CC are (−2,1)(−2,1), (2,7)(2,7), (5,5)(5,5) respectively. Prove that these points form three corners of a rectangle…

To prove that the points form three corners of a rectangle, we need to show that the two lines ABAB and BCBC meet at right angles.

If this is true, then it must be possible to form a rectangle with these three points (and one other).

The slope of the line ABAB is mAB=ΔyΔx=7−12−(−2)=64=32mAB=ΔyΔx=7−12−(−2)=64=32.

The slope of BCBC is mBC=5−75−2=−23=−23mBC=5−75−2=−23=−23.

The product of the two slopes is 32×−23=−132×−23=−1, so the lines are perpendicular and thus there exists a rectangle ABCDABCD.

This is the most straightforward way to prove what we need, but it’s possible (with quite a lot more work) to use the cosine rule instead.

The length of ABAB is (7−1)2+(2−(−2))2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=52⎯⎯⎯⎯√(7−1)2+(2−(−2))2=52.

If DD is the fourth corner of the rectangle, calculate the distance of CC from the diagonal BDBD.

There are a few ways to do this problem.

We could calculate DD’s coordinates, but we can solve the problem without doing this.

This first method shown is more efficient than other methods, which require a greater amount of algebraic manipulation.

The distance of CC from the diagonal BDBD will be the shortest possible distance, which is the perpendicular distance, shown as hh in the diagram below.

By Pythagoras, BDBD is 5⎯⎯√d5d where dd is the length of BCBC. Using similar triangles, we also see that
