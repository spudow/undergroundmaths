Review question

Can we find the areas within the semicircles?

Solution

State the theorem of Pythagoras concerning the three sides of a right-angled triangle.

Pythagoras’s theorem states that, if we are given a right-angled triangle with sides a,ba,b and cc, where cc is the side opposite the right angle, then

a2+b2=c2.a2+b2=c2.

In fact the converse to this is true also, so we can say that a triangle with sides a,ba,b and cc, where cc is the longest side, is right-angled if and only if a2+b2=c2.a2+b2=c2.

The diagram shows an isosceles triangle ABCABC right-angled at BB. Semicircles S1S1 and S2S2 are drawn on ABAB and ACAC as diameter, as shown.

  1. Explain why S2S2 passes through BB.

We know the circle theorem, ‘The angle in a semicircle is a right angle’. We are given here that ACAC is a diameter of S2S2, and that BB is a right angle. If BB is inside S2S2, then we can find a point B′B′ on S2S2 where the angle AB′CAB′C is less than a right angle, which is a contradiction.

Similarly, if BB is outside S2S2, then we can find a point B′B′ on S2S2 where the angle AB′CAB′C is more than a right angle, which is also a contradiction. Thus BB is on S2S2.

Or alternatively we can say that AB=BC,AO=OCAB=BC,AO=OC (where OO is the centre of S2S2), and angle BAOBAO equals angle BCOBCO, and so the triangles ABOABO and BCOBCO are congruent right-angled isosceles triangles.

This means AO=BOAO=BO = radius of S2S2, and BB must be on the semicircle.
