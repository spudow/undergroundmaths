Thinking about Geometry
Review question

How far does this pool ball travel to reach the pocket?

Solution

A toy pool table is 6feet6feet long and 3feet3feet wide. It has pockets at each of the four corners PP, QQ, RR and SS. When a ball hits a side of the table, it bounces off the side at the same angle as it hit that side. A ball, initially 1 foot to the left of the pocket PP, is hit from the side SPSP towards the side PQPQ as shown.

How many feet from PP does the ball hit side PQPQ if it lands in pocket SS after two bounces?

Method 1

Look at the picture:

Point DD is given by the point AA, where the ball is hit, reflected in the line PQPQ while point EE is point SS reflected in RQRQ, and the ball takes the path A→B→C→SA→B→C→S.

We know that all of the red angles (the three angles at B) are the same, since the ball leaves the side of the table by the same angle it hits it (angle CBQCBQ), or because the triangles mirror one another (angle PBDPBD), and similarly for the blue angles (the three angles at C). Notice that the red angle and blue angle are related: they must add up to 90∘90∘. Let’s call the blue angles ββ.

Remember that the angles of a triangle add up to 180∘180∘.

We can work out that the points E,C,BE,C,B and DD all lie on a straight line: angle SCB=180∘−2βSCB=180∘−2β so

angle ECB=β+β+SCB=2β+180∘−2β=180∘.angle ECB=β+β+SCB=2β+180∘−2β=180∘.
A similar calculation for the red angles shows that angle CBDCBD is also 180∘180∘. So ESDESD is a triangle, and furthermore, since ESDESD and BPDBPD are both right-angled triangles which share an angle (BDPBDP), they are similar.

For two similar triangles, the ratios of the sides are the same:

ABA′B′=BCB′C′=ACA′C′.ABA′B′=BCB′C′=ACA′C′.

We can now calculate the length of BPBP:

BPPDBP1BP=ESSD,=67,=67.BPPD=ESSD,BP1=67,BP=67.

Method 2

The solution above might be a bit difficult to spot, since it involves the idea about reflecting the path outside the table.

We’ll now give a different solution using the triangles we can already see, using simultaneous equations to find the unknown length.

Consider the picture below:

The triangles AA, BB and CC are all similar since the red angles and blue angles are the same. We know this because we are told that the ball bounces off the side of the table at the same angle at which it hits it, and also since we know the two non-right angles in each triangle must add up to 90∘90∘. We can derive an equation for each pair of triangles:

A&B:x1=3−x6−y,A&B:x1=3−x6−y,
A&C:x1=3y,A&C:x1=3y,
B&C:3y=3−x6−y.B&C:3y=3−x6−y.

(Note that these equations are not independent; the last is given by the first two.)

Rearranging the equation from triangles AA and BB we see that

6x−xy=3−x,6x−xy=3−x,
so
xy=6x+x−3=7x−3.xy=6x+x−3=7x−3.

We leave it in this form because one of our other equations after rearranging will have an xyxy on one side and a constant on the other.

The equation from triangle AA and CC says xy=3xy=3. So we can see that

7x−3=xy=3.7x−3=xy=3.
Now we have an equation that’s easy to solve:
7x−3=3⟹x=67.7x−3=3⟹x=67.

We can use this to find yy, and then substitute these values back in to the equation from BB and CC to check we have done everything right and really do have a solution.
