The spider and the fly (Part 2)

Solution

You may recall the spider from Part 1. It is sitting in the middle of one of the smallest walls in my living room, and a fly is resting by the side of the window on the opposite wall, some way above the floor and 0.5m0.5m from the adjacent wall.

The room is 8m8m long, 4m4m wide and 3m3m high.

This time, the fly is at a height hmhm above the floor.

  • What is the shortest distance the spider could crawl to reach the fly?

  • How does the spider’s best route depend on hh?

As we saw in the solution to Part 1, there are a number of possible routes: the nearby side wall route, the ceiling route and two possible combinations of these. In part 1, we did not even consider the floor route, as the fly is nearer to the ceiling than the floor. But now, as the fly may be nearer the floor, we need to consider routes involving the floor as well.

Using symmetry

A crucial insight in the above paragraph was that we can ignore the floor route if the fly is above the middle of the room, because the spider is in the very centre of the end wall. This suggests to us that the problem has symmetry.

If we reflect the whole room from top to bottom, we see that the fly being hmhm above the floor is essentially the same as it being hmhm below the ceiling, just with the roles of the floor and ceiling switched:

So if we solve the problem for the fly being in the top half of the room hmhm above the floor, then we automatically know the solution when the fly is in the bottom half (3−h)m(3−h)m above the floor.

The room is 3m3m high, so the top half corresponds to h≥1.5h≥1.5 and the bottom half to h≤1.5h≤1.5.

Since we looked at the case h=2h=2 in Part 1, which was closer to the ceiling, we will continue that work and consider the case where the fly is in the top half of the room, so h≥1.5h≥1.5.

The possible routes and their lengths

As we saw in the solution to Part 1, there are four possible routes. We work out the length of each of them again, this time with the fly being at a height hmhm above the floor.

In Part 1, we rejected the route via the side wall and then the ceiling as it was obviously longer than the side wall route. It is not clear whether that is the case for all values of hh, so we include it for consideration here.

However, we can still reject any routes which involve the spider travelling via the other side wall (the one nearer to us in the picture), since the spider is nearer to the far side wall. With a little bit of thought, it is also possible to see that the spider should not follow a route which passes through both the floor and the ceiling via the side wall, as that will be longer than the route via only the ceiling.

In the following nets, we have indicated the length hh with a thick green line, and marked it hh. We call the distances that the spider walks along the different routes d1d1, d2d2, d3d3 and d4d4 for ease of later reference.

(Note that the spider is above the midpoint, so h≥1.5h≥1.5. If the spider were below the midpoint, we would have to replace the length h−1.5h−1.5 by 1.5−h1.5−h.)

Route 3: via the ceiling and then the wall

d3=distance the spider walks=102+(5−h)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=h2−10h+125⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√.d3=distance the spider walks=102+(5−h)2=h2−10h+125.

If we imagine what happens as hh gets close to 33, we may notice that the spider’s route no longer stays within our net. What might we be able to do in this situation?

We can rescue this by imagining that there’s a second copy of the end wall attached to the end of the ceiling, so that when hh is very close to 33, the spider first reaches the end wall at some point along the join with the ceiling.

So the route suggested by this diagram doesn’t “exist” when kk is very close to 33: we haven’t ever crossed the side wall. Whatever number the calculation therefore gives, it is going to have to be longer than the straight line ceiling route in Route 2 for these values of kk. So we can safely ignore this difficulty, as we are looking for the shortest route.

Route 4: via the wall and then the ceiling

d4=distance the spider walks=22+(13−h)2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=h2−26h+173⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√.d4=distance the spider walks=22+(13−h)2=h2−26h+173.

Comparing the routes

Now that we have all four possible routes, we need to determine which is the shortest. We only need to compare the squares of the distances, so we can ignore the square root signs. (This is because if aa and bb are positive, then a⎯⎯√<b⎯⎯√a<b if and only if a<ba<b.) We have
d21d22d23d24=h2−03h+112.5=h2−25h+158.5=h2−10h+125=h2−26h+173d12=h2−03h+112.5d22=h2−25h+158.5d32=h2−10h+125d42=h2−26h+173
 We see that they all have an identical h2h2 term, so we might as well subtract this and just compare the linear parts; we write y1=d21−h2y1=d12−h2 and so on:
y1y2y3y4=112.5−3h=158.5−25h=125−10h=173−26hy1=112.5−3hy2=158.5−25hy3=125−10hy4=173−26h

We are interested in the range 1.5≤h≤31.5≤h≤3 (the fly is in the upper half of the room).

Comparing these still looks a little tricky, so we use graph-plotting software to draw the four graphs; this will give us a good idea of what is going on and save us from having to do lots of unnecessary algebra.

This is the resulting graph. (The hh-axis extends beyond 1.51.5 and 33 for convenience, even though hh can only take values between them.)

From the graph, we see that Route 1 is shortest for 1.5≤h<1.81.5≤h<1.8 (or thereabouts), then Route 3 is the shortest until h≈2.25h≈2.25. Then Route 2 is shortest until h=3h=3. Route 4, via the wall and then the ceiling, is never shortest.

To find the transition points exactly, we need only solve equations such as y1=y3y1=y3 to find where Route 1 and Route 3 are equal in length, and similarly for when Routes 3 and 2 are the same length.

If we had not used graph-plotting software, we would have been forced to work purely algebraically. We would have had to compare several other pairs of lines, so the software has saved us lots of needless effort.

The first transition occurs when y1=y3y1=y3: we can substitute our formulae for y1y1 and y3y3 in to get

112.5−3h=125−10h112.5−3h=125−10h
which gives 7h=12.57h=12.5, so h=1.79h=1.79 to 2 d.p.

The second transition occurs when y3=y2y3=y2, giving

125−10h=158.5−25h,125−10h=158.5−25h,
so 15h=33.515h=33.5, so h=2.23h=2.23 to 2 d.p.

Using symmetry and finishing off

We now know that Route 2, the ceiling route, is best for heights in the range 2.23m2.23m to 3m3m. Thus by symmetry, the floor route is best for heights in the range the range 0m0m to 0.77m0.77m.

Route 3, via the ceiling and then the side wall, is best for heights in the range 1.79m1.79m to 2.23m2.23m. By symmetry, therefore, the route via the floor and then the side wall will be best for heights in the range 0.77m0.77m to 1.21m1.21m.

Finally, Route 1, via the side wall, is best for heights in the range 1.5m1.5m to 1.79m1.79m, and by symmetry, also for heights in the range 1.21m1.21m to 1.5m1.5m. We can combine these ranges, and deduce that the side wall route is best for heights in the range 1.21m1.21m to 1.79m1.79m.
