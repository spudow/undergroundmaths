Things you might have noticed

Take a look at the image below.

What do you make of it?

Can I find line segments of lengths 22, 33, 44, ……?

How many steps would I need to construct 5⎯⎯√5 in the diagram?

Are there any lengths that do not occur in the diagram?

One side of each right-angled triangle is always of length 11 and the side perpendicular to this is equal to the hypotenuse of the previous triangle.

Starting with the 1×11×1 triangle I can use Pythagoras’ theorem to calculate the length of the hypotenuse to be 2⎯⎯√2. Therefore the perpendicular side lengths of the second triangle in the spiral are 11 and 2⎯⎯√2.

Repeating this process shows me that the length of the second hypotenuse is 3⎯⎯√3, the third is 4⎯⎯√4, the fourth is 5⎯⎯√5, and so on.

I notice that the third hypotenuse is of length 4⎯⎯√=24=2. Given that the sequence of hypotenuse lengths will continue I predict that the 88th hypotenuse will have a length of 9⎯⎯√=39=3 and the 1515th will have a length of 16⎯⎯⎯⎯√=416=4. The whole number lengths appear further and further apart as the spiral continues.

Can I construct a length of 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13 in any other way (using straight-edge and compasses)?

Which other lengths can I construct in other ways (using straight-edge and compasses)?

Each of the lengths in this image is created using the previous triangle. So to get to 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13 I had to construct 1212 triangles in the spiral.

If I want to construct a length of 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13 directly then I could do this if 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13 is the hypotenuse of a right-angled triangle with integer side lengths. I would then require that a2+b2=13a2+b2=13 where aa and bb are integers. This can be satisfied if I take a=2a=2 and b=3b=3.

The question of whether I can construct other lengths in different, potentially more efficient ways, is less obvious. I saw that it was possible to produce a length of 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13 by constructing a single right-angled triangle. Perhaps it makes sense to start by considering other lengths that can be constructed using a single right-angled triangle. Then to produce a length nn we require that a2+b2=n2a2+b2=n2 where aa and bb are integers, so for example 8⎯⎯√8 can be produced by taking a=b=2a=b=2.

It is interesting to notice here that I am saying that 8⎯⎯√8 can be produced from an enlargement (scale factor 22) of the original 1×11×1 right-angled triangle. From this I can deduce that 8⎯⎯√=22⎯⎯√8=22.

Perhaps this is useful to help me identify some other lengths that can be produced by enlarging the original 1×11×1 right-angled triangle.

I need to be aware that I cannot produce all lengths by constructing a single right-angled triangle. For example, there are no integer values for aa and bb that will satisfy a2+b2=6a2+b2=6. However, this does not mean that I cannot construct lengths like this more efficiently than in the spiral.

If we take 12⎯⎯⎯⎯√12 then in the spiral I would need to construct 1111 right-angled triangles to reach it. However, if I construct a 2×22×2 right-angled triangle and then a second right-angled triangle from this of side lengths 22 and 8⎯⎯√8 then I produce 12⎯⎯⎯⎯√12.

Alternatively I could construct a right-angled triangle with a base length of 22 and a hypotenuse of 44. The remaining side length would be 12⎯⎯⎯⎯√12 as required.

If I want to construct a right-angled triangle with a base length of 22 and a hypotenuse of 44, using straight-edge and compasses, then it might help to think of it as half of an equilateral triangle of side length 44. Once I’ve constructed this equilateral triangle I can bisect an angle and extend the line to the opposite side to create the required right-angled triangle.

How will the spiral change if I start with a 2×22×2 right-angled triangle instead?

I began to answer this question above, when I realised that 8⎯⎯√8 could be constructed from a 2×22×2 right-angled triangle. Assuming that the spiral will have the property that every triangle will have one constant side length of 22 then I get hypotenuse lengths of 8⎯⎯√8, 12⎯⎯⎯⎯√12, 16⎯⎯⎯⎯√16, 20⎯⎯⎯⎯√20, 24⎯⎯⎯⎯√24, and so on. This time the values inside the square roots are going up in fours (compared to ones in the original image). This is to be expected as the constant side length is 22 and the next hypotenuse is given by a2+22=c2a2+22=c2.

It is also interesting to notice that this sequence could be written as 22⎯⎯√22, 23⎯⎯√23, 24⎯⎯√24, 25⎯⎯√25, 26⎯⎯√26, ……

How will the spiral change if I start with a right-angled triangle that isn’t isosceles?

If I start with a 1×21×2 right-angled triangle then my first hypotenuse length is 5⎯⎯√5. If my spiral continues to follow the pattern of keeping one side length constant then this spiral will progress in a similar way to the 2×22×2 spiral considered above: 5⎯⎯√5, 9⎯⎯√9, 13⎯⎯⎯⎯√13, 17⎯⎯⎯⎯√17, ……

I wonder what would happen if the spiral did not have a constant side length…
