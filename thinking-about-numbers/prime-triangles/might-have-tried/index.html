Things you might have tried

Which triangular numbers are also prime?

How many ways can you find to justify your answer?

T1T1 T2T2 T3T3 T4T4 T5T5 T6T6 T7T7 T8T8 T9T9 T10T10 T11T11 T12T12
11 33 66 1010 1515 2121 2828 3636 4545 5555 6666 7878

Looking at the first twelve triangular numbers, we see that only 33 is prime, the others are all composite numbers (apart from 11). So our conjecture might be that 33 is the only triangular number which is prime. How can we go about justifying this?

The table above should allow us to see some interesting patterns.

We might notice that

  • the triangular numbers go in the order two odd, two even, two odd and so on.

  • in every three numbers, the second and third numbers are divisible by 33.

If these two patterns are true, then in every twelve triangular numbers, there are only two that are not divisible by 22 or 33. Can you explain why these patterns must continue?

  • that T1T1 and T2T2 are divisible by 11, T3T3 and T4T4 are divisible by 22, T5T5 and T6T6 are divisible by 33, and so on. Is it possible to show this pattern continues?

Can you see why these come in pairs? It might help to think about the recursive relationship of triangular numbers,

Tn=Tn1+n.Tn=Tn1+n.

If a rectangle has integer valued sides, then as long as it is not a 1n1n rectangle then we know the area cannot be prime, as the lengths of the sides will be factors.

With this in mind, can we rearrange the triangles into rectangles?

We cant change T1T1, and we know T2T2 is prime. We can see there is a difference in how we have split T3T3 and T4T4, so lets group our triangular numbers (TnTn) into nn being even and nn being odd.

Even case:

Weve seen it works with n=4n=4 so lets think more generally. If nn is even we will be able to split the triangle down the middle of each short side into three sections, a square and two congruent triangles, all with sides of n2n2.

Regardless of the value of nn, we will always be able to put the two triangles together to form a rectangle as they are the same width.

The new rectangle will have dimensions of n2n2 and n+1n+1, so these will both be factors of the original triangle number. As long as n4n4 then the two factors will be greater than 11 so the triangular number cannot be prime.

Odd case:

This time we cannot split the triangle equally, although we can still split it into a square and two congruent triangles.

  • Can you see what the factors will be this time?

This time the rectangle will have dimensions of nn and n+12n+12, so as long as n3n3 then the two factors will be greater than 11 so it cannot be prime.

You may have noticed that the two factors from each case are very similar. This is because effectively we have found the formula that gives us triangular numbers. For even cases we found it was given by

n2(n+1)n2(n+1)

and for odd cases

n(n+1)2.n(n+1)2.

These are the same, and give the general formula,

Tn=n(n+1)2.Tn=n(n+1)2.

If we worked out, or were already familiar with the formula for the nnth triangle number, then we might have used this to prove our conjecture.

Tn=n(n+1)2Tn=n(n+1)2

Similar to the geometric approach we can consider the two cases of nn being odd or nn being even.

nn is odd

If nn is odd, then n+1n+1 will be even. Therefore n+12n+12 will be an integer value. So in this case, TnTn has factors of nn and n+12n+12, as they are both integers. As long as n3n3 then neither factor can be 11, so TnTn cannot be prime.

nn is even

If nn is even, then n2n2 will be an integer value. So in this case, TnTn has factors of n+1n+1 and n2n2, as they are both integers. As long as n4n4 then neither factor can be 11, so TnTn cannot be prime.

This means that there can be no triangular number greater than T2=3T2=3 which is also prime.

Another way to think about even and odd numbers is to say that n=2kn=2k or n=2k+1n=2k+1. You can substitute these expressions in for nn and see what happens. This can be quite a useful way of expressing odd and even numbers that you are likely to come across again.

You might have found that much of the final reasoning of the three different approaches involved some similar algebra. However, how you arrived at that point and the thinking that went on to get there was very different. There are almost always different ways to journey through a problem, and depending on the approach you take, you may gain different insights and understandings compared to someone who took a different route.
