Thinking about Numbers
Review question

How many grid-points can be inside this circle?

Solution

Suppose that aa, bb, cc are integers such that

a2⎯⎯√+b=c3⎯⎯√.a2+b=c3.

  1. By squaring both sides of the equation, show that a=b=c=0a=b=c=0.

[You may assume that 2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√2,3 and 2/3⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√2/3 are all irrational numbers. An irrational number is one which cannot be written in the form p/qp/q where pp and qq are integers.]

Squaring both sides of the equation gives

2a2+2ab2⎯⎯√+b2=3c2.2a2+2ab2+b2=3c2.

If ab≠0ab≠0 then

2⎯⎯√=3c2−2a2−b22ab,2=3c2−2a2−b22ab,
but this is a contradiction because 2⎯⎯√2 is irrational and so cannot be written in the form pqpq for integers pp and qq.

So we must have either a=0a=0 or b=0b=0.

If a=0a=0, then the initial equation becomes b=c3⎯⎯√b=c3. Either we have b=c=0b=c=0 or 3⎯⎯√=b/c3=b/c. The latter is impossible because 3⎯⎯√3 is irrational.

Similarly, if b=0b=0, then the initial equation becomes a2⎯⎯√=c3⎯⎯√a2=c3. Again, we either have a=c=0a=c=0 or 2/3⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=c/a2/3=c/a. The latter is impossible as 2/3⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√2/3 is irrational.

So either way, we must have a=b=c=0a=b=c=0.

  1. Suppose now that mm, nn, MM, NN are integers such that the distance from the point (m,n)(m,n) to (2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√)(2,3) equals the distance from (M,N)(M,N) to (2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√)(2,3).

Show that m=Mm=M and n=Nn=N.

Given that the distance from the point (m,n)(m,n) to (2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√)(2,3) equals the distance from (M,N)(M,N) to (2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√)(2,3), we have

(m−2⎯⎯√)2+(n−3⎯⎯√)2=(M−2⎯⎯√)2+(N−3⎯⎯√)2,(m−2)2+(n−3)2=(M−2)2+(N−3)2,
which after rearranging becomes
22⎯⎯√(M−m)+(m2+n2−M2−N2)=23⎯⎯√(n−N).22(M−m)+(m2+n2−M2−N2)=23(n−N).

Now this is in the same form as the given initial equation, with
abc=2(M−m),=(m2+n2−M2−N2),=2(n−N).a=2(M−m),b=(m2+n2−M2−N2),c=2(n−N).

From the first part of the question, we know that a=b=c=0a=b=c=0, and so we have

2(M−m)=0=2(n−N),2(M−m)=0=2(n−N),
and hence m=Mm=M and n=Nn=N.

Given real numbers aa, bb and a positive number rr, let N(a,b,r)N(a,b,r) be the number of integer pairs xx, yy such that the distance between the points (x,y)(x,y) and (a,b)(a,b) is less than or equal to rr. For example, we see that N(1.2,0,1.5)=7N(1.2,0,1.5)=7 in the diagram below.

  1. Explain why N(0.5,0.5,r)N(0.5,0.5,r) is a multiple of 44 for any value of rr.

If a particular point (x,y)(x,y) is within a distance rr of (12,12)(12,12), then so are its reflections in the lines x=12x=12, y=12y=12, and in both lines.

These are the points (1−x,y)(1−x,y), (x,1−y)(x,1−y) and (1−x,1−y)(1−x,1−y) respectively.

No points with integer coordinates can lie on these reflection lines.

So the points with integer coordinates that are a maximum distance of rr away from (12,12)(12,12) can be split into 44 sets of equal size by the lines x=12x=12 and y=12y=12, as shown in the diagram below.

Hence N(0.5,0.5,r)N(0.5,0.5,r) must be a multiple of 44.

  1. Let kk be any positive integer. Explain why there is a positive number rr such that N(2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√,r)=kN(2,3,r)=k.

As rr increases, the circle grows and contains more lattice points (points with integer coordinates), without limit.

But in part (ii), we showed that there are no two lattice points equidistant from (2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√)(2,3), so the circle must only ‘consume’ one lattice point at a time.

Hence N(2⎯⎯√,3⎯⎯√,r)N(2,3,r) can take any positive integer value.
