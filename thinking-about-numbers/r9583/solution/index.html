Thinking about Numbers
Review question

Can we express this unit fraction as the sum of two others?

Solution

A number of the form 1/N1/N, where NN is an integer greater than 11, is called a unit fraction.

Noting that

12=13+16and13=14+112,12=13+16and13=14+112,
guess a general result of the form
1N=1a+1b1N=1a+1b
 and hence prove that any unit fraction can be expressed as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

Based on the two examples given, we guess

1N=1N+1+1N(N+1),1N=1N+1+1N(N+1),
i.e. a=N+1a=N+1 and b=N(N+1)b=N(N+1). To justify this, it suffices to note that
1N+1+1N(N+1)=N+1N(N+1)=1N.1N+1+1N(N+1)=N+1N(N+1)=1N.
 In this expression, we can choose NN to be an arbitrary integer greater than 11. Since N(N+1)>N+1N(N+1)>N+1 if N>1N>1, it follows that any unit fraction can be expressed as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

By writing the previous equation in the form

(a−N)(b−N)=N2(a−N)(b−N)=N2
and by considering the factors of N2N2, show that if NN is prime, then there is only one way of expressing 1/N1/N as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

We can convert the original equation into this form by first multiplying both sides by abNabN to get

ab=Nb+Na.ab=Nb+Na.
Now subtracting Na+NbNa+Nb from both sides gives ab−Na−Nb=0ab−Na−Nb=0. If we add N2N2 to both sides, then the resulting expression can be factorised to get (a−N)(b−N)=N2(a−N)(b−N)=N2, which is what we wanted.

Without loss of generality we may assume a≤ba≤b. If NN is prime then there are only two possible ways of factorising N2N2, namely N2=N×NN2=N×N and N2=1×N2N2=1×N2. Comparing this with

(a−N)(b−N)=N2(a−N)(b−N)=N2
gives a−N=b−N=Na−N=b−N=N in the first case and a−N=1a−N=1 and b−N=N2b−N=N2 in the latter case. If we have a−N=b−N=Na−N=b−N=N then a=ba=b whereas a−N=1a−N=1 and b−N=N2b−N=N2 yields b=N(N+1)>N+1=ab=N(N+1)>N+1=a. Therefore, there is indeed only one way of expressing 1/N1/N as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

Prove similarly that any fraction of the form 2/N2/N, where NN is prime number greater than 22, can be expressed uniquely as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

Method 1

We begin by writing

2N=1a+1b2N=1a+1b
and again assume that a<ba<b (as we wish aa and bb to be distinct). Multiplying both sides by abNabN and subtracting terms gives
2ab−Na−Nb=0.2ab−Na−Nb=0.
 Unfortunately, this does not appear to factorise nicely because of the 22, so we try halving to get
ab−12Na−12Nb=0.ab−12Na−12Nb=0.
 This is now almost what we had before, and we can factorise to get
(a−12N)(b−12N)=14N2.(a−12N)(b−12N)=14N2.
 Multiplying through by 44 removes the fractions, giving
(2a−N)(2b−N)=N2,(2a−N)(2b−N)=N2,
 so as before we must have 2a−N=12a−N=1, 2b−N=N22b−N=N2 (as a≠ba≠b), so a=N+12a=N+12, b=N2+N2b=N2+N2. These are both integers as NN is an odd prime (making N+1N+1 and N2+N=N(N+1)N2+N=N(N+1) both even), so there is a unique way to express 2/N2/N as the sum of two distinct unit fractions.

Method 2

In this final part to the question, we are asked to solve a problem that is a modified version of an earlier part.

Sometimes we can do that using a similar approach, and that’s what we did in Method 1.

But sometimes we can also do it by using the answer to the earlier part to help with the later part, and that’s what we do in Method 2.

For the last part, we want to prove that if N>2N>2 is prime then we can uniquely write

2N=1c+1d2N=1c+1d
with c≠dc≠d. If we have such a way then
1N=12c+12d1N=12c+12d
 where both 2c2c and 2d2d are integers. On the other hand, since NN is a prime we already know that
1N=1N+1+1N(N+1)1N=1N+1+1N(N+1)
 is the unique way of expressing 1/N1/N as the sum of two distinct unit fractions. Moreover, as NN is a prime greater than 22 it must be odd and therefore, both N+1N+1 and N(N+1)N(N+1) are even. Thus, (N+1)/2(N+1)/2 and N(N+1)/2N(N+1)/2 are integers and therefore, there is indeed a unique way of expressing 2/N2/N as the sum of two distinct unit fractions, namely
2N=1N+12+1N(N+1)2.2N=1N+12+1N(N+1)2.
