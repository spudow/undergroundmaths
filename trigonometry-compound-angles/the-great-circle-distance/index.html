The great circle distance

How can we find the formula for the great circle distance?

In Lost but lovely: the haversine we explored great circle distances on the Earth and looked at the haversine formula for calculating them:

haversin(dR)=haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cosϕ1cosϕ2haversin(λ2−λ1).haversin⁡(dR)=haversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(λ2−λ1).

Recall that in this formula, we are finding the great circle distance dd between two points PP and QQ which have latitudes ϕ1ϕ1 and ϕ2ϕ2 and longitudes λ1λ1 and λ2λ2 respectively. The radius of the Earth (or sphere) is RR, and the haversine is defined by

haversinx=sin2(x2).haversin⁡x=sin2⁡(x2).
(On the left hand side of the first equation, dRdR is thought of as an angle in radians.)

So we can rewrite the haversine formula in more familiar notation as

sin2(d2R)=sin2(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(λ2−λ12).sin2⁡(d2R)=sin2⁡(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(λ2−λ12).

In this piece, we explore where this formula comes from, and why navigators might have used the unfamiliar haversine rather than ordinary sines and cosines in their calculations.

Preliminaries

We can use any units of distance that we wish when we do our calculations: changing units does not affect the value of dRdR (as long as we use the same units for both dd and RR). Most conveniently, we can choose units so that the radius of the earth is R=1R=1, meaning that we are working with a unit sphere. Then the haversine formula becomes
haversind=haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cosϕ1cosϕ2haversin(λ2−λ1)(1)(1)haversin⁡d=haversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(λ2−λ1)
 or
sin2(d2)=sin2(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(λ2−λ12),(2)(2)sin2⁡(d2)=sin2⁡(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(λ2−λ12),

which looks a little simpler. We will work with these forms throughout.

The great circle distance, dd, is the shorter arc joining two points on a great circle. We can also consider the chord (straight line) joining the two points, and we let its length be CC.

We can immediately observe some relationships between dd, CC and the angle σσ (measured in radians) that the great circle arc makes with the centre of the sphere: we have d=σd=σ (as the radius is 11), and by drawing the isosceles triangle POQPOQ, we see that C=2sin(σ2)C=2sin⁡(σ2):

Putting these together by eliminating σσ,
C2=sin(d2).C2=sin⁡(d2).
 If we square this and multiply by 44, we get an alternative expression:
C2=4sin2(d2)=4haversind.(3)(3)C2=4sin2⁡(d2)=4haversin⁡d.

We now offer four approaches to deducing the haversine formula. The first two of these use coordinates, so we begin with this.

Coordinates

Given a point on the unit sphere with latitude ϕϕ and longitude λλ, we can calculate the corresponding Cartesian coordinates, as shown in the following diagram.

What are the Cartesian coordinates of PP (that is, its (x,y,z)(x,y,z) coordinates)?

From the diagram we can see that

firstlyandsoandzOP′xy=sinϕ=cosϕ,=cosϕcosλ=cosϕsinλ.firstlyz=sin⁡ϕandOP′=cos⁡ϕ,sox=cos⁡ϕcos⁡λandy=cos⁡ϕsin⁡λ.

Once we know the Cartesian coordinates of the points PP and QQ with latitudes ϕ1ϕ1 and ϕ2ϕ2 and longitudes λ1λ1 and λ2λ2 respectively, we can calculate the differences in the xx, yy and zz coordinates of the two points and use those to calculate appropriate distances or angles in order to find dd.

However, the expressions are going to become quite cumbersome with so many variables, so we look for a way to simplify things before going further.

One trick is to notice that the distances and angle σσ all remain unchanged if we rotate the sphere about the north-south axis. This only changes the longitudes of the points, so we could rotate it until PP has longitude 00 and QQ has longitude λ2−λ1λ2−λ1, which we will call ΔλΔλ (the difference between the λλs).

Then the coordinates of PP and QQ become:
PQ=(cosϕ1,0,sinϕ1)=(cosϕ2cos(Δλ),cosϕ2sin(Δλ),sinϕ2).P=(cos⁡ϕ1,0,sin⁡ϕ1)Q=(cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ),cos⁡ϕ2sin⁡(Δλ),sin⁡ϕ2).

We could perhaps try rotating further so that PP lies at the north pole, say, but it is not clear what would happen to QQ if we did that, so we will stop rotating at this point.

Approach 1: Pythagoras and trigonometry

What is the distance between PP and QQ?

Using Pythagoras’s theorem, the square of the distance is given by
(cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−cosϕ1)2+(cosϕ2sin(Δλ))2+(sinϕ2−sinϕ1)2=cos2ϕ2cos2(Δλ)−2cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)+cos2ϕ1+cos2ϕ2sin2(Δλ)+sin2ϕ2−2sinϕ1sinϕ2+sin2ϕ1=cos2ϕ2−2cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−2sinϕ1sinϕ2+1+sin2ϕ2=2−2cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−2sinϕ1sinϕ2.(cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−cos⁡ϕ1)2+(cos⁡ϕ2sin⁡(Δλ))2+(sin⁡ϕ2−sin⁡ϕ1)2=cos2⁡ϕ2cos2⁡(Δλ)−2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)+cos2⁡ϕ1+cos2⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(Δλ)+sin2⁡ϕ2−2sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2+sin2⁡ϕ1=cos2⁡ϕ2−2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−2sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2+1+sin2⁡ϕ2=2−2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−2sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2.

(We have used the identity sin2x+cos2x=1sin2⁡x+cos2⁡x=1 a few times here.)

Since the distance between them is actually CC, we have

C2=2−2cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−2sinϕ1sinϕ2.C2=2−2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−2sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2.

We can take the square root of this to find CC if we wish to.

Recalling from above that C2=sin(d2)C2=sin⁡(d2), can you use this to obtain an expression for sin2(d2)sin2⁡(d2) as it appears in the haversine formula (equation (2)(2) above)?

We have to divide our previous expression by 4=224=22 to get

sin2(d2)=(C2)2=12(1−cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−sinϕ1sinϕ2).sin2⁡(d2)=(C2)2=12(1−cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2).

Comparing this with the expression we want, we are reminded of the half-angle formula sin2x2=12(1−cosx)sin2⁡x2=12(1−cos⁡x) and also the compound angle formula

cos(ϕ2−ϕ1)=cosϕ2cosϕ1+sinϕ2sinϕ1.cos⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)=cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡ϕ1+sin⁡ϕ2sin⁡ϕ1.

Why this particular compound angle formula?

So rewriting cos(Δλ)cos⁡(Δλ) first, we get
sin2(d2)=12(1−cosϕ1cosϕ2(1−2sin2(12Δλ))−sinϕ1sinϕ2)=12(1−cosϕ1cosϕ2+2cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(12Δλ)−sinϕ1sinϕ2)=12(1−cos(ϕ2−ϕ1)+2cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(12Δλ))=12(2sin2(12(ϕ2−ϕ1))+2cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(12Δλ))=sin2(12(ϕ2−ϕ1))+cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(12Δλ)=sin2(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cosϕ1cosϕ2sin2(λ2−λ12)sin2⁡(d2)=12(1−cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2(1−2sin2⁡(12Δλ))−sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2)=12(1−cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2+2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(12Δλ)−sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2)=12(1−cos⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)+2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(12Δλ))=12(2sin2⁡(12(ϕ2−ϕ1))+2cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(12Δλ))=sin2⁡(12(ϕ2−ϕ1))+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(12Δλ)=sin2⁡(ϕ2−ϕ12)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2sin2⁡(λ2−λ12)

as we wanted.

Approach 2: Vectors

Since we know the coordinates of PP and QQ, we also know the vectors p=OP−→−p=OP→ and q=OQ−→−q=OQ→. They each have unit length, so the angle between them, σσ, is given by cosσ=p⋅qcos⁡σ=p⋅q.

Can you use this to find an expression for sin2(d2)sin2⁡(d2)?

Recalling that P=(cosϕ1,0,sinϕ1)P=(cos⁡ϕ1,0,sin⁡ϕ1) and Q=(cosϕ2cos(Δλ),cosϕ2sin(Δλ),sinϕ2)Q=(cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ),cos⁡ϕ2sin⁡(Δλ),sin⁡ϕ2), we have
cosσ=p⋅q=cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)+0+sinϕ1sinϕ2.cos⁡σ=p⋅q=cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)+0+sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2.

Now σ=dσ=d, so we can use the formula sin2x2=12(1−cosx)sin2⁡x2=12(1−cos⁡x) to write down

sin2(d2)=12(1−cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−sinϕ1sinϕ2).sin2⁡(d2)=12(1−cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2).

We can then continue exactly as in Approach 1.

Approach 3: Spherical trigonometry

Just as there are sine and cosine rules for triangles in the plane, there are similar rules for triangles on a sphere.

Here is a drawing of a spherical triangle:

The angles AA, BB and CC are the angles at the vertices AA, BB and CC of the triangle, while aa, bb and cc are the side lengths as usual. But if we take the sphere to have radius 11, then they can also be thought of as angles: aa is the angle ∠BOC∠BOC, for example. We therefore assume the sphere to have radius 11 from now on.

There are two spherical cosine rules and one spherical sine rule, which are:

  • spherical cosine rule (1):

    cosa=cosbcosc+sinbsinccosAcos⁡a=cos⁡bcos⁡c+sin⁡bsin⁡ccos⁡A
    (and similar equations with aa, bb, cc and AA, BB, CC permuted)

  • spherical cosine rule (2):

    cosA=−cosBcosC+sinBsinCcosacos⁡A=−cos⁡Bcos⁡C+sin⁡Bsin⁡Ccos⁡a
    (and similar equations with aa, bb, cc and AA, BB, CC permuted)

  • spherical sine rule:

    sinAsina=sinBsinb=sinCsincsin⁡Asin⁡a=sin⁡Bsin⁡b=sin⁡Csin⁡c

Can you use this information to find an expression for cosσcos⁡σ and then sin2(d2)sin2⁡(d2) in our case?

If we take AA to be the north pole, BB to be our point PP and CC to be our point QQ, then a=σa=σ, b=π2−ϕ2b=π2−ϕ2 (sometimes called the colatitude of QQ – can you see why?), c=π2−ϕ1c=π2−ϕ1 and A=ΔλA=Δλ.

This gives another way of seeing why only the difference between the longitudes is important.

So we know bb, cc and AA, and we want to find aa, meaning that we can use the first spherical cosine rule. This immediately gives
cosσ=cos(π2−ϕ2)cos(π2−ϕ1)+sin(π2−ϕ2)sin(π2−ϕ2)cos(Δλ)=sinϕ1sinϕ2+cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ).cos⁡σ=cos⁡(π2−ϕ2)cos⁡(π2−ϕ1)+sin⁡(π2−ϕ2)sin⁡(π2−ϕ2)cos⁡(Δλ)=sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ).

As in Approach 2, we have σ=dσ=d, so we can use the formula sin2x2=12(1−cosx)sin2⁡x2=12(1−cos⁡x) to write down

sin2(d2)=12(1−cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)−sinϕ1sinϕ2).sin2⁡(d2)=12(1−cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)−sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2).

Again, we can then continue exactly as in Approach 1.

Approach 4: Using haversines directly

The above approaches have all proven the haversine formula via the familiar sine and cosine functions. In this approach, we will use equation (3)(3) above for the length of a chord in terms of the haversine function:

C2=4haversind,C2=4haversin⁡d,
where dd is the great circle distance (or angle subtended) and CC is the corresponding chord length.

We wish to find the great-circle distance dd between the points PP and QQ with latitudes ϕ1ϕ1 and ϕ2ϕ2 and longitudes λ1λ1 and λ2λ2 respectively.

By thinking about symmetry, we are prompted to also consider the points RR and SS with latitudes ϕ1ϕ1 and ϕ2ϕ2 but with swapped longitudes λ2λ2 and λ1λ1 respectively. This gives us the following configuration:

In this diagram, the blue lines are all straight chords (though an optical illusion makes it look otherwise), and the red line is the great circle between PP and QQ.

Can you use this diagram to deduce the haversine formula?

The first thing to observe is that the lines PRPR and SQSQ are parallel. This is because the circles at latitudes ϕ1ϕ1 and ϕ2ϕ2 are just scaled versions of each other. This means that the quadrilateral PRQSPRQS is actually planar and a trapezium. Furthermore, the lengths PSPS and RQRQ are the same by construction. The trapezium is therefore an isosceles trapezium. We can redraw it like this:

We can work out the length ee directly, as the great circle between PP and SS is the line of longitude, so

e2=4haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1).e2=4haversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1).
(It might be that the angle between PP and SS is actually ϕ1−ϕ2ϕ1−ϕ2, but as haversin(−x)=haversinxhaversin⁡(−x)=haversin⁡x, it makes no difference.)

If PP and RR were on the equator, we would have PR2=4haversin(λ2−λ1)PR2=4haversin⁡(λ2−λ1) in the same way. But the circle of latitude ϕ1ϕ1 has radius cosϕ1cos⁡ϕ1, so scaling by a factor of cosϕ1cos⁡ϕ1 gives us
ba=2cosϕ1haversin(λ2−λ1)⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=2cosϕ2haversin(λ2−λ1)⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√,b=2cos⁡ϕ1haversin⁡(λ2−λ1)a=2cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(λ2−λ1),

with the second line following similarly.

We can finish the argument off with two applications of Pythagoras’ theorem on the triangles PQTPQT and QTRQTR:
C2e2=h2+(b+a2)2=h2+(b−a2)2C2=h2+(b+a2)2e2=h2+(b−a2)2

Eliminating h2h2 from these gives

C2=e2+(b+a2)2−(b−a2)2=e2+ab.C2=e2+(b+a2)2−(b−a2)2=e2+ab.
But C2=4haversindC2=4haversin⁡d and we have now worked out e2e2, aa and bb, so substituting for these gives
4haversind=4haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1)+4cosϕ1cosϕ2haversin(λ2−λ1).4haversin⁡d=4haversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)+4cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(λ2−λ1).
 Dividing by 44 then gives the haversine formula.

How do these four approaches compare?

Why use haversines?

Surely we could just use cos(dR)=cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)+sinϕ1sinϕ2cos⁡(dR)=cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)+sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2 to calculate great circle distances, as we found in Approaches 2 and 3? Why would anyone need the haversine? (We have reinserted the radius RR here, as practical calculations will use standard units of measurement. Also, a reason is given in Lost but lovely: the haversine for introducing the haversine, but that was just to simplify the formula involving three occurrences of sin2x2sin2⁡x2.)

Navigators in the past would have had to make do with tables and slide rules: there were no calculators. So what would happen if we try to work out the distance between Dover and Calais, say, using the two different formulae, but restricting ourselves to the limited calculating tools of the past?

Here is some relevant information:

  • Dover: latitude 51.15∘N51.15∘N, longitude 1.33∘E1.33∘E
  • Calais: latitude 50.97∘N50.97∘N, longitude 1.85∘E1.85∘E
  • Radius of the Earth: 6378km6378km

And trigonometric values, accurate to 4 significant figures or 4 decimal places:

sin51.15∘sin50.97∘cos0.52∘haversin0.18∘haversin0.52∘=0.7788=0.7768=1.0000=2.467×10−6=2.059×10−5cos51.15∘cos50.97∘=0.6273=0.6297sin⁡51.15∘=0.7788cos⁡51.15∘=0.6273sin⁡50.97∘=0.7768cos⁡50.97∘=0.6297cos⁡0.52∘=1.0000haversin⁡0.18∘=2.467×10−6haversin⁡0.52∘=2.059×10−5

Using these values, calculate the great circle distance from Calais to Dover using the two different formulae (and you can use a calculator for the multiplications, and for working out the final inverse cosine and haversine). To truly emulate the past, round all of your intermediate calculations to 4 significant figures as well.

Using the formula

cos(dR)=cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)+sinϕ1sinϕ2cos⁡(dR)=cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)+sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2
we can calculate as follows (working to 4 significant figures):

cosϕ1cosϕ2cosϕ1cosϕ2cos(Δλ)sinϕ1sinϕ2cos(dR)dRd=0.6273×0.6297=0.3950=0.3950×1.0000=0.3950=0.7788×0.7768=0.6050=0.3950+0.6050=1.0000=0=0cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2=0.6273×0.6297=0.3950cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2cos⁡(Δλ)=0.3950×1.0000=0.3950sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2=0.7788×0.7768=0.6050cos⁡(dR)=0.3950+0.6050=1.0000dR=0d=0

This is quite unfortunate: we have lost too much information because cos(Δλ)cos⁡(Δλ) is so close to 11. In fact, it is 0.99995880.9999588 to 7 decimal places, but even that increased accuracy would be of no help if we continue to work to 4 decimal places further on. Tables were not available with that level of accuracy, so this formula is useless for calculating short distances.

On the other hand, the haversine formula is

haversin(dR)=haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cosϕ1cosϕ2haversin(λ2−λ1).haversin⁡(dR)=haversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)+cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(λ2−λ1).
So we can calculate dd (remembering at the end that we need to work in radians when taking the inverse haversine):

haversin(ϕ2−ϕ1)haversin(λ2−λ1)cosϕ1cosϕ2cosϕ1cosϕ2haversin(Δλ)haversin(dR)dRd=haversin0.18∘=2.467×10−6=haversin0.52∘=2.059×10−5=0.6273×0.6297=0.3950=0.3950×(2.059×10−5)=8.133×10−6=2.467×10−6+8.133×10−6=1.060×10−5=haversin−1(1.060×10−5)=6.512×10−3=6378×(6.512×10−3)=41.53kmhaversin⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1)=haversin⁡0.18∘=2.467×10−6haversin⁡(λ2−λ1)=haversin⁡0.52∘=2.059×10−5cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2=0.6273×0.6297=0.3950cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2haversin⁡(Δλ)=0.3950×(2.059×10−5)=8.133×10−6haversin⁡(dR)=2.467×10−6+8.133×10−6=1.060×10−5dR=haversin−1⁡(1.060×10−5)=6.512×10−3d=6378×(6.512×10−3)=41.53km

And this agrees to 4 significant figures with the value calculated on a calculator, because we have not lost any significant accuracy when working with the haversine of small angles.

The actual great circle distance between Dover and Calais is this, plus or minus a couple of kilometers, as the latitude and longitude are only accurate to 0.01∘0.01∘; they would need to be more precise to give a more accurate answer.

The reason the simple formula fails for small distances is that if ΔλΔλ is small, then cos(Δλ)cos⁡(Δλ) is almost exactly 11. Then the formula becomes

cos(dR)≈cosϕ1cosϕ2+sinϕ1sinϕ2=cos(ϕ2−ϕ1).cos⁡(dR)≈cos⁡ϕ1cos⁡ϕ2+sin⁡ϕ1sin⁡ϕ2=cos⁡(ϕ2−ϕ1).
If the difference in latitudes, ϕ2−ϕ1ϕ2−ϕ1, is also small, then this gives cos(dR)≈1cos⁡(dR)≈1, and so dR≈0dR≈0.

What the haversine formula does instead is to work in terms of the sines of the differences between the latitudes and longitudes. If ΔλΔλ is small, then sin(Δλ)≈Δλsin⁡(Δλ)≈Δλ (as long as we are working in radians), so working to 4 significant figures will still be very accurate.
