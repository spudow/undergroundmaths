Trigonometry: Triangles to Functions
Bigger picture

From stars to waves
Add to your resource collection

Printable version Fullscreen mode

The trigonometric functions sine and cosine seem to have split personalities. We know them as prime examples of periodic functions, since

sin(x)=sin(x+360∘)sin⁡(x)=sin⁡(x+360∘)
and
cos(x)=cos(x+360∘)cos⁡(x)=cos⁡(x+360∘)
 for all xx. With their beautifully undulating shapes they also give us the most perfect mathematical description of waves. The periodic behaviour is illustrated in these videos.

When we first learn about sine and cosine though, it’s in the context of right-angled triangles.

How do the two representations of these two functions fit together?

Imagine looking up at the night sky and try to visualise the stars and planets all lying on a sphere that curves around us, with the Earth at its centre. This is called a celestial sphere.

Around 2000 years ago, when early astronomers were trying to chart the night sky, doing astronomy meant doing geometry involving spheres—and that meant being able to do geometry involving circles. Looking at two stars on the celestial sphere, we can ask how far apart they are. We can formulate this as a problem about points on a circle.

Given two points AA and BB on a cirle, what is the shortest distance between them, measured along the straight line that lies inside the circle? Such a straight line is called a chord of the circle.

The length of the chord clearly depends on the angle αα formed by the two radii that connect AA and BB to the centre of the circle: for a given circle, the smaller αα is, the smaller the length of the chord will be, provided α<180∘α<180∘. This is true no matter how small or big your circle is. Have a play with the GeoGebra applet below if you need convincing!

To help them with their calculations astronomers produced tables of chords, showing the chord lengths that correspond to given angles. The first person to have written down such a table of chords may have been the Greek astronomer Hipparchus of Nicaea (ca 180BC - ca 125BC). Here are the first few entries in his table of chords:

α∘α∘ 00 712712 1515 22122212 3030 37123712 4545 52125212
chord (units) 00 450450 897897 13411341 17791779 22102210 26312631 30413041

Hipparchus used a circle of radius ≈3438≈3438 units.

What is the length of the chord when

  1. α=90∘α=90∘,

  2. α=60∘α=60∘?

  1. When α=90∘α=90∘, we have a right-angled triangle where two of the sides are the radius and the last side is the chord length.

As we know the radius is ≈3438≈3438 units, we can use Pythagoras’ theorem to find the length of the chord:

AB2soAB=r2+r2=2r2=2r2⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯⎯√=2⎯⎯√r=2⎯⎯√×3438≈4862unitsAB2=r2+r2=2r2soAB=2r2=2r=2×3438≈4862units
  1. When α=60∘α=60∘, we have an isosceles triangle with the two equal sides being of length rr and the angle 60∘60∘ between them.

Therefore the other angles in the triangle are also 60∘60∘ and the triangle is equilateral. This means all three sides of the triangle are the same length, so the chord length is equal to the radius, 34383438 units.

During the 4th and 5th centuries AD, mathematicians and astronomers in India decided that, rather than producing tables to relate chord lengths to angles αα, they would produce tables to relate half-chord lengths to half angles α/2α/2. Splitting the angle αα in two gives a right-angled triangle TT, with the corners being the centre OO of the circle, the point AA and the mid-point MM of the original chord. The side opposite OO is half the original chord, and the hypotenuse of the triangle is the radius rr of the circle.

What is the length of the half chord?

As this is a right-angled triangle, we can use trigonometry to find the length of the half chord. It is

rsinα2.rsin⁡α2.

When Indian mathematicians produced tables relating half angles to half chords, they were effectively producing sine tables. Our modern trigonometric notions reflect their Indian roots. The word “sine” comes from the Sanskrit word for “chord”, jīvā. It was mistakenly transformed into the Arab word jiba which means “bosom” or “fold” and then translated into the Latin word “sinus”.

The definition of sine in terms of right-angled triangles works only for angles α/2α/2 that are less than 90∘90∘. But there’s a neat way of defining the sine function for angles greater than 90∘90∘.

Consider the whole circle on coordinate axes, with its centre at the origin OO and the chord’s midpoint MM lying on the xx-axis. The point AA now has coordinates (x,y)(x,y), which are both positive. We can assume that the radius of the circle (the hypotenuse of our triangle) is 11 and we can also write ββ (rather than α/2α/2) for the angle that the triangle subtends at OO.

What is the yy-coordinate of the point AA?

y=sinβy=sin⁡β.

What is the xx-coordinate of the point AA?

x=cosβx=cos⁡β.

Now imagine sliding the point AA anti-clockwise around the circle, thereby increasing the angle ββ. We can define sinβsin⁡β to be the yy-coordinate of the corresponding point AA for any value of ββ.

Using the circle, what are the values of

  1. sin90∘sin⁡90∘,
  2. sin180∘sin⁡180∘,
  3. sin270∘sin⁡270∘,
  4. sin360∘sin⁡360∘?
  1. sin90∘=1sin⁡90∘=1,
  2. sin180∘=0sin⁡180∘=0,
  3. sin270∘=−1sin⁡270∘=−1
  4. sin360∘=0sin⁡360∘=0.

We can even define sinβsin⁡β for angles greater than 360∘360∘: it’s simply the yy-coordinate of the point you get to when sliding AA round and round the circle in an anti-clockwise direction by ββ, starting with AA sitting at the point (1,0)(1,0). Similarly, if ββ is less than 0∘0∘, we can do the same – this corresponds to AA travelling clockwise from (1,0)(1,0).

And this is where the waviness of the sine function comes from. As AA moves once around the circle, starting and ending at the point (1,0)(1,0), its yy-coordinate moves from 00 up to a maximum of 11 (when AA is at the top of the circle) back down to 00 (the leftmost point of the circle), down to −1−1 (the bottom of the circle) and back up to 11, its starting position. Another turn around the circle, corresponding to angles between 360∘360∘ and 720∘720∘, repeats this single wave segment. A third turn, corresponding to angles between 720∘720∘ and 1080∘1080∘, gives yet another repeat, and so on.

Something very similar happens for the cosine function. Looking back at the unit circle diagram, for A=(x,y)A=(x,y) in the first quadrant, we see that the usual triangle definition of cosine (as mentioned above) gives cosβ=x.cos⁡β=x. Again, as AA moves round the circle in an anti-clockwise direction, we can define cosβcos⁡β to be the xx-coordinate of AA.

The graph of the cosine function has exactly the same wavy shape as that of the sine function, only it is offset by 90∘90∘ because of the way the xx and yy coordinates of the point AA relate to each other as AA moves around the circle.

You can see that the waves intersect at certain points.

Looking back to our right-angled triangle, we can see that the values of sine and cosine will be the same when the two shorter sides of the triangles are equal in length. The triangle is therefore isosceles, and with one angle of 90∘90∘, the remaining two angles must each be 45∘45∘.

Therefore sinβ=cosβsin⁡β=cos⁡β when β=45∘β=45∘.

If we take the hypotenuse of the isosceles right-angled triangle to have length 11, then the other side lengths will be equal to both sinxsin⁡x and cosxcos⁡x.

Using Pythagoras’ theorem we can find ℓℓ.

ℓ2+ℓ2=1so2ℓ2=1.ℓ2+ℓ2=1so2ℓ2=1.

Therefore

ℓ=12⎯⎯√=2⎯⎯√2.ℓ=12=22.
(Note we take the positive square root because we are finding a length.)

We have shown that sin45∘=cos45∘sin⁡45∘=cos⁡45∘ and these are both equal to 2⎯⎯√2.22.

We have found one solution to the question, but we know that sine and cosine are periodic, and so there will be other solutions too. If we think about the unit circle, we can see that by adding 360∘360∘ to the angle we will be back to the original triangle, and so

β=(360n+45)∘,n=0,±1,±2,…givessinβ=cosβ=2⎯⎯√2.β=(360n+45)∘,n=0,±1,±2,…givessin⁡β=cos⁡β=22.

If we think again about the unit circle, there is another point where the xx and yy coordinates are equal, as shown in this diagram where we have drawn the line y=xy=x on top of the circle:

The point BB clearly has coordinates which are the negatives of the coordinates of AA, and it lies 180∘180∘ further on. So β=225∘β=225∘ also gives sinβ=cosβsin⁡β=cos⁡β. We could do another revolution of the circle and reach the same point, so β=(360n+225)∘β=(360n+225)∘, where n=0n=0, ±1±1, ±2±2, … will give the same values of sine and cosine, namely −2√2−22.

Looking at the diagram, we know we have found all the solutions to sinβ=cosβsin⁡β=cos⁡β now because there are no other points on the circle which have the same xx and yy coordinates.

We could also have answered this question by looking at the points of intersection of the two waves. We can use the symmetries of the graphs and 90∘90∘ offset of the waves to locate points where sinx=cosxsin⁡x=cos⁡x. There is one point midway between 0∘0∘ and 90∘90∘ (giving a positive yy-value) and another midway between 180∘180∘ and 270∘270∘ (giving a negative yy-value), i.e. sin45∘=cos45∘sin⁡45∘=cos⁡45∘ and sin225∘=cos225∘sin⁡225∘=cos⁡225∘. Because the graphs are periodic, points of intersection with the same yy-values occur every 360∘360∘. We can also see from symmetry that sin225∘=−sin45∘sin⁡225∘=−sin⁡45∘. We could then use a right-angled triangle or the unit circle to find the value of sin45∘sin⁡45∘ and thus come up with the same answers as before. The waves and stars are in agreement.
