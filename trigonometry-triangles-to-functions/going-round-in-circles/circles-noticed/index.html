Things you might have noticed

In the diagram below, PP is a point on a circle of radius 11 and θθ is the angle between OPOP and the positive xx-axis.

We have drawn in the diameter through P.P.

  • MM is the point where the diameter through PP meets the line x=1x=1
  • NN is the point where the diameter through PP meets the line y=1y=1

Using the functions from the triangles problem, work out the coordinates of points PP, MM and N.N.

The triangles in the previous part of the problem may help you to work out some side lengths.

We have added some extra labels to the diagram.

The hypotenuse of OPQOPQ has length 11, so OQOQ has length cosθcos⁡θ and QPQP has length sinθsin⁡θ. This means that the coordinates of PP are (cosθ,sinθ)(cos⁡θ,sin⁡θ).

To find the yy-coordinate of MM, note that OUOU has length 11 so UMUM has length tanθtan⁡θ. Therefore MM has coordinates (1,tanθ)(1,tan⁡θ). The similarity of triangles OPQOPQ and OMUOMU again tells us that tanθ=sinθcosθtan⁡θ=sin⁡θcos⁡θ.

To find the xx-coordinate of NN, note that y=1y=1 is parallel to the xx-axis so angle ONVONV is θ.θ. As OVOV has length 11, this means the coordinates of NN are (cotθ,1)(cot⁡θ,1).

As θθ varies, how will MM and NN move? What happens when θθ isn’t acute?

  • When will MM and NN be moving quickly?
  • Do MM and NN always move smoothly?
  • When will their xx- or yy-coordinates change sign?

In the next diagram, we have drawn the tangent to the circle at PP and marked the points where the tangent at PP meets the axes.

What are the coordinates of KK and LL? How will KK and LL move along the axes as θθ varies?

The line LKLK is a tangent to the circle at PP, so OPOP is at right angles to LKLK.

Therefore triangle OPKOPK is right-angled with angle θθ at OO and adjacent side of length 11. This means that OKOK has length secθsec⁡θ and the coordinates of KK are (secθ,0)(sec⁡θ,0).

Likewise, triangle OPLOPL is right-angled with angle θθ at LL, so LL has coordinates (0,cosecθ)(0,cosec⁡θ).

You can see how KK and LL move as θθ varies in the GeoGebra file below. Could you sketch graphs of secθsec⁡θ and cosecθcosec⁡θ against θθ?

We have used θθ to parametrise the circle because as θθ varies, the locus of the point P(cosθ,sinθ)P(cos⁡θ,sin⁡θ) is a circle, centre 00, radius 1.1. We could say that θθ parametrises the lines x=1x=1 and y=1y=1 because the points M(1,tanθ)M(1,tan⁡θ) and N(cotθ,1)N(cot⁡θ,1) trace out these lines as θθ varies. Similarly, we could say that the xx and yy axes are parametrised by θθ via the points K(secθ,0)K(sec⁡θ,0) and L(0,cosecθ)L(0,cosec⁡θ) respectively.
