Solution

The trigonometric functions with which we are most familiar are sine and cosine. From them, we can obtain tangent, and also the reciprocals secant, cosecant and cotangent. In the past, mathematicians have used many other trigonometric functions too. Some of these are illustrated below.

Find the following lengths, in terms of sinθsin⁡θ and cosθcos⁡θ.

  1. OBOB

  2. ABAB

  3. BCBC (this was known as the versed sine or versine of θθ, written versinθversin⁡θ)

The triangle OABOAB is right-angled, with a right angle at BB and with hypotenuse of length 11, so we see that

OB=cosθ.OB=cos⁡θ.

Similarly, we have AB=sinθ.AB=sin⁡θ.

Since the circle is a unit circle, we know that OC=1OC=1 and so

BC=OC−OB=1−cosθ.BC=OC−OB=1−cos⁡θ.
This tells us that versinθ=1−cosθ.versin⁡θ=1−cos⁡θ.

  1. ODOD

  2. CDCD (this was known as the exsecant of θθ, written exsecθexsec⁡θ)

The triangle OADOAD is right-angled, with a right angle at AA and with ∠DOA=θ∠DOA=θ, and the side OAOA has length 11, so we find that cosθ=1OD,cos⁡θ=1OD, so

OD=1cosθ.OD=1cos⁡θ.

Therefore we have

CD=OD−OC=1cosθ−1=secθ−1.CD=OD−OC=1cos⁡θ−1=sec⁡θ−1.
This means we can write exsecθ=secθ−1.exsec⁡θ=sec⁡θ−1.

  1. ADAD

  2. AEAE

Looking again at triangle OADOAD, we find that

AD=tanθ=sinθcosθ.AD=tan⁡θ=sin⁡θcos⁡θ.

We can consider triangle AOEAOE, which has a right angle at AA and which has angle θθ at EE. The side AOAO has length 11, and so we see that tanθ=1AE,tan⁡θ=1AE, and therefore

AE=cotθ=cosθsinθ.AE=cot⁡θ=cos⁡θsin⁡θ.

  1. AGAG

  2. GOGO

  3. FGFG (this was known as the coversed sine or coversine of θθ, written coversinθcoversin⁡θ or coversθcovers⁡θ or cvsθcvs⁡θ)

Triangle AGEAGE has a right angle at GG and angle θθ at EE, and we have just seen that its hypotenuse has length cotθcot⁡θ. So

AG=cotθsinθ=cosθ.AG=cot⁡θsin⁡θ=cos⁡θ.

Quadrilateral ABOGABOG is a rectangle, because its angles are all right angles, so

GO=AB=sinθ.GO=AB=sin⁡θ.

The circle has radius 11, so

FG=FO−GO=1−sinθ.FG=FO−GO=1−sin⁡θ.
Therefore coversinθ=1−sinθ.coversin⁡θ=1−sin⁡θ.

  1. EFEF (this was known as the excosecant of θθ, written excosecθexcosec⁡θ or excscθexcsc⁡θ)

We can find the length EOEO using the right-angled triangle DOEDOE, which has an angle θθ (at EE). We know that OD=secθOD=sec⁡θ, and so we have

EO=secθtanθ=1sinθ.EO=sec⁡θtan⁡θ=1sin⁡θ.

Then we have

EF=EO−1=1sinθ−1.EF=EO−1=1sin⁡θ−1.
Therefore we can write excosecθ=cosecθ−1.excosec⁡θ=cosec⁡θ−1.

Can you find line segments on the diagram that have the following lengths?

  1. The versed cosine or vercosine of θθ, written as vercosθvercos⁡θ, which is defined by

    vercosθ=1+cosθ.vercos⁡θ=1+cos⁡θ.

  2. The coversed cosine or covercosine of θθ, written as covercosθcovercos⁡θ, which is defined by

    covercosθ=1+sinθ.covercos⁡θ=1+sin⁡θ.

Since the circle has radius 11 and OB=cosθOB=cos⁡θ, the line segment BHBH has length 1+cosθ1+cos⁡θ.

Similarly, GJGJ has length 1+sinθ1+sin⁡θ.

We know that cosθ=sin(π2−θ)cos⁡θ=sin⁡(π2−θ).

  1. How are versine and coversine related?

  2. How are vercosine and covercosine related?

We have
coversin(π2−θ)=1−sin(π2−θ)=1−cosθ=versinθ.coversin⁡(π2−θ)=1−sin⁡(π2−θ)=1−cos⁡θ=versin⁡θ.
 Similarly, we have
covercos(π2−θ)=1+sin(π2−θ)=1+cosθ=vercosθ.covercos⁡(π2−θ)=1+sin⁡(π2−θ)=1+cos⁡θ=vercos⁡θ.
  1. What do you think that the prefix “co-” might indicate here?

The prefix “co-” probably originates from the word “complementary”. Two angles are called complementary if they add up to a right angle. So:

  • the cosine of an angle is the sine of the complementary angle

  • the cosecant (cosec) of an angle is the secant (sec) of the complementary angle

  • the cotangent (cotan) of an angle is the tangent (tan) of the complementary angle

  • the coversine of an angle is the versine of the complementary angle

  • the covercosine of an angle is the vercosine of the complementary angle
