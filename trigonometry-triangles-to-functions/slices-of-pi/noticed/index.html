Things you might have noticed

Take a look at the diagram below.

We’ve used inequalities involving sinθsin⁡θ, cosθcos⁡θ and tanθtan⁡θ to divide the semicircle into sectors. Each sector in the diagram is defined by a different inequality. For example, one sector is defined by the angles θθ between 00 and ππ for which cosθ<sinθ<tanθ.cos⁡θ<sin⁡θ<tan⁡θ. Another sector is defined by cosθ<tanθ<sinθ.cos⁡θ<tan⁡θ<sin⁡θ.

  • Can you work out which inequality has been used to define each sector?

There are many ways to approach this problem, and we will illustrate a few of them here. If you have used different approaches, or used similar approaches but for different parts of the problem, you might find it interesting to compare the methods.

We need to think about how sinθsin⁡θ, cosθcos⁡θ and tanθtan⁡θ behave in relation to each other.

From the shapes and symmetries of the graphs y=sinθy=sin⁡θ, y=cosθy=cos⁡θ and y=tanθy=tan⁡θ, we can see that

  • the green sector is defined by cosθ<sinθ<tanθcos⁡θ<sin⁡θ<tan⁡θ

  • the yellow sector is defined by tanθ<cosθ<sinθtan⁡θ<cos⁡θ<sin⁡θ

  • the blue sector is defined by cosθ<tanθ<sinθcos⁡θ<tan⁡θ<sin⁡θ.

However, we may need to do a bit more work to complete the sketch graphs. For example, do the graphs y=tanθy=tan⁡θ and y=sinθy=sin⁡θ cross between θ=0θ=0 and θ=π2θ=π2? Here are a couple of ways to think about this.

Can you explain why the identity tanθ≡sinθcosθtan⁡θ≡sin⁡θcos⁡θ tells us the following?

  • For θθ between 00 and π2π2, sinθ<tanθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ.

  • The graphs of y=tanθy=tan⁡θ and y=sinθy=sin⁡θ only meet when θθ is a multiple of ππ.

We can compare side lengths of triangles in the unit circle diagram to obtain inequalities relating sinθsin⁡θ, cosθcos⁡θ and tanθtan⁡θ.

In this way we can show that sinθ<tanθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ for all values of θθ between 00 and π2.π2.

(Hint: which triangle has tanθtan⁡θ as its hypotenuse?)

Thinking more generally, we can also use the diagram to show the following.

  • For small values of θθ, sinθ<tanθ<cosθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ<cos⁡θ. This defines the red sector.

  • cosπ4=sinπ4cos⁡π4=sin⁡π4

  • For θθ between π4π4 and π2π2, cosθ<sinθ<tanθcos⁡θ<sin⁡θ<tan⁡θ, which defines the green sector, as we also saw before from the graph.

We now only need to decide which inequality defines the orange sector. You may find it helpful to look at the graphs or to visualise a point moving round the unit circle.

If we think about the behaviour of the functions as θθ increases from 00, we first have sinθ<tanθ<cosθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ<cos⁡θ (which defines the red sector). Another inequality must define the orange sector, and then we have cosθ<sinθ<tanθcos⁡θ<sin⁡θ<tan⁡θ for θθ between π4π4 and π2π2, which defines the green sector.

As tanθtan⁡θ and cosθcos⁡θ are continuous between 00 and π4π4, there must be some value of θθ between 00 and π4π4 where the graphs y=tanθy=tan⁡θ and y=cosθy=cos⁡θ cross over.

Therefore the inequality that defines the orange sector is sinθ<cosθ<tanθsin⁡θ<cos⁡θ<tan⁡θ. Why is this the only option?

Why is the boundary between the green and yellow sectors different from the boundaries between other sectors?

  • Which is the biggest sector?

The boundary of the orange and green sectors is at θ=π4θ=π4, and therefore the green sector is larger than the red sector. However, the yellow or blue sectors may be bigger than the green sector. Here are a few ways to tackle this problem.

What is the value of the different functions at 3π43π4?

What are the solutions of tanθ=cosθtan⁡θ=cos⁡θ? Identities may help to make this into a simpler equation to solve.

Do you need to solve this equation completely to decide which sector is the largest?

It may be of interest that solutions of tanθ=cosθtan⁡θ=cos⁡θ are related to the golden ratio.

From the symmetry of the graphs, the angle between the boundary of the yellow and blue sectors and θ=πθ=π must be the same as the angle between θ=0θ=0 and the boundary of the red and orange sectors. In other words, the blue and red sectors are the same size. What does this tell us about the sizes of the other sectors?

If you used more than one approach, can you connect them? For example, can you connect the symmetry argument with solving tanθ=cosθtan⁡θ=cos⁡θ?

  • If you extended the diagram to make a complete circle, how many extra sectors would you need?

To complete the circle we can use similar methods to those discussed above. Another approach might be to consider the possible orderings of sinθsin⁡θ, cosθcos⁡θ and tanθtan⁡θ.

How many ways can the functions sinθsin⁡θ, cosθcos⁡θ and tanθtan⁡θ be ordered?

  • Which of these ways of ordering occur between θ=0θ=0 and θ=πθ=π?

  • Which ways might be repeated?

  • Will all these ways of ordering occur between θ=0θ=0 and θ=2πθ=2π?

Can you explain how the behaviour of the functions gives us these sectors and inequalities?

There were many ways to approach this problem. Instead of considering all three functions at the same time, we could have considered pairs of functions.

  • When is sinθ<cosθsin⁡θ<cos⁡θ?

  • When is sinθ<tanθsin⁡θ<tan⁡θ?

  • When is tanθ<cosθtan⁡θ<cos⁡θ?
