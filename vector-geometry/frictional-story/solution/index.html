Solution

A particle of mass 10kg10kg starts at rest on a rough inclined plane. A force of magnitude PNPN is applied to the particle and acts up the slope, parallel to the plane.

When P=20P=20 the particle is on the point of sliding down the plane.

When P=40P=40 the particle is on the point of sliding up the plane.

Before we can tackle the problems below we need to think about the situation we have been given and what information we know. For both values of PP the particle is at rest, so the forces acting on it are in equilibrium.

Why is the frictional force going in different directions on each diagram?

What do we know about frictional force when a particle is on the point of moving?

Resolving the forces parallel to the plane for each diagram gives us the following equations:

For P=20P=20,

20+Fr=10gsinθ20+Fr=10gsin⁡θ

For P=40P=40,

40−Fr=10gsinθ40−Fr=10gsin⁡θ

The model we use for frictional force is Fr≤μRFr≤μR, so we have to be careful when writing down equations that we don’t assume that any FrFr’s appearing will represent the same value. In this case, both particles are on the point of moving, so Fr=μRFr=μR for both situations.

As FrFr is the maximum frictional force in both cases we can write

20+Fmax40−Fmax=10gsinθ=10gsinθ20+Fmax=10gsin⁡θ40−Fmax=10gsin⁡θ

where Fmax=μ10gcosθFmax=μ10gcos⁡θ.

We can solve the equations to find that Fmax=10NFmax=10N and 10gsinθ=30N10gsin⁡θ=30N.

What does the force 10gsinθ=30N10gsin⁡θ=30N represent?

For each of the following values of PP describe how the particle is moving and calculate the frictional force acting on it:

  • P=25P=25

As we are about to compare values, we need to be careful about the direction of any forces we calculate. We will take frictional force to be positive as it goes up the slope.

When P=25P=25 the particle must remain at rest as the value of PP is between 2020 and 4040. Therefore the forces are in equilibrium and

25+Fr=10gsinθ.25+Fr=10gsin⁡θ.
We know 10gsinθ=3010gsin⁡θ=30, so we find that Fr=5Fr=5 and is acting up the slope.

The frictional force could also be found by comparison. Compared to when P=20P=20, the force of PP has increased by 55, so the frictional force must have decreased by 55 in order to keep the equilibrium.

  • P=30P=30

When P=30P=30 the particle must remain at rest as the value of PP is between 2020 and 4040. Therefore the forces are still in equilibrium, so

30+Fr=10gsinθ,30+Fr=10gsin⁡θ,

giving us Fr=0Fr=0.

When P=50P=50 the particle must be accelerating up the plane, since it is on the point of sliding up when P=40P=40.

How do we know it’s accelerating? Do we know how fast?

As the particle is no longer in equilibrium we can apply Newton’s second law.
FP+Fr−10gsinθ=ma=maF=maP+Fr−10gsin⁡θ=ma

The model we use for friction when a particle is moving is Fr=μRFr=μR, which is the maximum frictional force of 10N10N that we found earlier. However, this force is acting down the slope so we will think of this as a negative value.

What would a graph of the frictional force against PP look like?

In order to sketch the graph, or check that your sketch seems reasonable, it might be helpful to think about the following questions:

  • What happens to friction between P=20P=20 and P=40P=40?
  • Will the graph be linear or curved? Why?
  • Does the graph fit with our model of friction, Fr≤μRFr≤μR?

When PP is between 2020 and 4040 the particle will stay at rest, so the magnitude of the frictional force will vary between 00 and FmaxFmax.
