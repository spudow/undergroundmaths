Vector Geometry

 Rich example

A frictional story

Teacher Notes

Printable version

Why use this resource?

This resource explores the classic situation of a particle on a slope. Students have to draw diagrams and resolve forces, but are also provided with an opportunity to think about how friction varies when a particle is not moving and why the variation is linear. Students will be reminded that the frictional force acting on a particle is modelled by Fr≤μRFr≤μR, which should help them avoid the misconception that Fr=μRFr=μR at all times.

Possible approach

It may initially look like a standard question, but the task of sketching a graph of friction against the force PP is unusual. Students may be unsure what information they need to find, so allowing individual thinking time is important. As there are a number of different ways to think about the problem, it may then be beneficial for students to pair up to discuss their approaches and findings. This might include reflecting on what values students calculated. For example, did they calculate μμ and θθ, and did they need to?

It is preferable to not substitute in a numerical value for gg, however students may choose to do so. To keep calculations simple, it is best if students use g=10g=10.

Key questions

  • When is F=μRF=μR?
  • What do you know about the particle when the value of PP is between 2020 and 4040?
  • When the particle is stationary, will there always be a frictional force acting on it?
  • What assumptions are involved in the frictional model F≤μRF≤μR?

Possible support

Asking students to imagine, or describe in words, what forces are acting on the particle for different values of PP, may help students to get a sense of what sort of answers they might expect and therefore, what the graph might look like.

Possible extension

  • What would happen if PP was not parallel to the plane? What changes and what stays the same?
