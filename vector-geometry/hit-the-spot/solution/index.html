Solution

Here are some vectors:

  • a=(61)a=(61)
  • bb has magnitude 22⎯⎯√22 and direction 45∘45∘ anti-clockwise from the xx-axis
  • cc has magnitude 44 in the positive xx direction
  • d=3i−jd=3i−j
  • e=7i−3je=7i−3j
  • ff is 22 units in the negative xx direction and 33 units in the positive yy direction
  • g=(−24)g=(−24)

Select a starting point, M(2,5)M(2,5) or N(3,1)N(3,1), and two of the vectors from the list so that the sum of the vectors will get you from your start to the target, T(7,6)T(7,6).

How many different ways can you pair up vectors from the list to get to the target?

First, let’s write all of the vectors in the same format. We’ve chosen to use column vectors.

a=(61),b=(22),c=(40),d=(3−1),e=(7−3),f=(−23),g=(−24)a=(61),b=(22),c=(40),d=(3−1),e=(7−3),f=(−23),g=(−24)

The vector from MM to TT is MT−→−=(51)MT→=(51) and we can make this by adding,

b+d=(2+32−1)=(51).b+d=(2+32−1)=(51).

Notice that we can add these two vectors the other way round, d+bd+b, with the same result. If we draw these two “routes” they look like this.

You can decide whether these two are different solutions or the same solution!

What shape have we constructed? Can you prove it?

We can also make MT−→−MT→ by adding ee and gg, and we can make NT−→−NT→ by adding aa and gg.

In all, we have three different pairs of vectors which will get us to the target, TT, and each pair can be combined in either of two orders.

You might also have noticed that starting at MM, if we apply cc first then bb it takes us through TT but overshoots. So can you write MT−→−MT→ as a combination of multiples of cc and bb?

If we allow multiples (including negatives), is it also possible to write NT−→−NT→ as a combination of cc and bb? Which other points can you reach by adding multiples of cc and bb?

Can you add three of the vectors to get from one of the starts to the target?

NT−→−=b+c+fNT→=b+c+f

In how many different orders could we arrange these three vectors?
