Solution

Each of the following situations, A-D, shows a system of forces acting on a particle of given mass and the initial velocity of the particle. Work out the single extra constant force that can be added to this system, so that the particle would be brought instantaneously to rest at the specified time.

A 4s4s

There are many ways to solve these problems. We’ll approach the first couple of examples using different mechanical principles and different notation. Both approaches could be used for all four problems, but a particular example may tempt you to choose one approach rather than another.

Without any extra force, this 6kg6kg particle is moving with velocity 8ms−18ms−1 and accelerating perpendicular to this, due to the 60N60N force.

How will the force of magnitude FNFN bring the particle to rest?

Can we say anything about the approximate values of FF and θθ? Thinking about this now may help us to check that our final answers are sensible. For example, Fsinθ=60Fsin⁡θ=60, so the magnitude of the force is greater than 60N.60N. Is θθ likely to be greater than or less than 45∘45∘?

To work out the actual value of FF and the angle θθ, let’s consider perpendicular components along the dotted lines in the diagram.

Firstly, we know that Fsinθ=60Fsin⁡θ=60 because we want to balance out the 60N60N force.

Why do we want to put the particle in equilibrium in this direction?

Secondly, FcosθFcos⁡θ needs to provide the acceleration to bring the 6kg6kg particle to rest in 44 seconds. Using v=u+atv=u+at and taking left to be the positive direction, we have 0=−8+4a.0=−8+4a. So the acceleration we need is 2ms−22ms−2 in the opposite direction to the initial velocity. It follows from Newton’s Second Law that Fcosθ=12.Fcos⁡θ=12.

We now know the perpendicular components of the force, FcosθFcos⁡θ and FsinθFsin⁡θ, so we can find FF and θθ.

With the extra force added, the resultant force on the particle is acting in the opposite direction to the initial velocity. Therefore the particle will be restricted to moving in a straight line, reversing direction after 44 seconds. Without this force, the particle would have maintained the 8ms−18ms−1 component of its initial velocity, but also accelerated in the direction of the 60N60N force at a rate of 10ms−210ms−2, so the component of velocity in this direction after time tt seconds would have been 10tms−1.10tms−1. B 2s2s

For this example we’ll use two approaches.

We’ll use unit vectors ii and jj where the directions of ii and jj are shown in this diagram.

As we know the mass and initial velocity of the particle, we know its initial momentum. We want to find the force that over 2s2s will provide an impulse which brings the particle to rest.

In terms of ii and jj, the initial velocity is (6cos30∘i+6sin30∘j)ms−1.(6cos⁡30∘i+6sin⁡30∘j)ms−1. This is (6×3√2i+6×12j)ms−1(6×32i+6×12j)ms−1, which simplifies to 3(3⎯⎯√i+j)ms−1.3(3i+j)ms−1.

Therefore the initial momentum is 15(3⎯⎯√i+j)Ns.15(3i+j)Ns.

The total impulse, II, (due to forces acting for 2s2s) needs to satisfy 15(3⎯⎯√i+j)+I=0.15(3i+j)+I=0.

How do the forces acting on the particle provide this impulse?

The −30jN−30jN force contributes an impulse in the direction we want, but this is likely to be too large for the stopping time required. We also need impulse in the direction of −i−i so we need a further force to act on the particle. We’ll write this extra force as (bi+cj)N(bi+cj)N where we expect bb to be negative and cc to be positive.

Therefore 15(3⎯⎯√i+j)+2(−30j+bi+cj)=015(3i+j)+2(−30j+bi+cj)=0,

which gives us two equations to solve: 2b=−153⎯⎯√2b=−153 and 2(c−30)=−15.2(c−30)=−15.

Solving these gives us the required force of (−13.0i+22.5j)N(3 s.f.).(−13.0i+22.5j)N(3 s.f.). As expected, bb is negative and cc is positive.

The acceleration needed to bring this particle to instantaneous rest is 3ms−23ms−2 in the opposite direction to the initial velocity. This acceleration is provided by a force of magnitude 15N.15N.

If there were no other forces acting on the particle, the 15N15N force would be the extra force we’d need. But we need to work out what extra force we can apply so that the 15N15N force is the resultant of the 30N30N force and this extra force.

Using a vector triangle, as shown, we can work out the magnitude FF and direction θθ of the extra force.

The angle between the 15N15N force and the 30N30N force is 60∘60∘ and cos60∘=1530cos⁡60∘=1530, so the vector triangle shown is right-angled, with θ=30∘θ=30∘ and F2=675.F2=675. It follows that the extra force we need to apply has magnitude 153⎯⎯√N153N and is directed at 30∘30∘ to the upward dotted line, which means it is perpendicular to the direction of the initial velocity.

If the triangle had not been right-angled, how could we have found FF and θθ?

Compare these approaches with each other and our approach to the first example.

  • How did using momentum and impulse compare with using acceleration?

  • How did using a triangle of forces compare with using components?

  • How did using ii and jj affect the type of calculations we had to do? Note that we chose to define ii and jj in the directions given, but we could have chosen different directions for these. For example, we could have chosen ii to be in the direction of the initial velocity.

Describe how the particle behaves over time if this extra force is applied, assuming all the given forces acting on the particle remain unchanged. Compare this with how the particle would have behaved if the extra force had not been applied.

With the extra force in place the impulse is directed in the opposite direction to the initial momentum, so the particle will move in a straight line, coming to rest after 22 seconds as it reverses direction along this line. Without this extra force, the component of momentum in the direction of ii would have been maintained, but the particle would have received an impulse in the −j−j direction, first bringing the momentum in this direction to zero, but after this the velocity and momentum would have had components in the positive ii and negative jj directions.

Why will the extra force shown bring this particle to rest in 3s3s?

Describe how the particle behaves over time if this extra force is applied, assuming all the given forces acting on the particle remain unchanged. Compare this with how the particle would have behaved if the extra force had not been applied.

If the extra force is applied, the particle will continue to move in the direction of its initial velocity, travelling in a straight line but reversing direction after 55 seconds. Without this force, the component of the particle’s velocity parallel to its initial velocity would have decreased to zero and then reversed direction, whereas the component of velocity perpendicular to this would have continued to increase from zero, so at time 3s3s the particle would have been moving perpendicular to its initial velocity.
